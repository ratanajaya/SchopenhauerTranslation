## Active Continuity

- Book Three concerns "the world as representation, second consideration": representation independent of the principle of sufficient reason, the Platonic Idea, and the object of art.
- The Ideas are determinate grades of the objectivation of the will. Unlike individuals, they do not enter time, space, causality, multiplicity, or change.
- The Idea is not the thing in itself absolutely: it is the thing in itself, the will, under the most general form of representation, namely object-being for a subject.
- Individual things are mediated objectivations of the will because the Idea has entered the subordinate forms expressed by the principle of sufficient reason.
- Time is the individual being's distributed or fragmented view of timeless Ideas; Plato's phrase "time is the moving image of eternity" is part of the Book Three continuity.
- Cognition of Ideas requires the subject to cease being individual insofar as it cognizes the Idea.
- Aesthetic contemplation: cognition tears itself free from the will, the object becomes Idea, and the individual becomes the pure will-less subject of cognition.
- In aesthetic cognition, subject and object remain the form of the Idea but are in equilibrium; the world as representation appears purely only through their mutual penetration.
- Will is the in-itself of both contemplated object and contemplating individual; outside representation they are not distinct.
- Art is the work of genius: it repeats and communicates Ideas apprehended through pure contemplation, unlike science, which follows relations under the principle of sufficient reason.
- Keep the contrast: science/experience follows an endless horizontal line of grounds and consequents; art cuts vertically through it and isolates the individual as representative of the whole.
- Genius is complete objectivity: cognition withdrawn from the service of the will, able to remain as pure knowing subject or clear eye of the world.
- Imagination extends genius beyond actual personal experience and helps it see what nature strove to form, but imagination alone can also become mere fantasizing.
- The ordinary person seeks concepts useful for the will; the person of genius lingers with intuition and the Idea, often at the cost of practical prudence.
- The genius/madness link turns on relations and memory: madness falsifies the relation of present to absent/past, while genius neglects relations in order to see the Idea.
- Carry into Book Four: the will itself is groundless and outside the principle of sufficient reason; only individual appearances and acts have causes, motives, or ends.
