# Translation Glossary And Continuity Notes

Use this file as the compact source of truth for future chapters. Add only decisions that are likely to recur or that prevent inconsistency later.

## Translation Priorities

- Translate into clear modern English without flattening Schopenhauer's argument.
- Preserve the sequence of reasoning, technical distinctions, headings, footnotes, and mottoes.
- Improve English paragraphing where it helps clarity; do not mirror German paragraph length mechanically.
- Keep recurring philosophical terms consistent unless a later context genuinely forces a change.
- For non-German quotations, keep the original and add an English translation.

## Chapter Continuity

- Front matter: use "Volume" for `Band` and "Preface" for `Vorrede`.
- From chapter 0003 onward, prioritize readable English paragraphing over one-to-one source paragraphing.
- Chapter 0007: preserve the distinction between empirical reality and transcendental ideality.
- Chapter 0008: use "understanding" for `Verstand`; animals have understanding but not reason.
- Chapter 0008: keep "semblance" for `Schein` and "error" for `Irrtum`.
- Chapter 0009: preserve the methodological starting point: representation, not subject alone or object alone.
- Chapter 0010: reason's single function is "formation of the concept."
- Chapter 0011: translate `Sphaere` as "sphere" in logical diagrams and `Umfang` as "extension" when paired with it.
- Chapter 0012: translate `Wissen` as "knowledge" and reserve "cognition" for technical uses of `Erkenntnis`.
- Chapter 0013: `Gefuehl` is a negative catch-all for non-conceptual contents of consciousness; translate as "feeling" but do not imply mere emotion.
- Chapter 0014: abstract knowledge does not enlarge intuitive cognition, but makes it preservable, communicable, plannable, and applicable.
- Chapter 0014: art and bodily skill should keep the contrast between immediate intuitive guidance and reflection/concepts.
- Chapter 0015: laughter arises from sudden incongruence between concept and intuitive object; keep the contrast between wit, foolishness, pedantry, and wordplay.
- Chapter 0016: science is defined by systematic form and completeness, not by greater certainty; proof ultimately rests on intuitive evidence.
- Chapter 0016: the power of judgment mediates between intuition/understanding and abstract reason by fixing intuitive cognition in concepts.
- Chapter 0017: in mathematics, intuitive evidence from the ground of being is preferred to Euclidean logical proof; keep the that/why contrast.
- Chapter 0017: error is an inference from consequent to ground based on a falsely overgeneralized major premise.
- Chapter 0017: philosophy does not seek a world-cause or world-purpose, but asks what the world is and mirrors it in abstract concepts.
- Chapter 0018: practical reason means action guided by abstract concepts; it is independent of ethical worth and can serve good or evil.
- Chapter 0018: Stoic ethics is treated as rational eudaimonism aimed at happiness through peace of mind, not as pure virtue ethics.
- Chapter 0019: Book Two begins the move from representation to will; mathematics and natural science still leave the inner meaning of appearances unexplained.
- Chapter 0019: morphology classifies forms; aetiology orders causal changes but cannot explain the inner essence of natural forces.
- Chapter 0020: the body is given in two ways: as representation/object among objects and immediately as will.
- Chapter 0020: acts of will and actions of the body are one and the same, given immediately and as intuition; do not render the relation as cause and effect.
- Chapter 0020: use "objecthood of the will" for `Objektitaet des Willens`, distinct from "objectivation of the will" for `Objektivation des Willens`.
- Chapter 0020: the identity of body and will is a distinctive "philosophical truth" that can be shown from immediate consciousness but not proved from something more immediate.
- Chapter 0021: the analogy from one's own body extends the will from immediate self-cognition to the inner essence of all appearances in nature.
- Chapter 0021: theoretical egoism is treated as an irrefutable skeptical sophism, not a serious philosophical stopping point.
- Chapter 0021: when will is attributed to natural forces such as gravity, do not imply cognition or movement from known motives; cognition belongs only to higher grades of the will's appearance.
- Chapter 0022: motives explain the appearance of individual acts only under the presupposition of empirical character; the will itself is groundless.
- Chapter 0022: physiology explains bodily functions aetiologically, but this does not undermine the philosophical claim that the body is objectivation/objecthood of the will.
- Chapter 0022: preserve the teleological point that bodily organs visibly express chief desires, without reducing the claim to biological design.
- Chapter 0023: section 21 identifies will alone as the thing in itself; all objects are appearances, visibility, or objecthood of will.
- Chapter 0023: when extending will to plant life, crystallization, magnetism, electricity, chemical affinity, and gravity, preserve the claim of identical inner essence without implying cognition or motive in lower grades.
- Chapter 0024: section 22 expands the concept of will by naming the genus after its clearest species, human willing; keep the point that this is not merely a verbal choice.
- Chapter 0024: do not render force as the more basic concept. The chapter insists that force is abstracted from appearance, while will is immediately known from within.
- Chapter 0025: section 23 keeps will as thing in itself groundless and outside multiplicity, while every appearance of will remains subject to necessity and the principle of sufficient reason.
- Chapter 0025: preserve the distinction between cause, stimulus, and motive. Cause in the strict sense has proportionate reaction; stimulus governs organic processes; motive is causality through cognition.
- Chapter 0025: instinct and constructive drives show will acting blindly even where cognition accompanies the action but does not guide it toward a known end.
- Chapter 0025: the extension from animals to plants and inorganic forces must keep the same claim as prior chapters: identical inner essence as will, with differences only in grades and forms of appearance.
- Chapter 0026: section 24 contrasts form and content: mathematics and aetiology explain relations and lawful entry into time and space, but never the inner what of appearance.
- Chapter 0026: preserve the polemic against reducing organic life, chemism, and natural forces to mechanism; reductions may consolidate forces but cannot eliminate original forces or the groundless residue.
- Chapter 0026: `qualitates occultae` and `forma substantialis` should remain in the text with English glosses.
- Chapter 0026: the Spinoza stone example is inverted: Spinoza emphasizes necessity; Schopenhauer adds that the stone's inner quality is, in essence, will.
- Chapter 0027: section 25 emphasizes that multiplicity belongs only to appearance; the will is not divided into parts across individuals or species.
- Chapter 0027: grades of visibility/objectivation differ by degree, but the one will is present whole and undivided in each appearance.
- Chapter 0027: use capitalized "Idea" for Plato's Ideas as fixed grades of the objectivation of the will; avoid Kant's later technical sense here.
- Chapter 0028: section 26 treats universal natural forces as the lowest grades of the objectivation of the will; the forces themselves are groundless, while only their individual appearances have causes.
- Chapter 0028: a law of nature is the relation of an Idea to the form of its appearance; it governs the entry and exit of appearances in shared matter.
- Chapter 0028: individuality fades downward from human character through animals and plants to inorganic nature, where the species/natural force appears wholly in each instance.
- Chapter 0028: Malebranche's occasional causes align with the claim that causes only occasion the will's becoming-visible at a place and time, not the inner essence of the appearance.
