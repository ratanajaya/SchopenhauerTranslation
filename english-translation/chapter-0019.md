# §. 17.

## Second Book.

First Consideration of the World as Will:<br>
The Objectivation of the Will.

> `Nos habitat, non tartara, sed nec sidera coeli:`
> `Spiritus, in nobis qui viget, illa facit.`
> Translation: It is not Tartarus, nor the stars of heaven, that dwells in us; the spirit that lives in us makes them.

In the first book we considered representation only as such, and therefore only according to its general form. As for abstract representation, the concept, we did also come to know it according to its content, insofar as it has all content and meaning only through its relation to intuitive representation, without which it would be without worth and without content. Referred entirely, then, to intuitive representation, we will now demand to know its content as well, its closer determinations, and the forms it presents to us.

Above all, it will matter to us to obtain some clarification about its real meaning, about that meaning which otherwise is only felt. By virtue of this meaning, these images do not pass by us as something wholly alien and meaningless, as they otherwise would have to do; rather, they speak to us immediately, are understood, and acquire an interest that lays claim to our whole being.

We direct our gaze toward mathematics, natural science, and philosophy, each of which lets us hope that it will give part of the desired clarification.

First, however, we find philosophy as a monster with many heads, each of which speaks a different language. To be sure, they are not all at odds with one another about the point raised here, the meaning of intuitive representation. With the exception of the skeptics and idealists, the others, in the main, speak rather consistently of an *object* that would lie at the *ground* of representation, and which, though different from representation in its whole being and essence, would nevertheless be as similar to it in every respect as one egg is to another.

But this will not help us. For we do not know how to distinguish such an object from representation at all. Rather, we find that the two are one and the same, since every object always and forever presupposes a subject and therefore still remains representation. We have also recognized objecthood itself as belonging to the most universal form of representation, which is precisely the falling apart into object and subject.

Moreover, the principle of sufficient reason to which people appeal here is likewise for us only a form of representation: namely, the lawful connection of one representation with another. It is not the connection of the whole finite or endless series of representations with something that would not be representation at all, and therefore could not be representable at all. Skeptics and idealists were discussed above, in the treatment of the dispute over the reality of the external world.

If we now seek the desired closer knowledge of that intuitive representation, which has become known to us only quite generally and merely according to form, from mathematics, then mathematics will speak to us of those representations only insofar as they fill time and space, that is, insofar as they are magnitudes. It will state the how-much and how-large with the greatest exactness. But since this is always only relative, that is, a comparison of one representation with others, and indeed only in the one-sided respect of magnitude, this too will not be the information we are chiefly seeking.

Finally, if we look toward the wide domain of natural science, divided into many fields, we can first distinguish two main divisions of it. It is either description of forms, which I call *morphology*, or explanation of changes, which I call *aetiology*. The former considers the enduring forms; the latter considers changing matter according to the laws of its transition from one form into another.

The former is what, though improperly, is called natural history in its whole scope. Especially as botany and zoology, it teaches us to know the different organic forms, enduring through the ceaseless change of individuals and thereby firmly determined, which make up a large part of the content of intuitive representation. It classifies them, separates them, unites them, orders them according to natural and artificial systems, and brings them under concepts that make an overview and knowledge of all of them possible.

Furthermore, morphology shows an infinitely nuanced analogy running through all these forms, both in the whole and in the parts, the `unite de plan` (unity of plan), by virtue of which they resemble very many variations on a theme that is not given along with them. The transition of matter into those forms, that is, the origin of individuals, is not a main part of its consideration, since each individual proceeds from one like itself through generation. This process, everywhere equally mysterious, has so far eluded distinct cognition. The little that is known of it finds its place in physiology, which already belongs to aetiological natural science.

Mineralogy, which in the main belongs to morphology, already inclines toward aetiology as well, especially where it becomes geology. Proper aetiology consists in all those branches of natural science in which cognition of cause and effect is everywhere the main thing. These teach how, according to an infallible rule, *one* state of matter is necessarily followed by a definite other state; how a definite change necessarily conditions and brings about another definite change. This demonstration is called *explanation*. Here we find chiefly mechanics, physics, chemistry, and physiology.

But if we give ourselves over to their instruction, we soon become aware that the information we are chiefly seeking is given to us by aetiology no more than by morphology. The latter presents us with innumerable forms, infinitely varied and yet related by an unmistakable family resemblance. For us, they are representations that remain forever alien to us along this path, and, when considered merely in this way, stand before us like hieroglyphs we do not understand.

Aetiology, by contrast, teaches us that, according to the law of cause and effect, this definite state of matter brings about that other one. With this it has explained it and done what belongs to it. Yet fundamentally it does nothing more than demonstrate the lawful order according to which states enter into space and time, and teach for all cases which appearance must necessarily occur at this time and in this place. It therefore determines their place in time and space according to a law whose determinate content has been taught by experience, but whose general form and necessity are conscious to us independently of experience.

But through this we receive not the least clarification about the inner essence of any of those appearances. This is called *natural force*, and it lies outside the domain of aetiological explanation. Aetiology calls the unchanging constancy with which the manifestation of such a force occurs, whenever the known conditions for it are present, a *law of nature*. This law of nature, these conditions, this occurrence with reference to a definite place at a definite time, are all that aetiology knows and ever can know.

The force itself that manifests itself, the inner essence of the appearances that occur according to those laws, remains forever a mystery to it, something wholly alien and unknown, in the simplest as in the most complicated appearance. For although aetiology has so far reached its goal most completely in mechanics and most incompletely in physiology, the force by virtue of which a stone falls to the earth, or one body pushes another onward, is no less alien and mysterious to us in its inner essence than the force that produces the movements and growth of an animal.

Mechanics presupposes matter, gravity, impenetrability, the communicability of motion by impact, rigidity, and so on as unfathomable, calls them natural forces, calls their necessary and regular appearance under certain conditions a law of nature, and only then begins its explanation. This explanation consists in stating faithfully and with mathematical exactness how, where, and when each force manifests itself, and in reducing every appearance that comes before it to one of those forces.

Physics, chemistry, and physiology proceed in the same way in their own domains, except that they presuppose much more and accomplish less. Accordingly, even the most complete aetiological explanation of the whole of nature would never really be more than a catalogue of inexplicable forces, and a secure statement of the rule according to which their appearances enter into time and space, succeed one another, and make room for one another. But the inner essence of the forces thus appearing would always have to remain unexplained, because the law aetiology follows does not lead there. It would have to stop at the appearance and its order.

In this respect it would be comparable to a cross-section of marble, which shows many veins beside one another, but does not allow us to recognize the course of those veins inside the marble up to that surface. Or, if I may allow myself a humorous comparison because it is more striking: even with the completed aetiology of the whole of nature, the philosophical investigator would still have to feel like someone who, without knowing how, had fallen into a society entirely unknown to him, whose members, one after another, each presented another to him as his friend and cousin and thereby made him sufficiently acquainted with them. Meanwhile, each time he assured the person presented that he was pleased to meet him, he would always have the question on his lips: "But how the devil did I get into this whole company?"

Thus aetiology, too, can never give us the desired clarification that would lead us beyond those appearances which we know only as our representations. For after all its explanations, they still stand before us as mere representations whose meaning we do not understand, wholly alien. The causal connection gives only the rule and relative order of their occurrence in space and time, but does not teach us to know more closely what it is that occurs in this way.

Moreover, the law of causality itself has validity only for representations, for objects of a definite class, under whose presupposition alone it has meaning. Like these objects themselves, it therefore exists always only in relation to the subject, and hence conditionally. For this reason it is known just as much when one starts from the subject, that is, `a priori` (prior to experience), as when one starts from the object, that is, `a posteriori` (after experience), as Kant has taught us.

What now drives us to inquiry is precisely that it does not satisfy us to know that we have representations, that they are such and such, and that they are connected according to these and those laws, whose universal expression is always the principle of sufficient reason. We want to know the meaning of that representation. We ask whether this world is nothing more than representation, in which case it would have to pass by us like an insubstantial dream or a ghostly airy image, not worth our attention; or whether it is also something else, something besides representation, and what this then is.

This much is immediately certain: what is being asked about must be something completely different from representation, in its whole essence and nature. Therefore the forms and laws of representation must also be wholly foreign to it. Hence, starting from representation, one cannot reach it by following the thread of those laws that connect only objects, representations, with one another, namely the forms of the principle of sufficient reason.

We already see here that the essence of things can never be reached from *outside*. However much one investigates, one gains nothing but images and names. One resembles someone who walks around a castle, vainly seeking an entrance and meanwhile sketching the facades. And yet this is the path all philosophers before me have taken.
