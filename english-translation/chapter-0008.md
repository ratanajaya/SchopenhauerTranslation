# §. 6.

Meanwhile, for now, in this first book, we consider everything only as representation, as object for the subject. Like all other real objects, we look at our own body too, from which the intuition of the world begins for each of us, only from the side of knowability; and it is therefore for us only a representation.

To be sure, the consciousness of each person, which already rebelled against declaring other objects to be mere representations, resists still more when one's own body is supposed to be merely a representation. This comes from the fact that the thing in itself, insofar as it appears as one's own body, is known to each person immediately, while insofar as it objectifies itself in the other objects of intuition it is known to him only mediately.

But the course of our investigation makes this abstraction, this one-sided way of considering things, this forceful separation of what essentially belongs together, necessary. That resistance must therefore for the time being be suppressed and calmed by the expectation that the following considerations will supplement the one-sidedness of the present one, toward a complete cognition of the essence of the world.

Here, then, the body is immediate object for us: that is, the representation that forms the starting point of the subject's cognition. With its immediately known alterations, the body precedes the application of the law of causality and thereby supplies the first data for that application.

As has been shown, the whole essence of matter consists in its acting. But effect and cause exist only for the understanding, which is nothing other than their subjective correlate. Yet the understanding could never come into application if there were not something else from which it starts. Such a thing is mere sensory sensation, the immediate consciousness of the alterations of the body, by virtue of which the body is immediate object.

Accordingly, we find the possibility of the knowability of the intuitive world in two conditions. The first, *if we express it objectively*, is the capacity of bodies to act upon one another, to produce alterations in one another. Without this universal property of all bodies, no intuition would be possible even by means of the sensibility of animal bodies.

But if we want to express this same first condition *subjectively*, we say: above all, the understanding makes intuition possible. For the law of causality, the possibility of effect and cause, springs only from the understanding and is valid only for it; and therefore the intuitive world exists only for and through it.

The second condition is the sensibility of animal bodies, or the property of certain bodies of being immediate objects of the subject. The mere alterations that the sense organs undergo through the specifically appropriate action upon them from outside may indeed already be called representations, insofar as such actions produce neither pain nor pleasure, that is, insofar as they have no immediate significance for the will and nevertheless are perceived, and so exist only for *cognition*. In this respect, then, I say that the body is immediately *known*, that it is *immediate object*.

Yet here the concept object is not even to be taken in its most proper sense. For through this immediate cognition of the body, which precedes the application of the understanding and is mere sensory sensation, the body itself does not actually stand there as *object*. Rather, only the bodies acting upon it do so. For every cognition of a genuine object, that is, of a representation intuitable in space, exists only through and for the understanding; therefore it exists not before, but only after, the understanding has been applied.

Accordingly, the body as genuine object, that is, as intuitive representation in space, is known, like all other objects, only mediately: through application of the law of causality to the action of one of its parts upon another, as when the eye sees the body or the hand touches it. Consequently, the shape of our own body is not known to us through mere common sensation. Only through cognition, only in representation, that is, only in the brain, does one's own body first present itself as extended, articulated, and organic.

A person born blind receives this representation only gradually, through the data that touch gives him. A blind person without hands would never come to know his shape, or at most would gradually infer and construct it from the action of other bodies upon him. It is with this restriction, then, that we are to understand our calling the body immediate object.

Moreover, according to what has been said, all animal bodies are immediate objects, that is, starting points of the intuition of the world, for the subject that knows everything and, precisely for that reason, is never known. *Cognition*, together with movement according to motives conditioned by cognition, is therefore the true *character of animality*, just as movement according to stimuli is the character of the plant. The inorganic, however, has no movement other than that effected by causes proper in the narrowest sense. I have discussed all this more fully in the essay on the principle of sufficient reason, 2nd ed., §. 20; in the *Ethics*, first essay, III; and in *On Vision and Colors*, §. 1, to which I therefore refer.

It follows from what has been said that all animals have understanding, even the most imperfect; for they all cognize objects, and this cognition determines their movements as motive.

The understanding is the same in all animals and all human beings. Everywhere it has the same simple form: cognition of causality, transition from effect to cause and from cause to effect, and nothing besides. But the degrees of its sharpness and the extent of its sphere of cognition are extremely different, manifold, and graduated in many stages.

At the lowest degree, the understanding cognizes only the causal relation between the immediate object and mediated objects. This is just enough, by passing from the action undergone by the body to its cause, to intuit that cause as object in space. At higher degrees, the understanding cognizes the causal connection of merely mediated objects among one another, and this extends as far as understanding the most complex linkages of causes and effects in nature.

Even this latter achievement still belongs to the understanding, not to reason. Reason's abstract concepts can serve only to receive what has been immediately understood, to fix it, and to connect it; they can never produce understanding itself.

Every force of nature, every law of nature, and every case in which such a force or law manifests itself must first be immediately cognized by the understanding, grasped intuitively, before it can enter reflective consciousness `in abstracto` (in the abstract) for reason. Robert Hooke's discovery of the law of gravitation, and his reduction of so many great appearances to this one law, which Newton's calculations then confirmed, was intuitive, immediate apprehension through the understanding. The same was true of Lavoisier's discovery of oxygen and its important role in nature; the same was true of Goethe's discovery of the way physical colors arise.

All these discoveries are nothing other than a correct, immediate return from the effect to the cause. Immediately after this comes cognition of the identity of the force of nature manifesting itself in all causes of the same kind. This entire insight is a manifestation, differing only in degree, of the same single function of the understanding through which an animal too intuits, as object in space, the cause acting upon its body.

Therefore all those great discoveries, just like intuition and every manifestation of the understanding, are immediate insight and, as such, the work of the moment: an `aperçu` (sudden insight), a flash, not the product of long chains of inference `in abstracto`. Such chains serve instead to fix immediate cognition by the understanding for reason by laying it down in reason's abstract concepts; that is, to make it distinct, and thereby to put oneself in a position to explain it and communicate it to others.

That sharpness of understanding in apprehending the causal relations of mediately known objects finds its application not only in natural science, whose entire discoveries are owed to it, but also in practical life, where it is called *prudence*. In the former application, however, it is better called acuteness, penetration, and sagacity. Strictly speaking, *prudence* designates exclusively the understanding in the service of the will.

Still, the boundaries of these concepts can never be drawn sharply. It is always one and the same function of the same understanding, already active in every animal in the intuition of objects in space, which, at its greatest sharpness, sometimes correctly investigates from a given effect in the appearances of nature the unknown cause and so gives reason the material for thinking universal rules as laws of nature; sometimes invents complicated and ingenious machines by applying known causes to intended effects; and sometimes, applied to motivation, either sees through and frustrates fine intrigues and machinations, or itself properly sets up the motives and the people receptive to each of them, and moves them at will, like machines by levers and wheels, directing them to its own ends.

Lack of understanding is called, in the proper sense, *stupidity*, and is precisely *dullness in the application of the law of causality*: incapacity for the immediate apprehension of the linkages of cause and effect, motive and action.

A stupid person does not see into the connection of natural appearances, whether they emerge when left to themselves or when they are intentionally directed, that is, made serviceable in machines. For this reason he readily believes in magic and miracles. A stupid person does not notice that different people, apparently independent of one another, are in fact acting in an agreed connection; he is therefore easily mystified and drawn into intrigues. He does not notice the hidden motives of advice given, judgments expressed, and so on. Yet what he always lacks is only one thing: sharpness, quickness, ease in applying the law of causality, that is, power of understanding.

The greatest and, for the respect under consideration, most instructive example of stupidity I ever encountered was a completely feeble-minded boy of about eleven in an asylum. He did have reason, since he spoke and understood speech, but in understanding he stood below many animals. Whenever I came, he would look at an eyeglass that I wore around my neck, in which, by reflection, the room's windows and the treetops beyond them appeared. Each time he showed great wonder and joy at this, and never tired of looking at it in astonishment, because he did not understand this wholly immediate causality of reflection.

Just as among human beings the degrees of sharpness of understanding are very different, they are probably still more different among the various animal species. In all animals, even those that stand closest to the plant, there is still enough understanding for the transition from the effect in the immediate object to the mediated object as cause, and therefore for intuition, for the apprehension of an object. This is precisely what makes them animals, since it gives them the possibility of movement according to motives and thereby of seeking, or at least seizing, nourishment. Plants, by contrast, have only movement according to stimuli; they must wait for the immediate action of these stimuli or wither away, and cannot go after them or seize them.

In the most perfect animals we admire their great sagacity: in the dog, the elephant, the ape, and the fox, whose prudence Buffon has described so masterfully. From these cleverest animals we can measure fairly accurately how much the understanding can achieve without the aid of reason, that is, without abstract cognition in concepts. We cannot know this so clearly in ourselves, because in us understanding and reason always support each other reciprocally.

For this reason we often find the manifestations of understanding in animals now above, now below our expectation. On the one hand, we are surprised by the sagacity of an elephant who, after having already crossed many bridges on his journey through Europe, once refuses to step onto a bridge over which he sees, as usual, the rest of the procession of people and horses crossing, because it seems to him too lightly built for his weight. On the other hand, we are surprised that clever orangutans do not maintain a fire they have found and at which they warm themselves by adding wood to it. This proves that doing so already requires a deliberation that does not come about without abstract concepts.

That cognition of cause and effect, as the universal form of the understanding, dwells even in animals `a priori` (prior to experience) is already completely certain from the fact that, for them as for us, it is the preceding condition of all intuitive cognition of the external world. If, however, one wants a special proof of this, one need only observe, for example, how even a very young dog does not dare to jump from a table, however much he wants to, because he foresees the effect of the weight of his body, even though he does not already know this particular case from experience.

In judging the understanding of animals, however, we must beware of attributing to it what is a manifestation of instinct. Instinct is a property entirely different from understanding as well as from reason, though it often acts in a way very analogous to the combined activity of both. Its discussion, however, does not belong here; it will have its place in the second book, when we consider the harmony, or so-called teleology, of nature, and chapter 27 of the supplements is specially devoted to it.

Lack of *understanding* was called *stupidity*. Later we shall recognize lack of application of *reason* to practical matters as *folly*; lack of *power of judgment* as *simple-mindedness*; and partial or even complete lack of *memory* as *madness*. But each of these in its own place.

What is correctly cognized by *reason* is *truth*: namely, an abstract judgment with sufficient ground (essay on the principle of sufficient reason, §. 29 ff.). What is correctly cognized by the *understanding* is *reality*: namely, correct transition from the effect in the immediate object to its cause. Opposed to *truth* is *error*, as deception of *reason*; opposed to *reality* is *semblance*, as deception of the *understanding*. A fuller discussion of all this may be read in the first chapter of my essay *On Vision and Colors*.

*Semblance* arises when one and the same effect can be brought about by two entirely different causes, one of which acts very frequently, the other rarely. The understanding has no datum for distinguishing which cause is acting here, since the effect is exactly the same; it therefore always presupposes the usual cause. And because its activity is not reflective and discursive, but direct and immediate, such a false cause stands before us as an intuited object. This is false semblance.

I have shown in the place cited how double vision and double touch arise in this way when the sense organs are brought into an unusual position, and have thereby given an irrefutable proof that intuition exists only through the understanding and for the understanding.

Further examples of such deception of the understanding, or semblance, are the stick dipped into water, which appears broken; the images of spherical mirrors, which appear somewhat behind the surface in convex mirrors and far in front of it in concave mirrors. Also belonging here is the apparently greater extension of the moon at the horizon than at the zenith. This is not optical, for, as the micrometer proves, the eye takes in the moon at the zenith at an angle of vision that is even somewhat larger than at the horizon. Rather, it is the understanding that assumes a greater distance as the cause of the weaker brightness of the moon and all stars at the horizon, estimating them like earthly objects according to aerial perspective. It therefore takes the moon at the horizon to be much larger than at the zenith, and at the same time takes the vault of heaven to be more extended at the horizon, that is, flattened.

The same wrongly applied estimate according to aerial perspective makes us take very high mountains, whose summits alone are visible to us in pure, transparent air, to be nearer than they are, to the detriment of their height: for example, Mont Blanc as seen from Salenche.

All such deceptive semblances stand before us in immediate intuition, and no reasoning of reason can remove them. Reasoning can only prevent error, that is, a judgment without sufficient ground, by means of an opposed true judgment. Thus one can cognize `in abstracto` that the cause of the weaker brightness of the moon and stars is not their greater distance, but the hazier vapors at the horizon. But the semblance remains immovable in all the cases mentioned, despite every abstract cognition.

For the understanding is completely and sharply distinct from reason, a faculty of cognition that has been added only in the human being; and the understanding in itself is, even in the human being, without reason. Reason can only ever *know*; intuition remains for the understanding alone, free from reason's influence.
