# §. 7.

With regard to our whole consideration so far, the following should still be carefully noted. We have not started from the object, nor from the subject, but from *representation*, which already contains and presupposes them both; for division into object and subject is its first, most universal, and most essential form.

We therefore first considered this form as such. Then, though here referring mainly to the introductory essay, we considered the other forms subordinate to it: time, space, and causality. These belong only to the *object*; yet because they are essential to the object *as such*, while the object is in turn essential to the subject *as such*, they can also be found from the side of the subject, that is, cognized `a priori` (prior to experience), and in this respect they are to be regarded as the common boundary of both. All of them, however, can be traced back to one common expression, the principle of sufficient reason, as is shown in detail in the introductory essay.

This procedure distinguishes our way of considering things entirely from all philosophies ever attempted. Those philosophies all started either from the object or from the subject, and accordingly tried to explain one from the other, indeed according to the principle of sufficient reason. We, by contrast, withdraw the relation between object and subject from the dominion of that principle, leaving to it only the object.

One might think that the identity philosophy, which has arisen in our own day and become generally known, is not included under the opposition just stated. For it makes neither object nor subject the true first starting point, but a third thing: the absolute, knowable through rational intuition, which is neither object nor subject, but the sameness of both.

Although, from complete lack of all rational intuition, I shall not presume to join in speaking of that venerable sameness and the absolute, I must nevertheless, relying only on the records of the rational intuiters that lie open to everyone, even to us profane people, observe that this philosophy is not exempt from the opposition of the two errors set out above. For despite the identity of subject and object, which is not thinkable but only intellectually intuitable, or to be experienced by sinking oneself into it, that philosophy still does not avoid the two opposed errors. Rather, it merely unites both within itself.

It divides into two disciplines. The first is transcendental idealism, which is Fichte's doctrine of the I and accordingly, after the principle of sufficient reason, has the object produced by, or spun out of, the subject. The second is philosophy of nature, which likewise lets the subject gradually come to be out of the object, by applying a method called construction. Of this method very little is clear to me, but this much is clear: it is a progression according to the principle of sufficient reason in various forms.

I renounce the deep wisdom contained in that construction; for to me, who entirely lack rational intuition, all lectures that presuppose it must be a book sealed with seven seals. Indeed this is so much the case that, strange to say, with those doctrines of deep wisdom it always seems to me as if I heard nothing but dreadful, and moreover extremely boring, bombast.

The systems that start from the object have indeed always had the whole intuitive world and its order as their problem. Yet the object they take as their starting point is not always this world itself, nor its basic element, matter. Rather, in accordance with the four classes of possible objects set out in the introductory essay, one can divide those systems as follows.

One may say that the following started from the first of those classes, or the real world: Thales and the Ionians, Democritus, Epicurus, Giordano Bruno, and the French materialists. From the second class, or the abstract concept, started Spinoza, namely from the merely abstract concept of substance, which exists only in its definition; and earlier, the Eleatics. From the third class, namely time and therefore numbers, started the Pythagoreans and Chinese philosophy in the *I Ching*. Finally, from the fourth class, namely the act of will motivated by cognition, started the Scholastics, who teach a creation from nothing through the act of will of an extra-worldly personal being.

The objective procedure is most consistent and can be carried out furthest when it appears as materialism proper. Materialism posits matter, and with it time and space, as existing absolutely, and skips over the relation to the subject in which all this alone exists. It further takes the law of causality as the guiding thread along which it wants to advance, taking it as an order of things existing in itself, a `veritas aeterna` (eternal truth), and thus skipping over the understanding, in which and for which alone causality exists.

Materialism then seeks to find the first, simplest state of matter, and from it to develop all others, ascending from mere mechanism to chemism, polarity, vegetation, and animality. Suppose this succeeded: the last link of the chain would be animal sensibility, cognition, which would then appear as a mere modification of matter, a state of matter brought about by causality.

Now if we had followed materialism this far with intuitive representations, then, once we arrived with it at its summit, we would feel a sudden attack of the inextinguishable laughter of the Olympians. Waking as if from a dream, we would suddenly become aware that its final result, cognition, brought about with such effort, was already presupposed as an indispensable condition at the very first starting point, mere matter. We had imagined, with materialism, that we were thinking matter, but in fact we had thought nothing other than the subject representing matter, the eye that sees it, the hand that feels it, the understanding that cognizes it.

Thus the enormous `petitio principii` (begging of the question) unexpectedly reveals itself: the last link suddenly appears as the support on which the first already hung, and the chain as a circle. The materialist resembles Baron Munchausen, who, while swimming on horseback in the water, pulls the horse upward with his legs and himself upward by the braid thrown forward over his head.

Accordingly, the fundamental absurdity of materialism consists in this: it starts from the *objective*, and takes something *objective* as the ultimate ground of explanation. This may be matter `in abstracto` (in the abstract), as it is only *thought*, or it may be matter already entered into form, empirically given, and thus material stuff: perhaps the chemical elements, together with their nearest compounds. Materialism takes such things as existing in themselves and absolutely, in order to let organic nature and finally the knowing subject arise from them, thereby explaining both completely.

In truth, however, everything objective, already as such, is conditioned in manifold ways by the knowing subject and by the forms of its cognition, and has these as its presupposition. Therefore it disappears entirely if one thinks the subject away.

Materialism is thus the attempt to explain what is immediately given to us from what is given only mediately. Everything objective, extended, acting, and therefore everything material, which materialism regards as so solid a foundation for its explanations that a reduction to it, especially if it finally came down to impact and counter-impact, could leave nothing more to be desired: all this is given only in the highest degree mediately and conditionally, and is therefore only relatively present. For it has passed through the machinery and fabrication of the brain, and has therefore entered into its forms, time, space, and causality, by virtue of which alone it presents itself as extended in space and acting in time.

From something given in this way, materialism now wants to explain even what is immediately given: representation, in which all that stands there, and in the end even the will, from which, on the contrary, all those fundamental forces that manifest themselves along the guiding thread of causes, and therefore lawfully, are in truth to be explained.

The assertion that cognition is a modification of matter is therefore always opposed, with equal right, by the reverse assertion that all matter is only a modification of the subject's cognition, as its representation. Nevertheless, the goal and ideal of all natural science is, at bottom, a completely carried out materialism. That we recognize this here as obviously impossible confirms another truth that will result from our further consideration: that all science in the strict sense, by which I mean systematic cognition along the guiding thread of the principle of sufficient reason, can never reach a final goal or give a fully satisfying explanation, because it never reaches the innermost essence of the world, never gets beyond representation, and at bottom teaches nothing more than the relation of one representation to another.

Every science always starts from two chief data. One of these is always the principle of sufficient reason in some form as organon; the other is its particular object as problem. Geometry, for example, has space as its problem and the ground of being in space as its organon. Arithmetic has time as problem and the ground of being in time as organon. Logic has the connections of concepts as such for its problem, and the ground of knowing as organon. History has the deeds of human beings that have happened, on a large scale and in the mass, for its problem, and the law of motivation as organon.

Natural science has matter as its problem and the law of causality as organon. Its aim and purpose, accordingly, is to reduce all possible states of matter to one another, and finally to one state, along the guiding thread of causality; and again to derive them from one another, and finally from one state.

Two states therefore stand opposed in natural science as extremes: the state of matter in which it is least immediate object of the subject, and the state in which it is most so; that is, the deadest, crudest matter, the first basic stuff, and then the human organism. Natural science seeks the first as chemistry, the second as physiology. Up to now, however, both extremes remain unreached, and only something between them has been gained. The prospect is also rather hopeless.

The chemists, on the assumption that the qualitative division of matter will not go on to infinity as the quantitative division does, seek to reduce more and more the number of their elements, still now about sixty. And if they had arrived at two, they would want to reduce these to one. For the law of homogeneity leads to the assumption of a first chemical state of matter, preceding all others, which are not essential to matter as such but only accidental forms or qualities, and which alone belongs to matter as such.

On the other hand, it is impossible to see how this first state, since there was not yet a second state to act upon it, could ever have undergone a chemical alteration. Here in chemistry, therefore, the same embarrassment appears that Epicurus encountered in mechanics when he had to explain how the first atom deviated from the original direction of its motion. Indeed, this contradiction, which develops entirely of itself and can neither be avoided nor resolved, could quite properly be set up as a chemical *antinomy*. As it appears here at the first of the two extremes sought by natural science, so at the second a corresponding counterpart will show itself to us.

There is just as little hope of reaching this other extreme of natural science, since it is becoming increasingly clear that something chemical can never be reduced to something mechanical, nor something organic to something chemical or electrical. Those who today strike out again on this old wrong path will soon creep back, like all their predecessors, silently and ashamed. More will be said about this in the following book.

The difficulties mentioned only in passing here confront natural science on its own territory. Taken as philosophy, however, natural science would furthermore be materialism; and materialism, as we have seen, carries death in its heart from birth, because it skips over the subject and the forms of cognition. These are presupposed just as much by the crudest matter from which materialism would like to begin as by the organism at which it would like to arrive.

For "no object without subject" is the proposition that forever makes all materialism impossible. Suns and planets without an eye that sees them and an understanding that cognizes them can certainly be said in words; but for representation these words are a `Sideroxylon` (ironwood).

Yet on the other hand, the law of causality and the consideration and investigation of nature that follows it necessarily leads us to the secure assumption that, in time, every more highly organized state of matter has followed only after a cruder one: animals before human beings, fish before land animals, plants before even these, the inorganic before everything organic. Consequently, the original mass had to pass through a long series of alterations before the first eye could open.

And yet the existence of that whole world always remains dependent on this first eye that opened, even if it belonged to an insect, as the necessary mediator of cognition for which and in which alone the world is and without which it cannot even be thought. For the world is representation through and through, and as such requires the knowing subject as bearer of its existence.

Indeed, that long temporal series itself, filled with countless alterations through which matter rose from form to form until at last the first knowing animal came to be, is itself thinkable only in the identity of a consciousness whose succession of representations, whose form of cognition, it is; outside that consciousness, it loses all meaning and is nothing at all.

Thus we see, on the one hand, the existence of the whole world necessarily dependent on the first knowing being, however imperfect it may be; and, on the other hand, this first knowing animal just as necessarily entirely dependent on a long chain of causes and effects that preceded it, into which it enters as a small link.

These two contradictory views, to each of which we are in fact led with equal necessity, could indeed again be called an *antinomy* in our faculty of cognition, and set up as the counterpart to the one found at the first extreme of natural science. Kant's fourfold antinomy, by contrast, will be shown in the critique of his philosophy appended to the present work to be a baseless sham fight.

The contradiction that has necessarily arisen for us here, however, finds its resolution in the fact that, to speak in Kant's language, time, space, and causality do not belong to the thing in itself, but only to its appearance, whose form they are. In my language this means that the objective world, the world as representation, is not the only side of the world, but only one side, as it were the outer side, which still has a wholly different side: its innermost essence, its kernel, the thing in itself. This we shall consider in the following book, naming it, after the most immediate of its objectivations, will.

The world as representation, however, which alone we are considering here, does indeed begin only with the opening of the first eye. Without this medium of cognition it cannot be, and therefore it also was not before. But without that eye, that is, outside cognition, there was also no before, no time.

For this reason, time has no beginning; rather, every beginning is in time. But since time is the most universal form of knowability, into which all appearances fit by means of the bond of causality, time too stands there, with its whole infinity on both sides, with the first cognition. The appearance that fills this first present must at the same time be known as causally connected with, and dependent on, a series of appearances extending infinitely into the past. Yet this past itself is just as much conditioned by this first present as, conversely, this present is conditioned by that past.

Thus, like the first present, the past from which it comes depends on the knowing subject and is nothing without it. Nevertheless, it brings with it the necessity that this first present presents itself not as the first, not as having no past for mother and as the beginning of time, but as the consequence of the past according to the ground of being in time; and likewise that the appearance filling it presents itself as the effect of earlier states filling that past, according to the law of causality.

Anyone fond of mythological interpretations may regard the birth of Kronos, `χρόνος` (time), the youngest Titan, as a symbol of the moment expressed here: the entrance of time, which nevertheless has no beginning. For with Kronos, since he castrates his father, the crude products of heaven and earth cease, and now the race of gods and human beings takes the stage.

This presentation, which we reached by following the most consistent of the philosophical systems that start from the object, materialism, also serves to make intuitively clear the inseparable mutual dependence, along with the irreducible opposition, between subject and object. This cognition leads us to seek the innermost essence of the world, the thing in itself, no longer in either of those two elements of representation, but rather in something wholly different from representation, something not afflicted with such an original, essential, and irresolvable opposition.

The procedure just discussed, which starts from the object in order to let the subject arise from it, is opposed by the procedure that starts from the subject and wants to make the object issue from it. As frequent and general as the former procedure has been in all previous philosophy, there is properly only a single example of the latter, and a very recent one: the sham philosophy of J. G. Fichte. In this respect it must be noted, however little genuine value and inner content his doctrine had in itself, indeed however much it was altogether only a sham fight. Presented with the air of deepest seriousness, sustained tone, and lively zeal, and defended with eloquent polemic against weak opponents, it could nevertheless shine and seem to be something.

But the genuine seriousness that, inaccessible to all external influences, keeps its goal, truth, steadily in view was entirely lacking in him, as in all similar philosophers who accommodate themselves to circumstances. This could hardly have been otherwise. One always becomes a philosopher through a perplexity from which one seeks to free oneself; this is Plato's `θαυμάζειν` (wonder), which he calls a `μάλα φιλοσοφικόν πάθος` (a very philosophical passion).

Here, however, what separates inauthentic philosophers from authentic ones is this: for the latter, that perplexity arises from looking at the world itself; for the former, it arises only from a book, from an existing system. This was Fichte's case, for he became a philosopher only over Kant's thing in itself, and without it he would most likely have pursued quite different things with much better success, since he possessed considerable rhetorical talent.

Had he penetrated even somewhat deeply into the meaning of the book that made him a philosopher, the *Critique of Pure Reason*, he would have understood that its main doctrine, according to its spirit, is this: the principle of sufficient reason is not, as all scholastic philosophy maintains, a `veritas aeterna` (eternal truth), that is, it does not have unconditional validity before, outside, and above all the world. Rather, it has only relative and conditioned validity, valid solely in appearance, whether it appears as the necessary nexus of space or of time, or as the law of causality, or as the law of the ground of knowledge.

Therefore the inner essence of the world, the thing in itself, can never be found along the guiding thread of that principle. Everything to which the principle leads is always itself again dependent and relative, always only appearance, not thing in itself. Further, the principle does not concern the subject at all, but is only the form of objects, which precisely for that reason are not things in themselves; and with the object the subject is already immediately there, and with the subject the object is there. Thus neither can the object first be added to the subject, nor the subject to the object, as consequent to its ground.

But none of this adhered in the least to Fichte. What alone interested him in the matter was the *starting from the subject*, which Kant had chosen in order to show that the previous starting from the object, which had thereby become the thing in itself, was false. Fichte, however, took this starting from the subject to be the essential point. Like all imitators, he thought that if he outdid Kant in this, he would also surpass him; and so he repeated in this direction the errors that previous dogmatism had committed in the opposite direction, and which had occasioned Kant's critique.

Thus in the main matter nothing had changed. The old fundamental error, the assumption of a relation of ground and consequent between object and subject, remained as before. The principle of sufficient reason therefore retained, just as before, unconditional validity; and the thing in itself, instead of being placed as formerly in the object, was now placed in the subject of cognition. The complete relativity of these two, which indicates that the thing in itself, or inner essence of the world, is to be sought not in them but outside this pair, as outside everything else that exists only relatively, remained unrecognized as before.

As if Kant had never existed, the principle of sufficient reason is still for Fichte exactly what it was for all the Scholastics: an `aeterna veritas` (eternal truth). Just as eternal fate still ruled over the gods of antiquity, so those `aeternae veritates` (eternal truths), that is, the metaphysical, mathematical, and metalogical truths, and for some also the validity of the moral law, still ruled over the God of the Scholastics. These truths alone depended on nothing; through their necessity, however, both God and world existed.

According to the principle of sufficient reason as such a `veritas aeterna`, the I is therefore, for Fichte, the ground of the world or of the non-I, of the object, which is precisely its consequent, its product. He therefore carefully avoided examining or checking the principle of sufficient reason any further.

If I had to state the form of that principle along whose guiding thread Fichte lets the non-I emerge from the I, as the web from the spider, I find that it is the principle of the ground of being in space. For only with reference to this do those painful deductions of the way in which the I produces and fabricates the non-I out of itself, which make up the content of the most senseless and therefore most boring book ever written, receive any sort of sense and meaning.

This Fichtean philosophy, otherwise not even worth mentioning, is therefore interesting to us only as the late-appearing true opposite of ancient materialism, which was the most consistent starting from the object, just as Fichte's philosophy was the starting from the subject.

Just as materialism overlooked that with the simplest object it had immediately also posited the subject, so Fichte overlooked that with the subject, whatever title he wished to give it, he had not only already posited the object, since no subject can be thought without one; he also overlooked that all derivation `a priori`, indeed all proof in general, rests on necessity, while all necessity rests solely on the principle of sufficient reason. For being necessary and following from a given ground are interchangeable concepts. [^1]

But the principle of sufficient reason is nothing other than the universal form of the object as such, and therefore already presupposes the object. It cannot, valid before and outside the object, first bring the object about and let it arise in conformity with its legislation. In general, then, the starting from the subject shares with the starting from the object presented above the same error: it assumes in advance what it claims first to derive, namely the necessary correlate of its starting point.

Our procedure is distinguished from both these opposed mistakes `toto genere` (in kind altogether), since we start neither from the object nor from the subject, but from *representation*, as the first fact of consciousness. The first, most essential basic form of representation is the division into object and subject. The form of the object, again, is the principle of sufficient reason in its various forms, each of which governs its own class of representations so thoroughly that, as shown, with cognition of that form the essence of the whole class is also cognized, since the class, as representation, is nothing other than that form itself.

Thus time itself is nothing other than the ground of being in it, that is, succession; space nothing other than the principle of sufficient reason in it, that is, position; matter nothing other than causality; the concept, as will immediately appear, nothing other than relation to the ground of knowledge.

This complete and thoroughgoing relativity of the world as representation, both according to its most universal form, subject and object, and according to the form subordinate to this, the principle of sufficient reason, points us, as said, toward seeking the inner essence of the world on an entirely different side of it, one *wholly different from representation*. The next book will show this in a fact equally immediately certain to every living being.

Before that, however, we must still consider the class of representations that belongs to the human being alone, whose material is the *concept* and whose subjective correlate is *reason*, just as the subjective correlates of the representations considered up to now were understanding and sensibility, which are also to be attributed to every animal.
