# §. 18.

In fact, the meaning we are seeking in the world that stands before me merely as my representation, or the transition from it as mere representation of the knowing subject to whatever it may be besides this, could never be found if the investigator himself were nothing more than the purely knowing subject, a winged angel's head without a body.

But he himself is rooted in that world. He finds himself in it as an *individual*; that is, his cognition, which is the conditioning bearer of the whole world as representation, is nevertheless thoroughly mediated by a body. As was shown, the affections of this body are the starting point from which the understanding intuits that world.

For the purely knowing subject as such, this body is a representation like any other, an object among objects. Its movements and actions are, to that extent, known to him in no other way than the changes of all other intuitive objects. They would be just as alien and incomprehensible to him if their meaning had not been deciphered for him in an entirely different way.

Otherwise he would see his action follow upon presented motives with the constancy of a law of nature, just as the changes of other objects follow upon causes, stimuli, and motives. But he would understand the influence of motives no more intimately than the connection between any other effect that appears to him and its cause. He would then call the inner, incomprehensible essence of those expressions and actions of his body a force, a quality, or a character, as he pleased, but he would have no further insight into it.

But this is not how things stand. Rather, to the subject of cognition who appears as an individual, the word of the riddle has been given; and this word is *will*. This, and this alone, gives him the key to his own appearance, reveals its meaning to him, and shows him the inner workings of his being, his doing, and his movements.

To the subject of cognition, who appears as an individual through his identity with the body, this body is given in two entirely different ways: first, as representation in intuition through the understanding, as an object among objects and subject to their laws; then also, at the same time, in an entirely different way, namely as that which is immediately known to everyone and which the word *will* designates.

Every true act of his will is at once and inevitably also a movement of his body. He cannot actually will the act without at the same time perceiving that it appears as a movement of the body. The act of will and the action of the body are not two different states objectively known, connected by the bond of causality; they do not stand in the relation of cause and effect. Rather, they are one and the same thing, given only in two entirely different ways: once quite immediately, and once in intuition for the understanding.

The action of the body is nothing other than the objectified act of will, that is, the act of will that has entered into intuition. Later it will become clear to us that this holds of every movement of the body, not only of movement following upon motives, but also of involuntary movement following upon mere stimuli; indeed, that the whole body is nothing other than the objectified will, that is, will become representation. All this will emerge and become clear in what follows.

I will therefore call the body, which in the previous book and in the essay on the principle of sufficient reason I called the *immediate object*, according to the standpoint deliberately taken there from one side only, namely the standpoint of representation, here, in another respect, the *objecthood of the will*. In a certain sense one can therefore also say: the will is the cognition `a priori` (prior to experience) of the body, and the body is the cognition `a posteriori` (after experience) of the will.

Resolutions of the will that relate to the future are mere deliberations of reason about what one will someday will, not genuine acts of will. Only execution stamps the resolution as such; until then it is always only a changeable intention, and exists only in reason, `in abstracto` (in the abstract). Only in reflection are willing and doing different; in actuality they are one.

Every true, genuine, immediate act of will is at once and immediately also an appearing act of the body. Correspondingly, on the other hand, every effect upon the body is at once and immediately also an effect upon the will. As such, it is called pain when it is contrary to the will, and well-being or pleasure when it accords with the will. The gradations of both are very different.

But one is entirely wrong to call pain and pleasure representations. They are by no means representations, but immediate affections of the will in its appearance, the body: a forced, momentary willing or not-willing of the impression the body undergoes.

Only a few impressions on the body are to be considered immediately as mere representations, and therefore exempted from what has just been said. These are the only impressions through which the body is the immediate object of cognition; for as intuition in the understanding it is already a mediate object, like all others. I mean here the affections of the purely objective senses, sight, hearing, and touch, though only insofar as these organs are affected in the special, specific, natural way proper to them. Such affection is an extremely weak stimulation of the heightened and specifically modified sensibility of these parts, so weak that it does not affect the will. Undisturbed by any arousal of the will, it merely supplies the understanding with the data from which intuition arises.

Every stronger or different kind of affection of those sense organs, however, is painful, that is, contrary to the will, and therefore also belongs to its objecthood.

Weakness of the nerves shows itself in the fact that impressions which should have only the degree of strength sufficient to make them data for the understanding reach the higher degree at which they move the will, that is, arouse pain or well-being. More often they arouse pain, though partly dull and indistinct; hence not only are individual tones and strong light felt painfully, but in general a morbid hypochondriacal mood is also produced, without being distinctly recognized.

Furthermore, the identity of body and will shows itself, among other things, in the fact that every violent and excessive movement of the will, that is, every affect, immediately shakes the body and its inner mechanism and disturbs the course of its vital functions. This is set out in detail in *On Will in Nature*, p. 27 of the second edition.

Finally, although the cognition I have of my will is immediate, it is nevertheless not to be separated from cognition of my body. I do not know my will as a whole, not as a unity, not completely according to its essence. I know it only in its individual acts, and therefore in time, which is the form of the appearance of my body, as of every object. Hence the body is the condition of cognition of my will. Accordingly, I cannot really represent this will without my body.

In the essay on the principle of sufficient reason, the will, or rather the subject of willing, is indeed presented as a special class of representations or objects. Yet even there we saw this object coincide with the subject, that is, precisely cease to be an object. There we called this coincidence the miracle `kat' exochen` (par excellence). In a certain sense, the whole present work is the explanation of that miracle.

Insofar as I actually know my will as an object, I know it as body. But then I am once again with the first class of representations established in that essay, that is, with real objects. As we proceed, we will see more and more that that first class of representations finds its clarification, its deciphering, only in the fourth class established there, which no longer really wanted to stand opposed to the subject as object. Correspondingly, we must learn to understand, from the law of motivation that governs the fourth class, the inner essence of the law of causality that holds in the first class, and of what occurs according to it.

The identity of the will and the body, which has now been provisionally set forth, can only be shown as it has been here, for the first time, and as it will be shown more and more as we proceed. That is, it can be raised from immediate consciousness, from cognition `in concreto` (in the concrete), to the knowledge of reason, or transferred into cognition `in abstracto` (in the abstract).

By its nature, however, it can never be proved, that is, derived as mediate cognition from some other immediate cognition, precisely because it is itself the most immediate cognition. If we do not grasp and hold fast to it as such, we will wait in vain to receive it again in some mediate way, as derived cognition.

It is cognition of an entirely distinctive kind. For this reason, its truth cannot even properly be placed under any of the four rubrics into which, in the essay on the principle of sufficient reason, section 29 and following, I divided all truth: logical, empirical, metaphysical, and metalogical. For it is not, as all of those are, the relation of an abstract representation to another representation, or to the necessary form of intuitive or abstract representing. Rather, it is the relation of a judgment to the relation that an intuitive representation, the body, has to what is not representation at all, but something different from it `toto genere` (in kind altogether): will.

For this reason I would like to distinguish this truth above all others and call it, `kat' exochen` (par excellence), *philosophical truth*. Its expression can be turned in various ways, and one may say: my body and my will are one; or, what as intuitive representation I call my body, insofar as I am conscious of it in an entirely different way, comparable with no other, I call my will; or, my body is the *objecthood* of my will; or, apart from the fact that my body is my representation, it is only my will; and so on.[^1]

[^1]: On this, chapter 18 of the second volume.
