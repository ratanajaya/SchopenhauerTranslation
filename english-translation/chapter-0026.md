# §. 24.

We have learned from the great Kant that time, space, and causality, in all their lawfulness and in the possibility of all their forms, are present in our consciousness quite independently of the objects that appear in them and make up their content. Or, in other words, they can be found just as well if one starts from the subject as if one starts from the object. For this reason one can with equal right call them modes of intuition belonging to the subject, or also properties of the object *insofar as it is object* (in Kant: appearance), that is, *representation*.

One can also regard these forms as the indivisible boundary between object and subject. Hence every object must indeed appear in them, but the subject too, independently of the appearing object, possesses and surveys them completely.

Now if the objects appearing in these forms are not to be empty phantoms, but are to have a meaning, then they must point to something, must be the expression of something that is not again, like themselves, object, representation, something present only relatively, namely for a subject. They must express something that exists without this dependence on something standing over against it as its essential condition, and without dependence on that thing's forms. That is, they must express precisely something that is *not representation*, but a *thing in itself*.

Accordingly, one could at least ask: are those representations, those objects, still something besides and apart from the fact that they are representations, objects of the subject? And what would they then be in this sense? What is their other side, different from representation `toto genere` (in kind altogether)? What is the thing in itself? *The will*: this has been our answer, though for the moment I set it aside.

Whatever the thing in itself may be, Kant rightly concluded that time, space, and causality (which we later recognized as forms of the principle of sufficient reason, and that principle as the general expression of the forms of appearance) could not be determinations of it. They could belong to it only after and insofar as it had become representation. Thus they belonged only to its appearance, not to it itself.

For since the subject recognizes and constructs them completely from itself, independently of every object, they must attach to *being-representation* as such, not to that which becomes representation. They must be the form of representation as such, not properties of what has assumed this form. They must already be given with the mere opposition of subject and object, not in concept but in fact, and therefore must be only the more precise determination of the form of cognition in general, whose most universal determination is that opposition itself.

Now whatever in appearance, in the object, is in turn conditioned by time, space, and causality, because it can be represented only by means of them, namely *multiplicity*, through alongside-one-another and after-one-another; *change and duration*, through the law of causality; matter, which is representable only under the presupposition of causality; and finally everything that is representable only by means of these: all this together does not essentially belong to *that* which appears, *that* which has entered into the form of representation. It belongs only to this form itself.

Conversely, however, what in appearance is *not* conditioned by time, space, and causality, cannot be traced back to them, and cannot be explained according to them, will be precisely that in which what appears, the thing in itself, immediately announces itself.

Accordingly, the most perfect knowability, that is, the greatest clarity, distinctness, and exhaustive comprehensibility, will necessarily belong to what is proper to cognition *as such*, and therefore to the *form* of cognition. It will not belong to what, in itself, is *not* representation, *not* object, and becomes knowable, that is, becomes representation and object, only by entering into these forms.

Only what depends solely on being known, on being-representation in general and as such (not on what is known, and only then has become representation), what therefore belongs without distinction to everything that is known, and for just this reason is found just as well when one starts from the subject as when one starts from the object: this alone can provide an unreservedly sufficient cognition, completely exhaustive and clear down to the final ground.

This consists in nothing other than the forms of all appearance of which we are conscious `a priori` (prior to experience), and which can be jointly expressed as the principle of sufficient reason. Its forms relating to intuitive cognition, with which alone we are concerned here, are time, space, and causality. On these alone are founded all pure mathematics and pure natural science `a priori` (prior to experience).

Only in these sciences, therefore, does cognition find no darkness, does it not run up against the unfathomable (the groundless, that is, will), against what cannot be further derived. In this respect Kant too, as has been said, wanted to call these cognitions, and logic along with them, sciences in the preferred, indeed exclusive, sense.

On the other hand, these cognitions show us nothing further than mere relations: relations of one representation to another, form without any content. Every content they receive, every appearance that fills those forms, already contains something that is no longer completely knowable according to its whole essence, no longer entirely explicable through something else, and therefore contains something groundless. Because of this, cognition at once loses evidence and forfeits complete transparency.

But this something that withdraws from investigation is precisely the thing in itself. It is what is essentially not representation, not object of cognition, but becomes knowable only by entering into that form. The form is originally foreign to it, and it can never become wholly one with the form. It can never be reduced to mere form; and since this form is the principle of sufficient reason, it can therefore never be completely *explained down to its ground*.

Thus, even if all mathematics gives us exhaustive cognition of whatever in appearances is magnitude, position, number, in short, spatial and temporal relation; and even if all aetiology completely specifies the lawful conditions under which appearances, with all their determinations, enter into time and space; still, in all this it teaches us no more than why each determinate appearance must show itself just now here and just here now.

With the help of mathematics and aetiology we never penetrate into the inner essence of things. There always remains something at which no explanation may venture, but which explanation always presupposes: the forces of nature, the determinate mode of action of things, the quality, the character of every appearance, the groundless element that does not depend on the form of appearance, the principle of sufficient reason. This form is foreign to it in itself; yet it has entered into this form and now comes forth according to its law. But this law determines only the coming forth, not *what* comes forth; only the how, not the what, of appearance; only the form, not the content.

Mechanics, physics, and chemistry teach the rules and laws according to which the forces of impenetrability, gravity, rigidity, fluidity, cohesion, elasticity, heat, light, elective affinities, magnetism, electricity, and so on act. That is, they teach the law, the rule, that these forces observe with respect to their entry at each time into time and space. But the forces themselves remain, however one may behave about it, `qualitates occultae` (occult qualities).

For it is precisely the thing in itself which, when it appears, presents those phenomena. It is entirely different from them. In its appearance it is wholly subject to the principle of sufficient reason as the form of representation, but it itself can never be reduced to this form. Therefore it can never be explained aetiologically down to the last point, never be completely fathomed. It is completely comprehensible insofar as it has assumed that form, that is, insofar as it is appearance; but its inner essence is not explained in the least by that comprehensibility.

Hence the more necessity a cognition carries with it, the more there is in it of what cannot be thought and represented otherwise, as for example spatial relations, and therefore the clearer and more sufficient it is, the less purely objective content it has, or the less genuine reality is given in it. Conversely, the more something in it must be grasped as purely contingent, the more something presses itself on us as merely empirically given, the more properly objective and truly real there is in such cognition; but at the same time the more inexplicable there is in it, that is, the more there is that cannot be further derived from something else.

Of course, in every age an aetiology that mistakes its own goal has striven to reduce all organic life to chemism or electricity, all chemism, that is, quality, again to mechanism, or action through the shape of atoms, and mechanism again partly to the object of phoronomy, that is, time and space united for the possibility of motion, and partly to that of mere geometry, that is, position in space. This is roughly the way one rightly constructs, in purely geometrical terms, the decrease of an effect according to the square of the distance and the theory of the lever.

Geometry can finally be resolved into arithmetic, which, because of the unity of its dimension, is the most comprehensible, surveyable, and completely fathomable form of the principle of sufficient reason.

Examples of the method indicated here in general are Democritus's atoms, Descartes's vortices, and the mechanical physics of Lesage, who, toward the end of the previous century, tried to explain both chemical affinities and gravitation mechanically, through impact and pressure, as can be seen more closely from the *Lucrèce Neutonien* (*Newtonian Lucretius*). Reil's appeal to form and mixture as the cause of animal life also tends in this direction.

Finally, wholly of this kind is the crude materialism that has just now, in the middle of the nineteenth century, been warmed over again and, out of ignorance, takes itself to be original. First, with stupid denial of the vital force, it wants to explain the appearances of life from physical and chemical forces; these again from the mechanical action of matter, position, shape, and motion of dreamt-up atoms; and so to reduce all forces of nature to impact and counter-impact, which are its "thing in itself."

Accordingly, even light is supposed to be the mechanical vibration, or even undulation, of an imaginary ether postulated for this purpose, which, when it arrives, drums on the retina. Then, for example, 483 trillion drumbeats per second yield red, and 727 trillion yield violet, and so on. Color-blind people, then, would presumably be those who cannot count the drumbeats, would they not?

Such crude, mechanical, Democritean, clumsy, and truly blockheaded theories are entirely worthy of people who, fifty years after Goethe's theory of colors appeared, still believe in Newton's homogeneous lights and are not ashamed to say so. They will learn that what one overlooks in the child, Democritus, will not be forgiven in the man. They may even come to a shameful end one day; but then each will creep away and act as if he had not been involved.

We will soon return once more to this false reduction of original natural forces to one another. Here only this much: suppose it were possible in this way. Then everything would indeed be explained and fathomed, indeed in the end reduced to an arithmetic problem, which would then be the holy of holies in the temple of wisdom, to which the principle of sufficient reason had at last happily led.

But all content of appearance would have vanished, and mere form would remain. *What* appears would have been reduced to *how* it appears; and this *how* would be what can also be known `a priori` (prior to experience), and therefore would be wholly dependent on the subject, therefore present only for it, and therefore finally a mere phantom, representation and form of representation through and through. No thing in itself could be asked about.

Supposing this were possible, then the whole world would really have been derived from the subject, and what Fichte wanted, through his windy bluster, to *seem* to accomplish would in fact have been accomplished.

But it is not possible. Fantasies, sophistries, castles in the air have been produced in that manner, but no science. It has been possible, and whenever it was possible it marked a true advance, to reduce the many and manifold appearances in nature to individual original forces. Several forces and qualities that were initially taken to be different have been derived from one another (for example, magnetism from electricity), and in this way their number has been reduced.

Aetiology will have reached its goal when it has recognized and established all original forces of nature as such, and determined their modes of action, that is, the rule according to which, by the guiding thread of causality, their appearances enter into time and space and determine their place with respect to one another. But original forces will always remain; an irresolvable residue will always remain as a content of appearance that cannot be reduced to its form, and therefore cannot be explained from something else according to the principle of sufficient reason.

For in every thing in nature there is something for which no ground can ever be given, no explanation is possible, and no further cause is to be sought. It is the specific mode of its acting, that is, the mode of its existence, its essence. To be sure, for every individual effect of the thing one can point to a cause from which it follows that the thing had to act just now, just here. But no cause can ever be given for the fact that it acts at all, and acts precisely in this way.

If it has no other properties, if it is a mote in a sunbeam, then at least as gravity and impenetrability it shows that unfathomable something. This, I say, is to it what his *will* is to a human being; and like the will, according to its inner essence, it is not subject to explanation. Indeed, in itself it is identical with the will.

Certainly for every expression of the will, for every individual act of it at this time and in this place, one can point to a motive from which, on the presupposition of the person's character, the act had to follow necessarily. But no ground can ever be given for the fact that he has this character, that he wills at all, that among several motives this one and no other moves his will, or indeed that any motive moves it.

What the human being's unfathomable character is to him, presupposed in every explanation of his deeds from motives, is precisely what the essential quality of every inorganic body is to it: the mode of its acting. Its expressions are called forth by external influence, whereas the quality itself is determined by nothing outside it, and therefore is also not explicable. Its individual appearances, through which alone it becomes visible, are subject to the principle of sufficient reason; it itself is groundless. The Scholastics already recognized this essentially correctly and called it `forma substantialis` (substantial form). On this, see Suarez, *Metaphysical Disputations*, disp. XV, sect. 1.

It is an error as great as it is common to think that the most frequent, universal, and simplest appearances are the ones we understand best. They are, rather, only the ones whose sight, and our ignorance concerning them, we are most accustomed to. It is just as inexplicable to us that a stone falls to the earth as that an animal moves.

As mentioned above, people have thought that, by starting from the most universal natural forces, for example gravitation, cohesion, and impenetrability, one could explain from them the forces that act more rarely and only under combined circumstances, for example chemical quality, electricity, and magnetism, and finally, from these again, understand the organism and the life of animals, indeed human cognition and willing.

They silently resigned themselves to starting from nothing but `qualitates occultae` (occult qualities), whose illumination was entirely given up, since they intended to build over them, not undermine them. As has been said, this cannot succeed. But apart from this, such a structure would always stand in the air.

What good are explanations that finally lead back to something just as unknown as the first problem was? Does one in the end understand more about the inner essence of those universal natural forces than about the inner essence of an animal? Is not the one just as unexplored as the other? Unfathomable, because groundless; because it is the content, the what, of appearance, which can never be traced back to its form, to the how, to the principle of sufficient reason.

We, however, who are aiming here not at aetiology but at philosophy, that is, not at relative but at unconditional cognition of the essence of the world, take the opposite path. We start from what is immediately known to us, what is known to us most completely and is altogether familiar, what lies nearest to us, in order to understand what is known to us only remotely, one-sidedly, and mediately. From the most powerful, significant, and distinct appearance we want to learn to understand the more imperfect and weaker appearance.

Of all things, with the exception of my own body, only *one* side is known to me: the side of representation. Their inner essence remains closed to me and a deep mystery, even if I know all the causes from which their changes occur.

Only by comparing them with what goes on in me when, as a motive moves me, my body performs an action, with what is the inner essence of my own changes determined by external grounds, can I gain insight into the manner in which those lifeless bodies change upon causes, and so understand what their inner essence may be. Knowledge of the cause gives me only the rule of the entry of this essence's appearance into time and space, and nothing more.

I can do this because my body is the only object of which I know not merely the *one* side, that of representation, but also the second, which is called *will*. Therefore, instead of believing that I would understand my own organization, then my cognition and willing, and my movement upon motives, better if only I could reduce them to movement from causes, through electricity, chemism, and mechanism, I must, insofar as I seek philosophy and not aetiology, do the reverse.

I must first learn to understand, according to their inner essence, even the simplest and commonest movements of inorganic body, which I see occur upon causes, from my own movement upon motives. I must recognize the unfathomable forces that express themselves in all bodies of nature as identical in kind with what in me is the will, and as differing from it only in degree.

This means: the fourth class of representations established in the essay on the principle of sufficient reason must become for me the key to cognition of the inner essence of the first class. From the law of motivation I must learn to understand the law of causality according to its inner meaning.

Spinoza says (`epist. 62`; letter 62) that if the stone flying through the air after a blow had consciousness, it would think it was flying by its own will. I add only this: the stone would be right.

The blow is for the stone what the motive is for me; and what appears in the stone as cohesion, gravity, and persistence in the state it has assumed is, according to its inner essence, the same as what I recognize in myself as will, and what the stone too, if cognition were added, would recognize as will.

In that passage, Spinoza had his eye on the necessity with which the stone flies, and wanted, rightly, to transfer this necessity to the necessity of the individual act of will in a person. I, by contrast, consider the inner essence that first gives meaning and validity to every real necessity, that is, to every effect from a cause, as its presupposition. In the human being this is called character; in the stone, quality. In both it is the same; where it is immediately known, it is called *will*. In the stone it has the weakest degree of visibility, of objecthood; in the human being, the strongest.

Saint Augustine too, with correct feeling, recognized this identity between the striving of all things and our willing, and I cannot refrain from setting down his naive expression of the matter:

> `Si pecora essemus, carnalem vitam et quod secundum sensum ejusdem est amaremus, idque esset sufficiens bonum nostrum, et secundum hoc si esset nobis bene, nihil aliud quaereremus. Item, si arbores essemus, nihil quidem sentientes motu amare possemus: verumtamen id quasi appetere videremur, quo feracius essemus, uberiusque fructuosae. Si essemus lapides, aut fluctus, aut ventus, aut flamma, vel quid ejusmodi, sine ullo quidem sensu atque vita, non tamen nobis deesset quasi quidam nostrorum locorum atque ordinis appetitus. Nam velut amores corporum momenta sunt ponderum, sive deorsum gravitate, sive sursum levitate nitantur: ita enim corpus pondere, sicut animus amore fertur quocunque fertur (de civ. Dei, XI, 28).`
>
> Translation: If we were beasts, we would love bodily life and whatever accords with its sense, and that would be our sufficient good; and if things were well with us in this respect, we would seek nothing else. Likewise, if we were trees, we could love nothing by a movement of sensation; nevertheless we would seem, as it were, to desire that by which we would be more fertile and more abundantly fruitful. If we were stones, or waves, or wind, or flame, or anything of that kind, without any sensation or life, we still would not lack, as it were, a certain appetite for our own places and order. For the movements of weights are like the loves of bodies, whether they strive downward by heaviness or upward by lightness; thus the body is carried by weight, just as the soul is carried by love, wherever it is carried (*The City of God*, XI, 28).

It also deserves to be noted that Euler already saw that the essence of gravitation must ultimately be traced back to an "inclination and desire" peculiar to bodies, that is, to will (in the sixty-eighth letter to the princess). Indeed, this is precisely what makes him averse to the concept of gravitation as it stands in Newton. He is inclined to try a modification of it in accordance with the earlier Cartesian theory, and so to derive gravitation from the impact of an ether upon bodies, as something that would be "more reasonable and more suitable to people who love clear and comprehensible principles."

He wants to see attraction banished from physics as a `qualitas occulta` (occult quality). This is simply in keeping with the dead view of nature that prevailed in Euler's time as the correlate of the immaterial soul. Yet it is noteworthy with respect to the fundamental truth I have set forth. This fine mind already saw that truth shining through from a distance, but hurried to turn back in time; and now, in his anxiety at seeing all the fundamental views of his time endangered, he even sought protection in the old absurdity that had already been disposed of.
