# §. 3.

The chief distinction among all our representations is that between the intuitive and the abstract. The latter make up only *one* class of representations: concepts. On earth, concepts are the possession of the human being alone, and the capacity for them, which distinguishes him from all animals, has always been called *reason*. [^1]

We shall later consider these abstract representations by themselves. For now, however, we shall speak exclusively of *intuitive representation*. This comprises the whole visible world, or the whole of experience, together with the conditions of its possibility.

As already said, it is one of Kant's very important discoveries that precisely these conditions, these forms of experience, that is, what is most universal in its perception, what belongs in the same way to all its appearances, time and space, can also be not only thought `in abstracto` (in the abstract), by themselves and separated from their content, but also immediately intuited. This intuition is not, say, a phantasm borrowed from experience by repetition. On the contrary, it is so independent of experience that experience must instead be thought as dependent on it; for the properties of space and time, as intuition cognizes them `a priori` (prior to experience), hold as laws for all possible experience, according to which experience everywhere must turn out.

For this reason, in my essay on the principle of sufficient reason, I treated time and space, insofar as they are intuited purely and without content, as a special class of representations existing by itself. Now, important as this character of the universal forms of intuition discovered by Kant is, namely that they can be intuited by themselves and independently of experience, and can be known in accordance with their entire lawfulness, on which mathematics with its infallibility rests, another property of theirs is no less worthy of attention.

The principle of sufficient reason, which determines experience as the law of causality and motivation, and thought as the law of the grounding of judgments, appears here in a quite distinctive form. I have given this form the name *ground of being*. In time, it is the succession of its moments; in space, it is the position of its parts, which determine one another reciprocally into infinity.

Anyone who has understood, from the introductory essay, the complete identity of the content of the principle of sufficient reason throughout all the difference of its forms will also be convinced how important it is, for insight into its innermost essence, to cognize the simplest of its forms precisely as such. We have recognized this simplest form as *time*.

In time, each moment exists only insofar as it has annihilated the preceding one, its father, in order to be annihilated just as quickly in turn. Past and future, apart from the consequences of their content, are as empty as any dream; the present is only the extensionless and durationless boundary between the two.

In just the same way, we shall recognize the same emptiness in all the other forms of the principle of sufficient reason. We shall see that, like time, so too space; and, like space, so too everything that exists in space and time at once, therefore everything that proceeds from causes or motives, has only a relative existence. It exists only through and for something else like itself, that is, something that again exists in just the same way.

The essence of this view is old. Heraclitus lamented in it the eternal flux of things. Plato downgraded its object as what is always becoming but never being. Spinoza called it mere accidents of the one substance that alone is and persists. Kant opposed what is known in this way, as mere appearance, to the thing in itself. Finally, the ancient wisdom of the Indians says:

> It is *Maya*, the veil of illusion, which covers the eyes of mortals and lets them see a world of which one can say neither that it is nor that it is not; for it is like a dream, like the sunlight on sand which the wanderer from a distance takes for water, or like a rope thrown down which he takes for a snake.

These similes are repeated in countless passages of the Vedas and Puranas.

But what all these meant, and what they speak of, is nothing other than what we are now considering: the world as representation, subject to the principle of sufficient reason.

[^1]: Kant alone confused this concept of reason. On this point I refer to the appendix, as well as to my *Two Fundamental Problems of Ethics*: "On the Basis of Morality," §. 6, pp. 148-154 of the first edition. (Second edition, pp. 146-151.)
