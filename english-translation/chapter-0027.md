# §. 25.

We know that *multiplicity* in general is necessarily conditioned by time and space, and is thinkable only in them. In this respect we call time and space the `principium individuationis` (principle of individuation). But we have recognized time and space as forms of the principle of sufficient reason, in which principle all our cognition `a priori` (prior to experience) is expressed. As was explained above, however, precisely as such this principle belongs only to the knowability of things, not to the things themselves. That is, it is only our form of cognition, not a property of the thing in itself.

As thing in itself, it is free from every form of cognition, even from the most universal form, that of being object for a subject; in other words, it is something wholly different from representation.

Now if this thing in itself, as I believe I have sufficiently demonstrated and made evident, is *the will*, then as such, and considered apart from its appearance, it lies outside time and space. It therefore knows no multiplicity, and consequently is *one*. Yet, as has already been said, it is not one as an individual is one, nor as a concept is one, but as something to which the condition of the possibility of multiplicity, the `principium individuationis`, is foreign.

The multiplicity of things in space and time, all of which are its *objecthood*, therefore does not affect the will, and it remains indivisible despite that multiplicity.

It is not, for example, that a smaller part of the will is in the stone and a larger part in the human being. The relation of part and whole belongs exclusively to space and no longer has any meaning once one has left behind this form of intuition. Rather, more and less apply only to appearance, that is, to visibility, to objectivation. There is a higher degree of this in the plant than in the stone, and a higher degree in the animal than in the plant. Indeed, the will's emergence into visibility, its objectivation, has infinitely many gradations, as there are gradations between the weakest twilight and the brightest sunlight, between the strongest tone and the faintest echo.

Later we will return to the consideration of these grades of visibility, which belong to the will's objectivation, to the image of its essence. But even less than the gradations of its objectivation directly affect the will itself does the multiplicity of appearances at these different levels affect it, that is, the number of individuals of each form or of the individual expressions of each force. For this multiplicity is immediately conditioned by time and space, into which the will itself never enters.

The will reveals itself just as wholly and just as much in *one* oak as in millions. Their number, their multiplication in space and time, has no significance whatever with respect to the will, but only with respect to the multiplicity of individuals that know in space and time and are themselves multiplied and scattered there. Yet this multiplicity, again, concerns only the will's appearance, not the will itself.

Thus one could even maintain that if, `per impossibile` (assuming the impossible), a single being, even the least, were entirely annihilated, the whole world would have to perish with it. Speaking from this feeling, the great mystic Angelus Silesius says:

> I know that without me God cannot live one instant;
> If I come to nothing, he must of necessity give up the spirit.

People have tried in various ways to bring the immeasurable greatness of the world's structure closer to everyone's capacity to grasp it, and then have taken occasion from this for edifying reflections, for example on the relative smallness of the earth, and still more of the human being; then again, by contrast, on the greatness of the spirit in this very small human being, who can discover, comprehend, indeed measure that vastness of the world, and so on. All very well.

Meanwhile, when I consider the immeasurability of the world, the most important thing to me is this: that the essence in itself whose appearance the world is, whatever it may be, cannot have drawn apart and divided its true self in this way throughout boundless space. Rather, this infinite extension belongs solely to its appearance. It itself, by contrast, is present whole and undivided in every thing of nature, in every living being.

For this reason one loses nothing by stopping with any single thing. True wisdom is not to be gained by measuring out the boundless world, or, what would be still more appropriate, by personally flying through endless space. It is gained rather by investigating any single thing completely, by trying to learn to know and understand its true and proper essence perfectly.

Accordingly, the following point, which will already have forced itself upon every student of Plato here, will be the object of a detailed consideration in the next book: those different grades of the objectivation of the will, which are expressed in countless individuals and stand as their unattained models, or as the eternal forms of things, do not themselves enter into time and space, the medium of individuals. Instead they stand fixed, subject to no change, always being, never having become; while the individuals arise and pass away, always becoming and never being.

These *grades of the objectivation of the will*, I say, are nothing other than *Plato's Ideas*. I mention this here provisionally so that from now on I can use the word *Idea* in this sense. In my usage it is therefore always to be understood in its genuine and original meaning, the one Plato gave it. One should not think at all here of those abstract productions of scholastically dogmatizing reason for whose designation Kant misused this word, already taken possession of and most appropriately used by Plato, both unsuitably and unlawfully.

By *Idea*, then, I understand every determinate and fixed *grade of the objectivation of the will*, insofar as the will is thing in itself and therefore foreign to multiplicity. These grades do indeed relate to individual things as their eternal forms, or their models.
