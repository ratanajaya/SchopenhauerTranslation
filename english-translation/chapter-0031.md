# §. 29.

Here I conclude the second main part of my presentation, in the hope that, so far as is possible in the very first communication of an unprecedented thought, which therefore cannot be entirely free of the traces of the individuality in which it first arose, I have succeeded in communicating the clear certainty that this world in which we live and are is, in its whole essence, through and through *will*, and at the same time through and through *representation*.

This representation, already as such, presupposes a form, namely object and subject, and is therefore relative. If we ask what remains after the cancellation of this form and of all the forms subordinate to it, which the principle of sufficient reason expresses, then this, as something different from representation `toto genere` (in kind altogether), can be nothing other than *will*, which accordingly is the true *thing in itself*.

Everyone finds himself as this will, in which the inner essence of the world consists, just as he also finds himself as the knowing subject whose representation the whole world is, which in this respect has existence only in relation to his consciousness as its necessary bearer. In this double respect, then, everyone is the whole world itself, the microcosm, and finds both sides of it wholly and completely in himself.

What he thus recognizes as his own essence also exhausts the essence of the whole world, the macrocosm. The world too, like himself, is therefore through and through will and through and through representation, and nothing more remains. Thus we see here the philosophy of Thales, which considered the macrocosm, and that of Socrates, which considered the microcosm, coincide, since the object of both proves to be the same.

The whole cognition communicated in the first two books, however, will gain greater completeness, and thereby also greater certainty, through the two books that still follow. In them, I hope, many a question that may have arisen, clearly or obscurely, in the course of our consideration so far will also find its sufficient answer.

Meanwhile, *one* such question may still be discussed separately, since it can really arise only so long as one has not yet fully penetrated the meaning of the preceding account, and precisely for that reason it can serve to clarify it. The question is this: every will is will toward something; it has an object, a goal of its willing. What, then, does that will ultimately want, or toward what does it strive, if it has been presented to us as the essence in itself of the world?

This question, like so many others, rests on confusing the thing in itself with appearance. The principle of sufficient reason, whose forms include the law of motivation, extends only to appearance, not to the thing in itself. Everywhere one can give a ground only for appearances as such, for individual things, never for the will itself, nor for the Idea in which it objectifies itself adequately.

Thus for every individual movement, or in general every change in nature, one must seek a cause, that is, a state that necessarily brought it about. But one must never seek a cause for the natural force itself that reveals itself in that appearance and in countless similar appearances. It is therefore sheer lack of understanding, arising from a lack of reflective awareness, when someone asks for a cause of gravity, electricity, and so on.

Only if one had shown that gravity or electricity were not original and peculiar natural forces, but only modes of appearance of a more universal natural force already known, could one ask for the cause that makes this natural force here produce the appearance of gravity or electricity. All this was explained at length above.

In just the same way, every individual act of will of a knowing individual, who himself is only appearance of the will as thing in itself, necessarily has a motive, without which that act would never occur. But just as the material cause contains only the determination that, at this time, in this place, and in this matter, an expression of this or that natural force must occur, so too the motive determines only the act of will of a knowing being at this time, in this place, under these circumstances, as something wholly individual. It by no means determines that this being wills at all, or that it wills in this way.

This is the expression of its intelligible character, which, as the will itself, the thing in itself, is groundless, lying outside the domain of the principle of sufficient reason. Thus every human being constantly has ends and motives by which he guides his action, and can always give an account of his individual deeds. But if one asked him why he wills at all, or why he wills to exist at all, he would have no answer. Rather, the question would seem absurd to him; and in this precisely would be expressed the consciousness that he himself is nothing but will, whose willing in general is therefore self-evident, and needs closer determination by motives only in its individual acts, for each point in time.

In fact, the absence of every goal and every limit belongs to the essence of the will in itself, which is an endless striving. This was already touched on above when centrifugal force was mentioned; and it reveals itself most simply at the very lowest grade of the objecthood of the will, namely in gravity, whose constant striving lies before our eyes despite the obvious impossibility of any final goal.

For even if, according to gravity's will, all existing matter were united into one lump, gravity within that lump, striving toward the center, would still continue to struggle with impenetrability as rigidity or elasticity. The striving of matter can therefore always be checked, but never fulfilled or satisfied.

This is exactly how it stands with all striving of all appearances of will. Every attained goal is again the beginning of a new course, and so on to infinity. The plant raises its appearance from seed through stem and leaf to blossom and fruit, which is again only the beginning of a new seed, a new individual that runs through the old course once more, and so through endless time.

The life-course of the animal is the same. Procreation is its summit; after this has been reached, the life of the first individual declines quickly or slowly, while a new one guarantees to nature the preservation of the species and repeats the same appearance. Indeed, the constant renewal of the matter of every organism is also to be regarded as the mere appearance of this continual drive and change. Physiologists are now ceasing to regard it as the necessary replacement of material used up in movement, since the possible wear of the machine can by no means be equivalent to the constant influx through nutrition. Eternal becoming, endless flow, belongs to the revelation of the essence of the will.

The same thing finally shows itself in human strivings and wishes, which always delude us into thinking that their fulfillment is the final goal of willing. But as soon as they are attained, they no longer look the same, and therefore are soon forgotten, become obsolete, and are really always laid aside as vanished illusions, even if this is not admitted.

Happy enough if something still remained to wish for and to strive after, so that the play of the constant transition from wish to satisfaction and from satisfaction to a new wish can be maintained. Its rapid movement is called happiness; its slow movement is called suffering. Otherwise it falls into that stagnation which shows itself as terrible, life-stiffening boredom, a dull longing without a determinate object, a deadening `languor` (languor).

According to all this, where cognition illuminates the will, the will always knows what it wants now and here, but never what it wants in general. Every individual act has an end; willing as a whole has none. In the same way, every individual natural appearance is determined by a sufficient cause for its entrance at this place and at this time, but the force manifesting itself in it has no cause at all, since it is the grade of appearance of the thing in itself, of the groundless will.

The will's only self-knowledge as a whole, however, is representation as a whole, the entire intuitive world. This is its objecthood, its revelation, its mirror. What it says in this capacity will be the object of our further consideration.[^1]

[^1]: On this, see chapter 23 of the second volume.
