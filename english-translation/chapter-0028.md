# §. 26.

The most universal forces of nature present themselves as the lowest grade of the objectivation of the will. Some of them appear in all matter without exception, such as gravity and impenetrability. Others have divided among themselves the matter that exists in general, so that some rule over this matter and others over that, thereby making matter specifically different: rigidity, fluidity, elasticity, electricity, magnetism, chemical properties, and qualities of every kind.

In themselves they are immediate appearances of the will, just as much as human action is. As such they are groundless, like the character of a human being. Only their individual appearances are subject to the principle of sufficient reason, as human actions are. The forces themselves, however, can never be called either effect or cause. Rather, they are the prior and presupposed conditions of all causes and effects, through which their own essence unfolds and reveals itself.

It is therefore unintelligent to ask for a cause of gravity or electricity. These are original forces. Their expressions indeed proceed according to cause and effect, so that each individual appearance of them has a cause, which is itself again just such an individual appearance, and which determines that here this force had to express itself, had to emerge in time and space. But the force itself is by no means the effect of a cause, nor is it the cause of an effect.

For this reason it is also false to say: "Gravity is the cause of the stone's falling." Rather, the nearness of the earth is the cause here, insofar as the earth draws the stone. Take the earth away, and the stone will not fall, although gravity remains.

The force itself lies entirely outside the chain of causes and effects, which presupposes time, since it has meaning only with reference to time; the force, however, also lies outside time. An individual change always has another equally individual change as its cause, not the force whose expression it is. For precisely what gives a cause its efficacy, however many countless times it may occur, is a natural force. As such it is groundless, that is, it lies entirely outside the chain of causes and outside the domain of the principle of sufficient reason in general. Philosophically, it is known as immediate objecthood of the will, which is the in-itself of all nature; in aetiology, here physics, it is demonstrated as original force, that is, as `qualitas occulta` (occult quality).

At the higher grades of the objecthood of the will we see individuality emerge significantly, especially in the human being, as the great difference of individual characters, that is, as complete personality, already outwardly expressed by a strongly marked individual physiognomy, which includes the whole embodiment.

No animal has individuality in anything like this degree. Only the higher animals have a trace of it, over which, however, the species-character still completely predominates; for this very reason they also have little individual physiognomy. The farther downward one goes, the more every trace of individual character disappears into the general character of the species, whose physiognomy alone remains.

One knows the physiological character of the species, and from this knows exactly what is to be expected from the individual. By contrast, in the human species each individual wants to be studied and fathomed for himself. Because the possibility of dissimulation first entered with reason, this is extremely difficult if one wishes to determine a person's conduct in advance with any certainty.

It is probably connected with this difference between the human species and all others that the furrows and convolutions of the brain, which are still entirely absent in birds and still very weak in rodents, are, even in the higher animals, far more symmetrical on both sides and more constant in each individual than they are in human beings.[^1]

Furthermore, one should regard the following as a phenomenon of that genuine individual character which distinguishes human beings from all animals: among animals, the sexual drive seeks its satisfaction without noticeable selection; whereas in human beings this selection, in a way independent of all reflection and instinctive, is carried so far that it rises to violent passion.

Thus, while every human being is to be regarded as a specially determined and characterized appearance of the will, indeed in a certain sense even as an Idea of his own, among animals this individual character is on the whole absent, since only the species still has a distinctive significance. The farther they stand from human beings, the more its trace disappears. Plants, finally, have no other individuality than what can be fully explained from favorable or unfavorable external influences of soil and climate and from other contingencies.

In the inorganic realm of nature, all individuality finally disappears altogether. Only the crystal can still be regarded, in a certain sense, as an individual. It is a unity of striving in determinate directions, seized by solidification, which makes the trace of this striving permanent. At the same time it is an aggregate from its kernel-form, bound into unity by one Idea.

In just the same way, the tree is an aggregate from the individual sprouting fiber, which presents and repeats itself in every rib of the leaf, every leaf, and every branch, and in a certain sense lets each of these be regarded as a plant of its own that feeds parasitically on the larger one. Thus the tree, like the crystal, is a systematic aggregate of small plants, although only the whole is the completed presentation of an indivisible Idea, that is, of this determinate grade of the objectivation of the will.

But the individuals of the same species of crystals can have no difference other than that brought about by external contingencies. One can even make each species shoot into large or small crystals at will. The individual as such, that is, with traces of an individual character, is no longer found at all in inorganic nature.

All its appearances are expressions of universal natural forces, that is, of those grades of the objectivation of the will which do not objectify themselves, as they do in organic nature, through the mediation of different individualities that partially express the whole of the Idea. Rather, they present themselves only in the species, and present the species wholly and without deviation in every single appearance.

Since time, space, multiplicity, and being conditioned by cause do not belong to the will, nor to the Idea, the grade of the objectivation of the will, but only to the individual appearances of the Idea, the natural force as such, for example gravity or electricity, must present itself in exactly the same way in all the millions of its appearances. Only external circumstances can modify the appearance.

This unity of its essence in all its appearances, this unchanging constancy of its occurrence as soon as the conditions for it are present along the guiding thread of causality, is called a *law of nature*. Once such a law is known through experience, the appearance of the natural force whose character is expressed and deposited in it can be exactly predicted and calculated.

But it is precisely this lawfulness of the appearances of the lower grades of the objectivation of the will that gives them such a different look from the appearances of the same will at the higher, that is, clearer grades of its objectivation, in animals, human beings, and their actions. There, the stronger or weaker emergence of individual character, and the being-moved by motives that, because they lie in cognition, often remain hidden from the observer, have until now made people entirely fail to recognize the identical inner essence of both kinds of appearance.

The infallibility of natural laws has something astonishing about it, indeed at times almost something uncanny, if one starts from cognition of the individual and not from cognition of the Idea. One might wonder that nature never forgets its laws even once. For example, if it accords with a law of nature that when certain substances meet under determinate conditions, chemical combination, development of gas, or combustion takes place, then whenever the conditions occur, whether through our arrangement or entirely by chance, the determinate appearance enters at once and without delay, today just as much as a thousand years ago. Where chance is involved, the punctuality is all the more surprising because it was unexpected.

We feel this marvelousness most vividly in rare appearances that occur only under very combined circumstances, but that under these circumstances are announced to us in advance. For example: when certain metals, alternating with one another and with an acidified fluid, are brought into contact, silver leaf placed between the ends of this chain must suddenly burst into green flames; or, under certain conditions, hard diamond must turn into carbonic acid.

It is the spectral omnipresence of natural forces that then surprises us. What no longer occurs to us in everyday appearances we notice here: that the connection between cause and effect is in truth as mysterious as the imagined connection between a magic formula and the spirit that necessarily appears when summoned by it.

By contrast, once we have penetrated into the philosophical cognition that a natural force is a determinate grade of the objectivation of the will, that is, of what we too recognize as our innermost essence; that this will in itself, as distinct from its appearance and the forms of appearance, lies outside time and space; and that therefore the multiplicity conditioned by time and space belongs neither to the will nor immediately to the grade of its objectivation, that is, to the Idea, but only to the appearances of the Idea; and that the law of causality has meaning only in relation to time and space, insofar as in them it determines the place of the multiplied appearances of the different Ideas in which the will manifests itself, regulating the order in which they must enter: once, I say, the inner meaning of Kant's great doctrine has dawned on us in this cognition, namely that space, time, and causality do not belong to the thing in itself, but only to appearance, that they are only forms of our cognition and not properties of the thing in itself, then we will see that the astonishment over the lawfulness and punctuality of a natural force's action, over the complete sameness of all its millions of appearances, over the infallibility of their occurrence, is in fact comparable to the astonishment of a child, or of a savage, who for the first time looks at a flower through a many-faceted glass, marvels at the complete sameness of the countless flowers he sees, and counts the petals of each one individually.

Every universal original natural force, then, is in its inner essence nothing other than the objectivation of the will at a lower grade. We call each such grade an eternal *Idea*, in Plato's sense. The *law of nature*, however, is the relation of the Idea to the form of its appearance.

This form is time, space, and causality, which have a necessary and inseparable connection and relation to one another. Through time and space, the Idea multiplies itself into countless appearances; but the order according to which these enter into those forms of multiplicity is firmly determined by the law of causality. This law is, as it were, the norm of the boundary-points of those appearances of different Ideas, according to which space, time, and matter are distributed among them.

This norm therefore necessarily relates to the identity of all existing matter, which is the common substrate of all those different appearances. If these appearances were not all referred to that common matter, in whose possession they must share, there would be no need for such a law to determine their claims. They could all at once and alongside one another fill infinite space throughout an infinite time. Only because all those appearances of the eternal Ideas are referred to one and the same matter did there have to be a rule of their entrance and exit; otherwise none would make room for another.

In this way the law of causality is essentially connected with the law of the persistence of substance. Both receive meaning only from one another. Space and time, again, stand in the same relation to them. For time is the mere possibility of opposed determinations in the same matter; space is the mere possibility that the same matter persists under all opposed determinations.

For this reason, in the previous book we explained matter as the union of time and space. This union shows itself as the change of accidents while substance persists, the general possibility of which is precisely causality, or becoming. We therefore also said that matter is causality through and through. We explained the understanding as the subjective correlate of causality, and said that matter, and thus the whole world as representation, exists only for the understanding, that the understanding is its condition, its bearer, as its necessary correlate.

All this is only a passing reminder of what was set out in the first book. Attention to the inner agreement of the two books is required for their complete understanding, since what is inseparably united in the actual world as its two sides, will and representation, has been torn apart in these two books so that each may be known more clearly in isolation.

It may perhaps not be superfluous to make still clearer by an example how the law of causality has meaning only in relation to time and space and to matter, which consists in the union of the two. The law determines the boundaries according to which the appearances of natural forces divide possession of matter among themselves. The original natural forces themselves, however, as immediate objectivations of the will, which as thing in itself is not subject to the principle of sufficient reason, lie outside those forms within which alone every aetiological explanation has validity and meaning, and for just this reason such explanation can never lead to the inner essence of nature.

For this purpose, let us imagine a machine constructed according to the laws of mechanics. Iron weights give the beginning of motion through their gravity; copper wheels resist through their rigidity, and by virtue of their impenetrability push and lift one another and the levers, and so on. Here gravity, rigidity, and impenetrability are original, unexplained forces. Mechanics gives only the conditions under which, and the way in which, they express themselves, emerge, and govern determinate matter, time, and place.

Now a strong magnet might act on the iron of the weights and overpower gravity. The motion of the machine stops, and the matter is at once the theater of an entirely different natural force, of which the aetiological explanation again gives nothing more than the conditions of its entrance: magnetism.

Or the copper disks of that machine are now placed on zinc plates, and acidified fluid is led between them. Immediately the same matter of the machine has fallen prey to another original force, galvanism, which now rules it according to its laws and reveals itself in it through its appearances. Aetiology can state of these appearances no more than the circumstances under which, and the laws according to which, they show themselves.

Now let us increase the temperature and introduce pure oxygen: the whole machine burns. That is, once again an entirely different natural force, chemism, has at this time and in this place an unavoidable claim upon that matter, and reveals itself in it as Idea, as determinate grade of the objectivation of the will.

Let the metallic calx that has thereby arisen combine with an acid: a salt arises, crystals shoot forth. They are the appearance of another Idea, which itself is again wholly unfathomable, while the entrance of its appearance depended on those conditions that aetiology knows how to state. The crystals weather, mix with other substances, and a vegetation rises from them: a new appearance of will.

And so one could follow the same persisting matter to infinity, and watch how now this, now that natural force gains a right to it and infallibly takes possession of it in order to come forth and reveal its essence. The determination of this right, the point in time and space where it becomes valid, is given by the law of causality. But the explanation grounded on this law goes only this far.

The force itself is appearance of the will, and as such is not subject to the forms of the principle of sufficient reason, that is, it is groundless. It lies outside all time, is omnipresent, and seems, as it were, constantly to await the entrance of the circumstances under which it can come forth and take possession of a determinate matter, displacing the forces that have ruled it until then.

All time exists only for the force's appearance, and has no meaning for the force itself. For thousands of years chemical forces slumber in matter, until contact with reagents sets them free; then they appear. But time exists only for this appearance, not for the forces themselves. For thousands of years galvanism slumbers in copper and zinc, and they lie quietly beside the silver that must burst into flames as soon as all three touch under the required conditions.

Even in the organic realm we see a dry seed preserve for three thousand years the slumbering force which, when favorable circumstances finally arrive, rises up as a plant.[^2]

If through this consideration the distinction has become clear to us between the natural force and all its appearances; if we have seen that the former is the will itself at this determinate grade of its objectivation, while multiplicity belongs only to the appearances through time and space, and the law of causality is nothing other than the determination of the place in time and space for the individual appearances: then we will also recognize the complete truth and deep meaning of Malebranche's doctrine of occasional causes, `causes occasionelles` (occasional causes).

It is very worthwhile to compare this doctrine, as Malebranche presents it in the *Search After Truth*, especially in the third chapter of the second part of the sixth book and in the *Elucidations* appended to that chapter, with my present account, and to observe the most complete agreement of the two doctrines despite so great a difference in the course of thought. Indeed, I must admire how Malebranche, entirely caught within the positive dogmas that his age irresistibly imposed upon him, nevertheless, in such bonds and under such a burden, so happily and so rightly hit upon the truth, and knew how to unite it with those very dogmas, at least with their language.

For the power of truth is incredibly great and of unspeakable endurance. We find its frequent traces again in all the dogmas of different times and lands, even the most bizarre, indeed the most absurd; often, to be sure, in strange company and in curious mixture, but still recognizable. It is then like a plant that germinates under a heap of large stones, but nevertheless climbs up toward the light, working its way through with many detours and bends, disfigured, pale, stunted; yet still toward the light.

Malebranche is indeed right: every natural cause is only an occasional cause. It gives only the occasion, the prompt, for the appearance of that one and indivisible will which is the in-itself of all things, and whose gradual objectivation is this whole visible world. Only the emergence, the becoming-visible at this place and at this time, is brought about by the cause and is dependent on it in this respect; not the whole of the appearance, not its inner essence. This is the will itself, to which the principle of sufficient reason has no application, and which is therefore groundless.

No thing in the world has a cause of its existence absolutely and in general. It has only a cause from which it is here and now. Why a stone now shows gravity, now rigidity, now electricity, now chemical properties, depends on causes, on external influences, and can be explained from these. But those properties themselves, and therefore its whole essence, which consists in them and consequently expresses itself in all the ways indicated, the fact that it is in general such as it is, and that it exists at all, has no ground. It is the becoming-visible of the groundless will.

Thus every cause is an occasional cause. We found this to be so in nature without cognition. But it is just the same where it is no longer causes and stimuli but motives that determine the point of entry of appearances, that is, in the action of animals and human beings.

For here as there it is one and the same will that appears, very different in the grades of its manifestation, multiplied in the appearances of those grades and, with respect to these appearances, subject to the principle of sufficient reason, while in itself it is free from all this.

Motives do not determine the character of the human being, but only the appearance of this character, that is, the deeds; the outer shape of the course of his life, not its inner meaning and content. These proceed from the character, which is the immediate appearance of the will and is therefore groundless.

Why one person is malicious and another good does not depend on motives and external influence, perhaps on teachings and sermons, and in this sense is absolutely inexplicable. But whether a malicious person shows his malice in petty injustices, cowardly intrigues, and low villainies that he practices in the narrow circle of his surroundings, or whether, as a conqueror, he oppresses nations, plunges a world into misery, and spills the blood of millions: this is the outer form of his appearance, its inessential element, and depends on the circumstances in which fate placed him, on surroundings, external influences, and motives. But his decision can never be explained from these motives; it proceeds from the will, of which this human being is the appearance. We will speak of this in the fourth book.

The way in which character unfolds its qualities is entirely comparable to the way in which every body of nature without cognition shows its qualities. Water remains water, with the properties inherent in it. Whether as a still lake it mirrors its banks, or foams as it plunges over rocks, or, artificially prompted, sprays upward in a long jet: this depends on external causes. One is as natural to it as the other. But according to the circumstances it will show the one or the other, equally ready for all, yet in every case true to its character and always revealing only this character. So too every human character will reveal itself under all circumstances; but the appearances that proceed from it will be according to the circumstances.
