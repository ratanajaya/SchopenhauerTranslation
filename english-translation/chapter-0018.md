# §. 16.

After this whole consideration of reason as a special faculty of cognition belonging to the human being alone, and of the achievements and phenomena peculiar to human nature that it brings about, it would now remain for me to speak of reason insofar as it guides human actions, and therefore can be called *practical* in this respect. But most of what should be mentioned here has found its place elsewhere, namely in the appendix to this work, where the existence of what Kant called practical reason had to be disputed. He presents it, very conveniently, as the immediate source of all virtue and as the seat of an absolute, that is, heaven-sent, *ought*. Later I gave a detailed and thorough refutation of this Kantian principle of morality in the *Two Fundamental Problems of Ethics*.

I therefore have only a little more to say here about the actual influence of reason, in the true sense of the word, on action. Already at the beginning of our consideration of reason we noted in general how greatly human doing and conduct differ from those of the animal, and that this difference is to be regarded solely as a consequence of the presence of abstract concepts in consciousness.

The influence of these concepts on our whole existence is so pervasive and significant that it places us in relation to animals somewhat as seeing animals stand to eyeless animals, such as certain larvae, worms, and zoophytes. The latter cognize by touch alone what is immediately present to them in space and in contact with them; the seeing animals, by contrast, cognize a wide circle of near and far.

In the same way, the absence of reason restricts animals to the intuitive representations immediately present to them in time, that is, to real objects. We, by contrast, by virtue of cognition `in abstracto` (in the abstract), encompass, alongside the narrow actual present, the whole past and future, together with the wide realm of possibility. We survey life freely on all sides, far beyond the present and actuality.

What the eye is in space and for sensible cognition, reason is, in a certain way, in time and for inner cognition. Yet just as the visibility of objects has value and meaning only because it announces their tangibility, the whole value of abstract cognition always lies in its relation to intuitive cognition.

For this reason the natural human being always places far more value on what is immediately and intuitively cognized than on abstract concepts, on what is merely thought. He prefers empirical cognition to logical cognition. The opposite disposition belongs to those who live more in words than in deeds, who have looked more into paper and books than into the actual world, and who in their greatest degeneration become pedants and people of the letter.

From this alone it becomes intelligible how Leibniz, Wolff, and all their followers could go so far astray, following Duns Scotus, as to declare intuitive cognition to be only confused abstract cognition. To Spinoza's honor I must mention that his more correct sense, conversely, explained all common concepts as arising from the confusion of what is intuitively cognized (`Ethics`, II, proposition 40, scholium 1).

From that inverted disposition it also arose that in mathematics people rejected its peculiar evidence in order to admit only logical evidence; that every non-abstract cognition was generally included under the broad name feeling and held in low esteem; and finally that Kantian ethics declared the good will that responds purely and immediately to cognition of the circumstances, and guides one to right-doing and beneficence, to be mere feeling and impulse, without worth or merit, and wanted to recognize moral worth only in action proceeding from abstract maxims.

The all-sided overview of life as a whole, which the human being has in advance of the animal through reason, may also be compared to a geometrical, colorless, abstract, reduced ground plan of his path through life. In this respect he stands to the animal as the navigator, who by means of chart, compass, and quadrant knows his course and his position on the sea at every moment, stands to the ignorant crew that sees only waves and sky.

It is therefore worth considering, indeed wonderful, how the human being, alongside his life `in concreto` (in the concrete), always also leads a second life `in abstracto`. In the first, he is given over to all the storms of actuality and to the influence of the present; he must strive, suffer, and die like the animal. But his life in the abstract, as it stands before rational reflection, is the quiet reflection of the first life and of the world in which he lives; it is the reduced ground plan just mentioned.

Here, in the region of calm deliberation, what wholly possesses him and violently moves him there appears cold, colorless, and for the moment alien. Here he is mere spectator and observer. In this withdrawal into reflection he resembles an actor who has played his scene and, until he must appear again, takes his place among the spectators; from there he calmly watches whatever may happen, even if it is the preparation for his death in the play, and then goes back and acts and suffers as he must.

From this double life arises that human composure which differs so greatly from animal thoughtlessness. After prior deliberation, a resolution once taken, or a necessity recognized, a person can cold-bloodedly let the most important, often most terrible things happen to him, or can carry them out: suicide, execution, duel, life-threatening ventures of every kind, and in general things against which his whole animal nature rebels.

Then one sees the extent to which reason becomes master over animal nature, and one calls out to the strong person:

> `σιδηϱειον νυ τοι ἡτοϱ!`
> `ferreum certe tibi cor!`
> Translation: Surely you have an iron heart! (`Iliad`, 24.521)

Here one can truly say that reason expresses itself *practically*. Wherever action is guided by reason, wherever the motives are abstract concepts, wherever the determining factor is not intuitive, individual representations or the impression of the moment, which guides the animal, there *practical reason* shows itself.

But all this is entirely different from, and independent of, the ethical worth of action. Rational action and virtuous action are two entirely different things. Reason can be united with great wickedness just as well as with great goodness, and by joining either one first gives it great efficacy. It is equally ready and serviceable for the methodical and consistent execution of a noble or a bad resolve, of a prudent or an unreasonable maxim. This follows from its feminine, receptive, preservative, and not self-generating nature.

I have set all this out in detail and illustrated it with examples in the appendix. What is said there would have its proper place here; but because of the polemic against Kant's alleged practical reason, it had to be moved there, and so I refer back to it from here.

The most complete development of *practical reason*, in the true and genuine sense of the word, the highest summit to which the human being can rise through the mere use of his reason, and on which his difference from the animal appears most clearly, is represented as an ideal in the *Stoic sage*.

For Stoic ethics is originally and essentially not a doctrine of virtue at all, but merely instruction for rational living, whose aim and purpose is happiness through peace of mind. Virtuous conduct enters into it, so to speak, only `per accidens` (accidentally), as a means, not as an end. Therefore Stoic ethics, in its whole essence and point of view, is fundamentally different from ethical systems that press directly toward virtue, such as the teachings of the Vedas, Plato, Christianity, and Kant.

The aim of Stoic ethics is happiness: `virtutes omnes finem habere beatitudinem` (all virtues have happiness as their end), as it says in Stobaeus' account of the Stoa (`Ecl.`, book II, chapter 7, p. 114, and also p. 138). Yet Stoic ethics shows that happiness is to be found securely only in inner peace and tranquility of mind, `ataraxia` (undisturbedness), and that this in turn is to be reached only through virtue. This alone is what the expression means when it says that virtue is the highest good.

If, however, the end is gradually forgotten over the means, and virtue is recommended in a way that betrays an entirely different interest from that of one's own happiness, indeed one that too plainly contradicts it, this is one of the inconsistencies by which, in every system, the immediately cognized, or as people say felt, truth forces conclusions back onto the right path. One sees this clearly, for example, in Spinoza's ethics, which derives a pure doctrine of virtue from the egoistic `suum utile quaerere` (to seek one's own advantage) by palpable sophisms.

As I understand the spirit of Stoic ethics, its origin lies in the thought whether the great prerogative of the human being, reason, which indirectly, through planned action and what follows from it, so greatly eases life and its burdens, might not also be capable of withdrawing him at once, either wholly or almost wholly, from the sufferings and torments of every kind that fill his life, immediately, that is, through mere cognition.

It was considered unworthy of reason's prerogative that the being endowed with it, who through it comprehends and surveys an infinity of things and conditions, should nevertheless be exposed, through the present and through the events that the few years of such a short, fleeting, uncertain life can contain, to such violent pains, such great anxiety and suffering, arising from the impetuous pressure of desire and avoidance. It was thought that the proper use of reason must be able to raise the human being above this and make him invulnerable.

Thus Antisthenes said:

> `aut mentem parandam, aut laqueum`
> Translation: Either one must prepare the mind, or prepare a noose. (`Plut. de stoic. repugn.`, chapter 14)

That is, life is so full of plagues and drudgeries that one must either, by means of corrected thoughts, set oneself above it, or leave it.

It was recognized that deprivation and suffering do not arise immediately and necessarily from not having, but only from wanting to have and yet not having. This wanting-to-have is therefore the necessary condition under which alone not-having becomes deprivation and produces pain.

> `non paupertas dolorem efficit, sed cupiditas`
> Translation: Poverty does not produce pain, but desire does. (`Epict. fragm. 25`)

It was also recognized from experience that hope alone, the claim, is what gives birth to and nourishes the wish. Therefore we are not disturbed and tormented by the many evils common to all and unavoidable, nor by goods that are unattainable, but only by the insignificant more or less of what human beings can avoid or attain. Indeed, not only what is absolutely, but even what is relatively unattainable or unavoidable leaves us quite calm. Thus evils that are once and for all attached to our individuality, or goods that must necessarily remain denied to it, are regarded with indifference; and, in accordance with this human peculiarity, every wish soon dies and can therefore no longer produce pain, if only no hope gives it nourishment.

From all this it followed that all happiness rests only on the relation between our claims and what we receive. Whether the two magnitudes in this relation are great or small is irrelevant; the relation can be established just as well by decreasing the first magnitude as by increasing the second. Likewise, all suffering really arises from the disproportion between what we demand and expect and what we receive. But this disproportion plainly lies only in cognition [^1], and could be entirely removed through better insight.

Thus Chrysippus said that one should live with proper knowledge of the course of things in the world (`Stob. Ecl.`, book II, chapter 7, p. 134). For whenever a human being loses composure in any way, is struck down by misfortune, becomes angry, or loses heart, he thereby shows that he finds things otherwise than he expected. Consequently, he had been caught in error; he did not know the world and life. He did not know how, at every step, lifeless nature through chance, and living nature through opposing aims and also through malice, cross the will of the individual.

He has either not used his reason in order to arrive at a universal knowledge of this character of life, or else he lacks power of judgment, since what he knows in general he fails to recognize again in the particular, and is therefore surprised and thrown out of composure by it. [^2]

So too every lively joy is an error, an illusion, because no attained wish can give lasting satisfaction, and because every possession and every happiness is only lent by chance for an indefinite time and can therefore be demanded back again in the next hour. Every pain rests on the disappearance of such an illusion. Both, then, arise from faulty cognition. Therefore jubilation and pain alike remain always far from the sage, and no event disturbs his `ataraxia` (undisturbedness).

In accordance with this spirit and purpose of the Stoa, *Epictetus* begins with, and constantly returns to, the core of his wisdom: that one should carefully consider and distinguish what depends on us and what does not, and therefore make absolutely no calculation on the latter. In this way one will reliably remain free from all pain, suffering, and anxiety.

What depends on us, however, is the will alone. Here there now occurs a gradual transition to the doctrine of virtue. It is observed that, just as the external world, which does not depend on us, determines happiness and unhappiness, so from the will arises inner satisfaction or dissatisfaction with ourselves.

Later the question was asked whether the names `bonum et malum` (good and evil) should be applied to the former pair or to the latter. This was really arbitrary and optional, and made no difference to the matter. Nevertheless the Stoics disputed over it endlessly with the Peripatetics and Epicureans, entertaining themselves with the inadmissible comparison of two wholly incommensurable magnitudes and with the opposed, paradoxical sayings that arose from it and that they hurled at one another. Cicero's `Paradoxa` gives us an interesting compilation of these from the Stoic side.

*Zeno*, the founder, seems originally to have taken a somewhat different path. His starting point was that, in order to attain the highest good, that is, blessedness and peace of mind, one should live in agreement with oneself.

> `Consonanter vivere: hoc est secundum unam rationem et concordem sibi vivere.`
> Translation: To live harmoniously: that is, to live according to one reason and in agreement with oneself. (`Stob. Ecl. eth.`, book II, chapter 7, p. 132)

Likewise:

> `Virtutem esse animi affectionem secum per totam vitam consentientem.`
> Translation: Virtue is a disposition of the soul agreeing with itself throughout the whole of life. (`ibid.`, p. 104)

But this was possible only by determining oneself throughout *rationally*, according to concepts, not according to changing impressions and moods. Since only the maxim of our action is in our power, not the success or the external circumstances, one had, in order always to remain consistent, to make only that maxim one's end, not success and external circumstances. In this way the doctrine of virtue was again introduced.

But already Zeno's immediate successors found his moral principle, living in agreement, too formal and empty of content. They therefore gave it material content by adding "to live in agreement with nature," which, as Stobaeus reports in the passage cited, was first added by *Cleanthes*. This pushed the matter very far afield, because of the wide sphere of the concept and the indefiniteness of the expression. For *Cleanthes* meant the whole of universal nature, but *Chrysippus* meant human nature in particular (`Diog. Laert.`, 7, 89).

What was appropriate to this latter alone was then supposed to be virtue, just as satisfaction of animal drives is appropriate to animal natures. Thus the doctrine was again forcibly turned toward virtue, and, come what may, ethics was to be grounded through physics. For the Stoics aimed everywhere at unity of principle; indeed, with them God and the world were not two different things at all.

Stoic ethics, taken as a whole, is in fact a very estimable and respectable attempt to use the great prerogative of the human being, reason, for an important and salutary purpose: namely, to raise him above the sufferings and pains to which every life has fallen prey, by means of an instruction:

> `Qua ratione queas traducere leniter aevum:`
> `Ne te semper inops agitet vexetque cupido,`
> `Ne pavor et rerum mediocriter utilium spes.`
> Translation: By what method you may pass your life gently: so that needy desire does not always drive and torment you, nor fear and hope for things of moderate use.

In this way it seeks to make the human being partake, in the highest degree, of the dignity that belongs to him as a rational being in contrast to the animal, and of which one can indeed speak in this sense, though not in another.

This view of Stoic ethics made it necessary to mention it here, in the presentation of what reason is and what it can accomplish. Yet however much that aim is attainable to a certain degree through the application of reason and through a merely rational ethics, as experience also shows that those purely rational characters commonly called practical philosophers are probably the happiest, and rightly so, since just as the genuine, that is, theoretical philosopher transfers life into the concept, they transfer the concept into life: still, a great deal is lacking before anything perfect of this kind can come about, or before correctly used reason could really withdraw us from all the burden and suffering of life and lead us to blessedness.

Rather, there is a complete contradiction in wanting to live without suffering, a contradiction already contained in the often-used expression "blessed life." This will certainly become clear to anyone who has grasped my following presentation to its end.

This contradiction already reveals itself in that ethics of pure reason itself, since the Stoic is forced to weave into his instruction for a blessed life, for that is what his ethics always remains, a recommendation of suicide. It is like the precious little vial of poison found among the magnificent ornament and equipment of Oriental despots. The recommendation applies in the case where bodily sufferings, which cannot be philosophized away by any propositions and inferences, become overwhelming and incurable. Then the Stoic's sole aim, blessedness, is nevertheless frustrated, and nothing remains for escaping suffering except death, which is then to be taken indifferently, like any other medicine.

Here a strong contrast becomes clear between Stoic ethics and all those doctrines mentioned above, which make virtue itself and immediately, even with the heaviest sufferings, the end, and do not want one to end life in order to escape suffering. None of them, however, knew how to state the true ground for rejecting suicide; instead they laboriously gathered all sorts of specious grounds. In the fourth book that ground will arise in the context of our consideration.

But the contrast just mentioned reveals and confirms precisely the essential difference, lying in the fundamental principle, between the Stoa, which is really only a special eudaimonism, and those doctrines just mentioned, although both often meet in their results and have an apparent kinship.

The inner contradiction already mentioned, with which Stoic ethics is burdened even in its basic thought, further shows itself in the fact that its ideal, the Stoic sage, could never gain life or inner poetic truth even in its own presentation. He remains a wooden, stiff mannequin, with whom one can do nothing, and who himself does not know what to do with his wisdom. His perfect calm, satisfaction, and blessedness directly contradict the essence of humanity and allow us to arrive at no intuitive representation of him.

How wholly different, when placed beside him, appear the world-conquerors and voluntary penitents that Indian wisdom presents to us and has actually produced; or even the Savior of Christianity, that excellent figure full of deep life, of the greatest poetic truth and highest significance, who nevertheless, with perfect virtue, holiness, and sublimity, stands before us in the state of the highest suffering.
