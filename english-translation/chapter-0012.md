# §. 10.

Through all this the question comes ever closer to us: how is *certainty* to be attained, how are *judgments to be grounded*, and in what do *knowledge* and science consist? Alongside language and deliberate action, we praise these as the third great advantage given by reason.

Reason is feminine in nature: it can give only after it has received. By itself alone it has nothing but the contentless forms of its operations. Indeed, there is no perfectly pure rational cognition other than the four propositions to which I have assigned metalogical truth: the propositions of identity, of contradiction, of excluded middle, and of the sufficient ground of knowing.

For even the rest of logic is no longer perfectly pure rational cognition, because it presupposes the relations and combinations of the spheres of concepts. But concepts in general exist only after preceding intuitive representations; their relation to these representations makes up their whole essence, and therefore they already presuppose them. Yet since this presupposition does not extend to the determinate content of the concepts, but only in general to their existence, logic can still, taken as a whole, count as a pure science of reason.

In all other sciences reason has received its content from intuitive representations: in mathematics, from the relations of space and time intuitively conscious before all experience; in pure natural science, that is, in what we know about the course of nature before all experience, the content of the science comes from the pure understanding, namely from cognition `a priori` (prior to experience) of the law of causality and its connection with those pure intuitions of space and time. In all other sciences, everything that is not borrowed from what has just been named belongs to experience.

*Knowledge* in general means having in one's mind, available for voluntary reproduction, judgments that have their sufficient ground of knowing in something outside themselves, that is, judgments that are *true*. Abstract cognition alone, therefore, is knowledge. Knowledge is consequently conditioned by reason; and, strictly speaking, we cannot say of animals that they *know* anything, although they have intuitive cognition and, for this cognition, memory, and precisely because of memory, imagination as well, which is also proved by their dreaming.

We attribute consciousness to animals; and the concept of consciousness, although the word is derived from knowing, therefore coincides with that of representation in general, whatever kind it may be. Accordingly, we attribute life to the plant, but not consciousness.

Thus *knowledge* is abstract consciousness: the holding fixed in concepts of reason of what has been cognized in some other way.
