# §. 12.

*Knowledge*, whose contradictory opposite I have just discussed under the concept of feeling, is, as stated, every abstract cognition, that is, rational cognition. But since reason always only brings before cognition again what has been received in some other way, it does not really enlarge our cognition, but merely gives it another form. What was cognized intuitively, what was cognized `in concreto` (in the concrete), reason allows us to know abstractly and universally.

This, however, is incomparably more important than it appears at first glance when stated in this way. For all secure preservation, all communicability, and every secure and far-reaching application of cognition to practical life depend on its having become knowledge, an abstract cognition. Intuitive cognition always holds only for the individual case; it is directed only to what is nearest and stops there, because sensibility and understanding can properly grasp only *one* object at a time. Every sustained, composite, planned activity must therefore proceed from principles, that is, from abstract knowledge, and be guided by them.

For example, the cognition that the understanding has of the relation of cause and effect is in itself much more perfect, deeper, and more exhaustive than anything that can be thought of it `in abstracto` (in the abstract). The understanding alone cognizes intuitively, immediately, and completely the manner in which a lever, a pulley, or a cogwheel acts, the way an arch supports itself, and so on. But because intuitive cognition, as just noted, is directed only to what is immediately present, the mere understanding is not sufficient for the construction of machines and buildings. Here reason must enter; it must put abstract concepts in place of intuitions, take them as the rule of action, and, if they are correct, success will follow.

Likewise, in pure intuition we cognize perfectly the essence and lawfulness of a parabola, hyperbola, or spiral. But in order to make secure use of this cognition in actuality, it first had to become abstract knowledge; in doing so, it of course loses intuitiveness, but gains the security and determinacy of abstract knowledge. Thus the whole differential calculus does not really enlarge our cognition of curves at all; it contains nothing more than what already lies in the mere pure intuition of them. But it changes the kind of cognition: it transforms intuitive cognition into abstract cognition, and this has the greatest consequences for application.

Here, however, a peculiarity of our faculty of cognition comes into view, one that could hardly have been noticed before the distinction between intuitive and abstract cognition had been made completely clear. It is this: relations of space cannot be transferred immediately and as such into abstract cognition; only temporal magnitudes, that is, numbers, are suited to this. Numbers alone can be expressed in abstract concepts exactly corresponding to them, not spatial magnitudes.

The concept *thousand* differs from the concept *ten* in exactly the same way as the two temporal magnitudes differ in intuition. In thinking *thousand*, we think a determinate multiple of *ten*, into which we can at will resolve it for intuition in time, that is, count it. But between the abstract concept of a mile and that of a foot, without any intuitive representation of either and without the help of number, there is no exact difference corresponding to those magnitudes themselves. In both, only a spatial magnitude in general is thought. If the two are to be sufficiently distinguished, either spatial intuition must be called in to help, in which case one has already left the domain of abstract cognition, or the difference must be thought in *numbers*.

If, therefore, one wants abstract cognition of spatial relations, they must first be transferred into temporal relations, that is, into numbers. For this reason arithmetic alone, not geometry, is the universal science of magnitude; and geometry must be translated into arithmetic if it is to have communicability, exact determinacy, and applicability to practical life. A spatial relation as such can certainly also be thought `in abstracto`, for example, "the sine increases according to the measure of the angle"; but if the magnitude of this relation is to be stated, number is needed.

This necessity, that space, with its three dimensions, must be translated into time, which has only one dimension, if one wants to have an abstract cognition, that is, a *knowledge* and not a mere intuition, of its relations: this necessity is what makes mathematics so difficult.

This becomes very clear when we compare the intuition of curves with their analytic calculation, or even merely the tables of logarithms of the trigonometric functions with the intuition of the changing relations of the parts of the triangle that are expressed by those tables. What intuition here grasps in a single glance, completely and with the utmost exactness, namely how the cosine decreases as the sine increases, how the cosine of one angle is the sine of the other, the inverse relation between the decrease and increase of both angles, and so on: what an enormous fabric of numbers, what laborious calculation, was needed in order to express this `in abstracto`! One might say: how time, with its *one* dimension, must torment itself in order to reproduce the three dimensions of space!

But this was necessary if, for the sake of application, we wanted to possess the relations of space deposited in abstract concepts. They could not pass into these concepts immediately, but only through the mediation of the purely temporal magnitude, number, which alone attaches itself immediately to abstract cognition.

It is further worth noting that just as space is so well suited to intuition and, by means of its three dimensions, allows even complicated relations to be easily surveyed, while it withdraws from abstract cognition, time conversely enters easily into abstract concepts but gives very little to intuition. Our intuition of numbers in their proper element, mere time, without the addition of space, scarcely reaches ten. Beyond that, we have only abstract concepts, no longer intuitive cognition of numbers. By contrast, we connect exactly determinate abstract concepts with every number word and with all algebraic signs.

Incidentally, it should be observed here that some minds find complete satisfaction only in what is intuitively cognized. What they seek is the ground and consequent of being in space, presented intuitively; a Euclidean proof, or an arithmetical solution of spatial problems, does not appeal to them. Other minds, by contrast, require the abstract concepts that alone are usable for application and communication. They have patience and memory for abstract propositions, formulae, demonstrations in long chains of inference, and calculations whose signs represent the most complicated abstractions. These seek determinacy; the former, intuitiveness. The difference is characteristic.

Knowledge, abstract cognition, has its greatest value in communicability and in the possibility of being preserved in fixed form. Only through this does it become so inestimably important for practical life. A person can have an immediate intuitive cognition, in the mere understanding, of the causal connection of the changes and motions of natural bodies, and can find complete satisfaction in it. But it first becomes fit for communication after he has fixed it in concepts.

Even for practical purposes, cognition of the first kind is sufficient when the person also undertakes the execution entirely by himself, and in an action that can be carried out while the intuitive cognition is still alive; but not when he needs the help of others, or even only his own action at different times and therefore a considered plan.

For example, a practiced billiards player can have a complete knowledge of the laws governing the impact of elastic bodies on one another merely in the understanding, merely for immediate intuition, and this is wholly sufficient for him. By contrast, only the scientific mechanic has genuine knowledge of those laws, that is, cognition `in abstracto` of them.

Even for the construction of machines, that merely intuitive cognition of the understanding is enough if the inventor of the machine also carries it out alone, as one often sees in talented craftsmen without any science. By contrast, as soon as several people and a composite activity occurring at different times are needed to carry out a mechanical operation, a machine, or a building, the person directing it must have designed the plan `in abstracto`; and such cooperative activity is possible only with the aid of reason.

It is remarkable, however, that in that first kind of activity, where one person alone is to carry something out in one uninterrupted action, knowledge, the application of reason, reflection, can often even be a hindrance. This is so, for example, in billiards, fencing, tuning an instrument, and singing. Here intuitive cognition must guide the activity immediately. Passing through reflection makes it uncertain, because it divides attention and confuses the person.

For this reason, wild and uncultivated people, who are very little accustomed to thinking, perform many bodily exercises, fights with animals, shooting with arrows, and the like with a certainty and speed that the reflective European never attains, precisely because deliberation makes him waver and hesitate. For example, he tries to find the right place or the right moment by locating it at an equal distance from the two false extremes; the natural person hits it immediately, without reflecting on the wrong paths.

Likewise, it does not help me to know how to state `in abstracto`, in degrees and minutes, the angle at which I must apply the razor, if I do not know it intuitively, that is, if I do not have it in my grip.

The application of reason is disturbing in the same way in the understanding of physiognomy. This too must occur immediately through the understanding. The expression, the meaning of the features, can only be *felt*, as one says; that is, it does not pass into abstract concepts. Every human being has his immediate intuitive physiognomics and pathognomics, though one person recognizes that `signatura rerum` (signature of things) more clearly than another.

But a physiognomics `in abstracto` for teaching and learning cannot be brought about, because the nuances here are so fine that the concept cannot descend to them. Abstract knowledge therefore stands to them as a mosaic picture does to a *van der Werff* or a *Denner*: however fine the mosaic may be, the boundaries of the stones always remain, and therefore no continuous transition from one tint into another is possible. So too concepts, with their rigidity and sharp boundaries, however finely one may try to divide them through closer determination, always remain incapable of reaching the subtle modifications of the intuitive, on which everything depends in the physiognomics used here as an example. [^1]

This same constitution of concepts, which makes them similar to the stones of a mosaic picture and by virtue of which intuition always remains their asymptote, is also the reason why nothing good is achieved in art through them. If the singer or virtuoso tries to guide his performance by reflection, it remains dead. The same holds for the composer, the painter, and indeed the poet. For art, the concept always remains unfruitful; it may guide only the technical element in art. Its domain is science. In the third book we will investigate more closely why all genuine art proceeds from intuitive cognition and never from the concept.

Even with regard to conduct, to personal agreeableness in social intercourse, the concept is useful only negatively, in holding back the crude outbursts of egoism and bestiality, as indeed politeness is its praiseworthy work. But what is attractive, graceful, and winning in conduct, what is loving and friendly, must not have proceeded from the concept; otherwise:

> One feels the intention and is put out of tune.

All dissimulation is the work of reflection, but it cannot be sustained for long and without interruption: `nemo potest personam diu ferre fictam` (no one can long wear a feigned character), says Seneca in the book `De clementia` (On Clemency). It is then usually recognized, and misses its effect.

In the high pressure of life, where quick decisions, bold action, and swift and firm grasping are needed, reason is certainly necessary. But if it gains the upper hand and confusingly hinders the intuitive, immediate, purely intellectual finding and seizing of the right thing, thereby producing indecision, it can easily spoil everything.

Finally, virtue and holiness do not arise from reflection either, but from the inner depth of the will and its relation to cognition. This discussion belongs in an entirely different place in this work. Here I only wish to note that the dogmas relating to ethics can be the same in the reason of whole nations, while action in each individual is different; and the reverse can also occur. Action takes place, as people say, according to *feelings*: that is, precisely not according to concepts, at least in its ethical content.

Dogmas occupy idle reason; action ultimately goes its own way independently of them, usually not according to abstract maxims but according to unspoken ones, whose expression is the whole human being himself. Therefore, however different the religious dogmas of nations may be, among all of them the good deed is accompanied by an inexpressible satisfaction, and the evil deed by infinite horror. No mockery shakes the former; no absolution by a confessor frees one from the latter.

Nevertheless, this is not meant to deny that the application of reason is necessary in carrying out a virtuous course of life. It is simply not the source of such a life. Its function is subordinate: preserving resolutions once formed, keeping maxims before us in order to resist the weakness of the moment and to maintain consistency in action.

In the end, reason performs the same service in art as well. There too it accomplishes nothing in the main matter, but it supports the execution, precisely because genius is not available at every hour, while the work must nevertheless be completed in all its parts and rounded into a whole. [^2]
