# §. 11.

In this respect the proper opposite of *knowledge* is *feeling*, and for that reason we must insert a discussion of it here.

The concept designated by the word *feeling* has throughout only a *negative* content: namely, that something present in consciousness is *not a concept, not abstract cognition of reason*. Apart from that, it may be whatever it likes; it falls under the concept *feeling*. The immeasurably wide sphere of this concept therefore includes the most heterogeneous things, and one can never see how they belong together until one has recognized that they agree only in this negative respect: that they are *not abstract concepts*.

For the most different, even hostile, elements lie peacefully beside one another in that concept: religious feeling, feeling of sensual pleasure, moral feeling, bodily feeling as touch, as pain, as feeling for colors, for tones and their harmonies and disharmonies, feelings of hatred, disgust, self-satisfaction, honor, shame, right, wrong, feeling for truth, aesthetic feeling, feeling of strength, weakness, health, friendship, love, and so on and so on. There is absolutely nothing common among them except the negative fact that they are not abstract rational cognition.

This becomes most striking when even the intuitive cognition `a priori` (prior to experience) of spatial relations, and still more that of the pure understanding, is brought under this concept; and when, in general, every cognition, every truth of which one is at first only intuitively conscious, but which one has not yet deposited in abstract concepts, is said to be something one *feels*.

For illustration, I will give a few examples from recent books, because they are striking evidence for my explanation. I remember reading, in the introduction to a German version of Euclid, that beginners in geometry should first be made to draw all the figures before proceeding to demonstration, because they would then already *feel* the geometrical truth beforehand, before the demonstration supplied them with complete cognition.

Similarly, F. Schleiermacher's *Critique of Ethics* speaks of logical and mathematical feeling (p. 339), and also of the feeling of equality or difference between two formulae (p. 342). Further, in Tennemann's *History of Philosophy*, vol. 1, p. 361, it says: "One *felt* that the fallacies were not correct, but still could not discover the error."

So long as one does not view this concept, *feeling*, from the right standpoint, and does not recognize the one negative mark that alone is essential to it, the concept must constantly give rise to misunderstandings and disputes, because of the excessive breadth of its sphere and its merely negative, wholly one-sidedly determined, and very meager content.

Since in German we still have the almost equivalent word *Empfindung* (sensation), it would be useful to reserve it for bodily feelings as a subspecies. The origin of that concept *feeling*, so disproportionate to all others, is without doubt the following. All concepts, and only concepts are what words designate, exist only for reason and proceed from it. With them, therefore, one already stands at a one-sided standpoint.

But from such a standpoint what is nearer appears distinctly and is posited as positive; what is farther away flows together and is soon considered only negatively. Thus every nation calls all others foreigners; the Greek called all others barbarians; the Englishman calls everything that is not England or English `the Continent` and `continental`; the believer calls all others heretics or heathens; the nobility calls all others `roturiers` (commoners); the student calls all others Philistines; and so forth.

The very same one-sidedness, one might say the same crude ignorance born of pride, is, strange as it sounds, something of which reason itself is guilty. It includes under the *one* concept *feeling* every modification of consciousness that does not belong immediately to *its* mode of representation, that is, that is *not an abstract concept*.

Until now reason has had to pay for this, because its own procedure had not become clear to it through thorough self-knowledge, with misunderstandings and aberrations in its own domain. People have even set up a special faculty of feeling and now construct theories of it.
