# §. 15.

If, with our conviction that intuition is the first source of all evidence, and that only an immediate or mediated relation to it is absolute truth; and further, that the shortest path to this truth is always the safest, since every mediation through concepts exposes us to many deceptions: if, I say, with this conviction we turn to *mathematics*, as Euclid established it as a science and as, on the whole, it has remained down to the present day, then we cannot help finding the path it takes strange, indeed perverse.

We demand that every logical grounding be led back to an intuitive one. Mathematics, however, makes great efforts deliberately to reject the intuitive evidence peculiar to it, which lies near at hand everywhere, in order to substitute logical evidence for it. We must find this like someone cutting off his legs in order to walk on crutches, or like the prince in *The Triumph of Sensibility*, who flees from real, beautiful nature in order to enjoy a theatrical decoration that imitates it.

Here I must recall what I said in the sixth chapter of the essay on the principle of sufficient reason, and I assume it to be freshly and fully present in the reader's memory. I therefore attach my remarks to it without again setting out the difference between the mere ground of knowing of a mathematical truth, which can be given logically, and the ground of being, which is the immediate connection of the parts of space and time, knowable only intuitively. Insight into this alone gives true satisfaction and thorough knowledge, whereas the mere ground of knowing always remains on the surface and can indeed give knowledge *that* it is so, but not knowledge of *why* it is so.

Euclid took this latter path, to the obvious detriment of the science. For example, at the very beginning, where he ought once and for all to have shown how, in the triangle, angles and sides determine one another reciprocally, and are ground and consequent of one another according to the form that the principle of sufficient reason has in mere space; where, as everywhere, this form gives the necessity that one thing is as it is because something else, entirely different from it, is as it is: instead of giving a thorough insight into the essence of the triangle, he sets up a few detached and arbitrarily chosen propositions about the triangle and gives a logical ground of knowing for them through a laborious proof conducted logically according to the principle of contradiction.

Instead of exhaustive cognition of these spatial relations, one therefore receives only a few arbitrarily communicated results from them, and is in the position of someone who is shown the different effects of an artificial machine while its inner connection and mechanism are withheld from him. One must, compelled by the principle of contradiction, admit that everything Euclid demonstrates is so. But one does not learn *why* it is so.

One therefore has almost the uneasy feeling one has after a conjuring trick; and in fact most Euclidean proofs are strikingly similar to such tricks. Almost always the truth enters by the back door, since it follows `per accidens` (accidentally) from some incidental circumstance. Often an apagogic proof closes all doors one after another and leaves only one open, through which one must enter merely for that reason. Often, as in the Pythagorean theorem, lines are drawn without one knowing why; afterward it appears that they were snares that unexpectedly tighten and capture the learner's assent. The learner must then, amazed, admit what remains wholly incomprehensible to him in its inner connection, so much so that he can study all of Euclid without gaining genuine insight into the laws of spatial relations, and instead merely memorizes a few results from them.

This really empirical and unscientific cognition resembles that of the physician who knows the disease and the remedy for it, but not the connection between them. All this is the consequence of capriciously rejecting the mode of grounding and evidence proper to one kind of cognition, and forcibly introducing in its place a mode foreign to its essence.

Yet the manner in which Euclid carried this through deserves all the admiration it has received for so many centuries. That admiration went so far that his treatment of mathematics was declared the model for every scientific presentation, and people even tried to model all other sciences on it, though later they drew back from this without quite knowing why. In our eyes, however, Euclid's method in mathematics can only appear as a very brilliant perversity.

Now, for every great error that is pursued deliberately and methodically and is accompanied by general approval, whether it concerns life or science, its ground can probably always be shown in the philosophy dominant at its time. The Eleatics were the first to discover the distinction, and indeed the frequent conflict, between what is intuited, `phainomenon` (appearance), and what is thought, `nooumenon` (object of thought), [^1] and they used this distinction in many ways for their philosophical doctrines and also for sophisms.

They were followed later by the Megarians, dialecticians, Sophists, New Academics, and Skeptics. These drew attention to semblance, that is, to the deception of the senses, or rather of the understanding that transforms their data into intuition, which often lets us see things to which reason with certainty denies reality, for example the stick broken in water and the like. It was recognized that sensible intuition was not to be trusted unconditionally, and people hastily concluded that rational, logical thinking alone grounds truth.

Yet Plato, in the *Parmenides*, the Megarians, Pyrrho, and the New Academics showed by examples, in the manner later used by Sextus Empiricus, that inferences and concepts also mislead, and indeed produce paralogisms and sophisms that arise much more easily and are much harder to resolve than semblance in sensible intuition. Meanwhile, rationalism, which had arisen in opposition to empiricism, retained the upper hand. Euclid worked mathematics accordingly, resting only the axioms, under compulsion, on intuitive evidence, and everything else on inferences.

His method remained dominant through all the centuries, and had to remain so as long as pure intuition `a priori` (prior to experience) had not been distinguished from empirical intuition. Euclid's commentator Proclus does seem already to have recognized that distinction completely, as is shown by the passage from him that Kepler translated into Latin in his book `De harmonia mundi` (On the Harmony of the World). But Proclus did not lay enough weight on the matter, set it up too much in isolation, remained unnoticed, and did not prevail.

Only two thousand years later, therefore, will Kant's doctrine, which is destined to bring about such great changes in all the knowledge, thinking, and activity of the European peoples, also bring about such a change in mathematics. For only after we have learned from this great mind that the intuitions of space and time are wholly different from empirical intuition, wholly independent of every impression on the senses, conditioning such impressions and not conditioned by them, that is, that they are `a priori`, and therefore are not at all open to sensory illusion: only now can we see that Euclid's logical treatment of mathematics is a useless precaution, a crutch for healthy legs.

It resembles a traveler who, at night, takes a bright, firm road for water, avoids stepping onto it, and keeps walking beside it on rough ground, satisfied when every so often he touches the supposed water. Only now can we assert with certainty that what announces itself to us as necessary in the intuition of a figure does not come from the figure that may be drawn very imperfectly on paper, nor from the abstract concept we think in connection with it, but immediately from the form of all cognition that is conscious to us `a priori`.

This form is everywhere the principle of sufficient reason. Here, as the form of intuition, that is, space, it is the principle of the ground of being. Its evidence and validity are just as great and immediate as those of the principle of the ground of knowing, that is, logical certainty. We therefore neither need nor may leave the proper territory of mathematics in order, simply from trust in logical certainty, to authenticate mathematics on a territory wholly foreign to it, that of concepts.

If we keep to the ground proper to mathematics, we gain the great advantage that in it knowledge *that* something is so is one with knowledge of *why* it is so. By contrast, the Euclidean method separates the two completely, and lets us know only the former, not the latter. Aristotle says quite excellently in `Analyt. post. 1, 27`:

> `Αϰϱιβεστεϱα δ' επστημη επστημης ϰαι πϱοτεϱα, ἡτε του ὁτι ϰαι του διοτι ἡ αυτη, αλλα μη χωϱις του ὁτι, της του διοτι.`
> `Suptilior autem et prastantior ea est scientia, qua quod aliquid sit, et cur sit una simulque intellegimus, non separatim quod, et cur sit.`
> Translation: More exact and superior is the science in which we understand both that something is and why it is at one and the same time, not separately the that and the why.

In physics, too, we are satisfied only when cognition *that* something is so is united with cognition of *why* it is so. That the mercury in the Torricellian tube stands twenty-eight inches high is poor knowledge unless one also adds that it is held there by the counterweight of the air. But in mathematics are we supposed to be satisfied with a `qualitas occulta` (hidden quality) of the circle, namely that the segments of any two chords intersecting in it always form equal rectangles? Euclid indeed proves that it is so in the thirty-fifth proposition of the third book; but the why still remains undecided.

Likewise, the Pythagorean theorem acquaints us with a `qualitas occulta` (hidden quality) of the right triangle. Euclid's stilted, indeed sly, proof leaves us stranded at the why; while the adjacent, already familiar, simple figure gives at one glance far more than that proof: insight into the matter and a firm inner conviction of that necessity and of the dependence of that property on the right angle.

Even with unequal legs it must be possible to bring the matter to such an intuitive conviction, as it must be with every possible geometrical truth, if only because its discovery always proceeded from such an intuited necessity and the proof was only devised afterward. One therefore needs only an analysis of the course of thought in the first discovery of a geometrical truth in order to recognize its necessity intuitively. In general, it is the analytic method that I wish for the presentation of mathematics, instead of the synthetic method Euclid used.

Of course, with complicated mathematical truths this will present very great, though not insurmountable, difficulties. Here and there in Germany people are already beginning to change the presentation of mathematics and to take more of this analytic path. Herr *Kosack*, teacher of mathematics and physics at the Gymnasium in Nordhausen, has done this most decisively by adding to the program for the school examination on April 6, 1852, a detailed attempt to treat geometry according to my principles.

To improve the method of mathematics, what is chiefly required is that one give up the prejudice that proved truth has any advantage over intuitively cognized truth, or that logical truth, resting on the principle of contradiction, has any advantage over metaphysical truth, which is immediately evident and to which the pure intuition of space also belongs.

The most certain and everywhere inexplicable thing is the content of the principle of sufficient reason. For this principle, in its different forms, designates the universal form of all our representations and cognitions. Every explanation is a reduction to it, a showing in the individual case of the connection of representations expressed by it in general. It is therefore the principle of all explanation, and for that reason is itself neither capable nor in need of explanation, since every explanation already presupposes it and receives meaning only through it.

None of its forms, however, has any privilege over another. It is equally certain and indemonstrable as the principle of the ground of being, of becoming, of acting, or of knowing. The relation of ground to consequent is necessary in one of its forms just as in another; indeed, it is in general the origin and sole meaning of the concept of necessity. There is no necessity other than that of the consequent when the ground is given, and there is no ground that would not bring about necessity of the consequent.

Therefore, just as surely as the consequent expressed in the conclusion flows from the ground of knowing given in the premises, so surely the ground of being in space conditions its consequent in space. If I have intuitively cognized the relation of these two, then this certainty is as great as any logical certainty.

Every geometrical theorem is the expression of such a relation, just as much as any of the twelve axioms. It is a metaphysical truth, and as such just as immediately certain as the principle of contradiction itself, which is a metalogical truth and the universal foundation of all logical demonstration. Whoever denies the intuitively displayed necessity of the spatial relations expressed in some theorem can with equal right deny the axioms, and with equal right deny the consequent of an inference from the premises, indeed the principle of contradiction itself. For all these are equally indemonstrable, immediately evident, and knowable `a priori`.

If, therefore, one first wants to derive the intuitively knowable necessity of spatial relations through a logical demonstration from the principle of contradiction, this is no different from someone first wanting to grant a country as a fief to its immediate lord. Yet this is what Euclid did. Under compulsion, he allows only his axioms to rest on immediate evidence. All the following geometrical truths are proved logically: on the assumption of those axioms, from agreement with the assumptions made in the theorem, or with an earlier theorem, or else from the contradiction of the opposite of the theorem with the assumptions, the axioms, the earlier theorems, or even with itself. But the axioms themselves have no more immediate evidence than any other geometrical theorem; they have only more simplicity because they have less content.

When one interrogates an accused person, one takes down his statements in a protocol in order to judge their truth from their agreement with one another. But this is a mere makeshift, and one does not stop there if one can investigate the truth of each of his statements directly in itself, especially since he may have lied consistently from the beginning.

Yet this first method is the one by which Euclid investigated space. He did indeed proceed from the correct presupposition that nature everywhere, and therefore also in its fundamental form, space, must be consistent; and that because the parts of space stand to one another in the relation of ground and consequent, no single spatial determination can be otherwise than it is without contradicting all the others. But this is a very laborious and unsatisfying detour. It prefers mediate cognition to immediate cognition, which is just as certain; it separates cognition *that* something is from cognition of *why* it is, to the great disadvantage of the science; and finally it wholly withholds from the learner insight into the laws of space.

Indeed, it accustoms him away from the genuine investigation of the ground and inner connection of things, and instead instructs him to be satisfied with a historical knowledge *that* it is so. The exercise of acuteness so incessantly praised in this method consists merely in the student's practicing inference, that is, applying the principle of contradiction, and above all straining his memory to retain all those data whose agreement is to be compared.

It is also noteworthy that this method of proof has been applied only to geometry, not to arithmetic. In arithmetic, truth is really allowed to become evident through intuition alone, which here consists in mere counting. Since the intuition of numbers is in *time alone*, and therefore cannot be represented through any sensible schema such as the geometrical figure, the suspicion fell away here that intuition is only empirical and therefore subject to semblance. That suspicion alone could have brought the logical mode of proof into geometry.

Because time has only one dimension, counting is the only arithmetical operation to which all others can be reduced. Yet this counting is nothing other than intuition `a priori`, to which one has no hesitation in appealing here, and through which alone everything else, every calculation, every equation, is ultimately confirmed. For example, one does not prove that `(7 + 9 x 8 - 2) / 3 = 42`; rather, one appeals to pure intuition in time, to counting, and thus makes every individual proposition into an axiom.

Instead of the proofs that fill geometry, the whole content of arithmetic and algebra is therefore a mere method for shortening counting. Our immediate intuition of numbers in time, as mentioned above, does not extend much beyond ten. Beyond that, an abstract concept of number, fixed by a word, must take the place of intuition; intuition is therefore no longer actually performed, but only quite determinately indicated.

Even so, by means of the important aid of the order of numbers, which always allows larger numbers to be represented by the same smaller ones, intuitive evidence for every calculation is made possible, even where abstraction is called in so extensively that not only numbers but indeterminate magnitudes and whole operations are thought only `in abstracto` and are designated in this respect, as in `sqrt(r-b)`, so that one no longer performs them but merely indicates them.

With the same right and the same certainty as in arithmetic, one could also allow truth in geometry to be grounded solely through pure intuition `a priori`. In fact, it is always this necessity, intuitively cognized according to the principle of the ground of being, that gives geometry its great evidence, and on which the certainty of its propositions rests in everyone's consciousness. It is by no means the logical proof walking on stilts, which always remains foreign to the matter and is usually soon forgotten without any harm to conviction. It could disappear entirely without diminishing the evidence of geometry, since that evidence is wholly independent of it; the proof always proves only what one already has complete conviction of beforehand through another kind of cognition. In this respect it resembles a cowardly soldier who wounds an enemy already slain by others, and then boasts that he killed him. [^2]

From all this it will, I hope, no longer be subject to doubt that the evidence of mathematics, which has become the model and symbol of all evidence, rests essentially not on proofs but on immediate intuition, which here, as everywhere, is the ultimate ground and source of all truth. Yet the intuition that grounds mathematics has a great advantage over every other intuition, and thus over empirical intuition. Since it is `a priori`, and therefore independent of experience, which is always given only partially and successively, everything in it lies equally near, and one can start at will from the ground or from the consequent.

This gives it complete infallibility, because in it the consequent is cognized from the ground, and this cognition alone has necessity. For example, the equality of the sides is cognized as grounded by the equality of the angles. By contrast, all empirical intuition and the greatest part of all experience proceeds the other way around, from the consequent to the ground. This kind of cognition is not infallible, since necessity belongs only to the consequent insofar as the ground is given, not to the cognition of the ground from the consequent, since the same consequent can arise from different grounds.

This latter kind of cognition is always only induction: that is, from many consequents that point to a ground, the ground is assumed as certain. But since the cases can never be completely gathered together, the truth here is never unconditionally certain. This kind of truth alone belongs to all cognition through sensible intuition and to the great majority of experience.

The affection of a sense occasions an inference of the understanding from the effect to the cause. But because the inference from what is grounded to the ground is never certain, false semblance, as sensory deception, is possible and often actual, as was explained above. Only when several, or all five, senses receive affections that point to the same cause has the possibility of semblance become extremely small, though it still exists; for in certain cases, for example with counterfeit coins, the whole of sensibility is deceived.

All empirical cognition is in the same position, and therefore so is all natural science, leaving aside its pure, or in Kant's sense metaphysical, part. Here too causes are cognized from effects. Therefore all natural theory rests on hypotheses, which are often false and then gradually give way to more correct ones. Only in deliberately arranged experiments does cognition proceed from the cause to the effect, and therefore along the secure path; but experiments themselves are undertaken only as a consequence of hypotheses.

For this reason no branch of natural science, for example physics, astronomy, or physiology, could be discovered all at once, as mathematics or logic could. It needed, and still needs, the collected and compared experiences of many centuries. Only repeated empirical confirmation brings the induction on which the hypothesis rests so near to completeness that, for practice, it takes the place of certainty. The fact that the hypothesis arose in this way is then considered no more damaging to it than the incommensurability of straight and curved lines is damaging to the application of geometry, or the unattainable complete exactness of the logarithm to the application of arithmetic.

For just as one brings the quadrature of the circle and the logarithm infinitely close to correctness by infinite fractions, so too through manifold experience induction, that is, cognition of the ground from the consequents, is brought not infinitely, but still so close to mathematical evidence, that is, to cognition of the consequent from the ground, that the possibility of deception becomes small enough to be neglected. Yet it is still present.

For example, an inductive inference is also the inference from innumerable cases to all cases, that is, actually to the unknown ground on which all depend. What inference of this kind seems more certain than that all human beings have the heart on the left side? And yet, as extremely rare and wholly isolated exceptions, there are human beings whose heart sits on the right side.

Sensible intuition and experiential science therefore have the same kind of evidence. The advantage that mathematics, pure natural science, and logic have over them as cognitions `a priori` rests solely on the fact that the formal element of cognition, on which all apriority is grounded, is given as a whole and at once. Therefore here one can always proceed from the ground to the consequent, while there one can usually proceed only from the consequent to the ground.

In itself, moreover, the law of causality, or the principle of the ground of becoming, which guides empirical cognition, is just as certain as those other forms of the principle of sufficient reason that the sciences mentioned above follow `a priori`.

Logical proofs from concepts, or inferences, share with cognition through intuition `a priori` the advantage of proceeding from the ground to the consequent, by which they are in themselves, that is, in their form, infallible. This has contributed much to bringing proofs in general into such great esteem. But this infallibility is relative: proofs merely subsume under the upper propositions of the science. These, however, contain the whole fund of truth of the science, and they must not themselves be merely proved, but must be grounded on intuition; in those few sciences mentioned above this intuition is pure `a priori`, while otherwise it is always empirical and is raised to universality only by induction.

Therefore, even if in the experiential sciences the individual is proved from the universal, the universal itself has received its truth only from the individual. It is only a storehouse of collected supplies, not a self-generating ground.

So much for the grounding of truth. Many explanations have been attempted, since Plato's pictorial solutions involving the dovecote, where one grasps the wrong dove, and so on (`Theaetetus`, p. 167 ff.), concerning the origin and possibility of *error*. Kant's vague and indeterminate explanation of the origin of error by means of the image of diagonal motion is found in the *Critique of Pure Reason*, p. 294 of the first edition and p. 350 of the fifth.

Since truth is the relation of a judgment to its ground of knowing, it is certainly a problem how the person judging can really believe he has such a ground and yet not have one: that is, how error, the deception of reason, is possible. I find this possibility wholly analogous to that of semblance, or deception of the understanding, explained above. My view, and this is precisely why this explanation has its place here, is that *every error is an inference from the consequent to the ground*. Such an inference is valid when one knows that the consequent can have this ground and absolutely no other; otherwise it is not valid.

The person who errs either assigns to the consequent a ground that it cannot have at all, in which case he shows a real lack of understanding, that is, of the capacity for immediate cognition of the connection between cause and effect; or, what is more often the case, he assigns to the consequent a ground that is indeed possible, but adds to the major premise of his inference from the consequent to the ground that the stated consequent *always* arises only from the ground he has indicated. Only a complete induction could entitle him to this, but he presupposes it without having made it.

That *always* is therefore too wide a concept; in its place there should stand only *sometimes* or *mostly*. Then the conclusion would turn out problematic and, as such, would not be erroneous. That the erring person proceeds in the way indicated is due either to haste or to too limited a knowledge of possibility, because of which he does not know the necessity of the induction to be made.

Error is therefore wholly analogous to semblance. Both are inferences from the consequent to the ground. Semblance is always carried out according to the law of causality and by the mere understanding, thus immediately in intuition itself. Error is carried out according to all forms of the principle of sufficient reason, by reason, and therefore in thinking proper, though most often also according to the law of causality. The following three examples may be regarded as types or representatives of three kinds of error.

1. Sensory semblance, deception of the understanding, occasions error, deception of reason: for example, when one takes a painting for a bas-relief and really holds it to be one. This occurs through an inference from the following major premise: "If dark gray passes locally through all nuances into white, then the cause is *always* light striking raised and recessed surfaces unequally: `ergo` (therefore) ..."

2. "If money is missing from my cashbox, then the cause is *always* that my servant has a duplicate key: `ergo` ..."

3. "If the sun's image refracted through the prism, that is, shifted upward or downward, now appears elongated and colored instead of round and white as before, then the cause, once and for all, is that differently colored and at the same time differently refrangible homogeneous rays of light were contained in the light, and these, separated by their different refrangibility, now show an elongated and at the same time differently colored image: `ergo - bibamus!` (therefore, let us drink!)"

Every error must be reducible to such an inference from a hypothetical major premise that has often been falsely generalized, and that arose from the assumption of a ground for a consequent. This does not include mere mistakes in calculation, which are not really errors but simple mistakes: the operation indicated by the concepts of the numbers was not carried out in pure intuition, in counting, but another operation was carried out in its place.

As for the *content* of the sciences in general, it is really always the relation of the appearances of the world to one another according to the principle of sufficient reason, and under the guidance of the why that is valid and meaningful through that principle alone. The demonstration of that relation is called *explanation*. Explanation, therefore, can never go further than showing two representations in the relation expressed by the form of the principle of sufficient reason that governs the class to which they belong.

Once explanation has reached this point, no further why can be asked at all. For the relation demonstrated is the one that simply cannot be represented otherwise, that is, it is the form of all cognition. Hence one does not ask why `2 + 2 = 4`, or why equality of angles in a triangle determines equality of sides, or why any given cause is followed by its effect, or why the truth of the premises makes the conclusion evident.

Every explanation that does not lead back to a relation of which no further why can be demanded stops at an assumed `qualitas occulta` (hidden quality). But every original force of nature is also of this kind. Every natural-scientific explanation must ultimately stop at such a force, and therefore at something wholly obscure. It must leave the inner essence of a stone just as unexplained as that of a human being; it can give no more account of the gravity, cohesion, chemical properties, and so on that the former exhibits than of the cognition and action of the latter.

Thus gravity, for example, is a `qualitas occulta` (hidden quality), for it can be thought away and therefore does not arise as something necessary from the form of cognition. This, by contrast, is the case with the law of inertia, since it follows from the law of causality; therefore a reduction to it is a completely sufficient explanation.

Two things, then, are absolutely inexplicable, that is, cannot be reduced to the relation expressed by the principle of sufficient reason. First, the principle of sufficient reason itself in all its four forms, because it is the principle of all explanation, that in relation to which alone explanation has meaning. Second, that which is not reached by this principle, but from which what is original in all appearances proceeds: the thing in itself, cognition of which is not at all subject to the principle of sufficient reason.

This latter must remain wholly unintelligible here, since it can first become intelligible through the following book, in which we will also resume this consideration of the possible achievements of the sciences. But at the point where natural science, indeed every science, leaves things standing, because not only its explanation of them, but even the principle of this explanation, the principle of sufficient reason, does not lead beyond this point: there philosophy really takes things up again and considers them in its own way, wholly different from that of science.

In the essay on the principle of sufficient reason, §51, I showed how, in the different sciences, one or another form of that principle is the chief guiding thread. In fact, perhaps the most fitting division of the sciences could be made according to this. But every explanation given along that guiding thread is, as stated, always only relative. It explains things in relation to one another, but always leaves something unexplained that it already presupposes.

In mathematics, for example, this is space and time; in mechanics, physics, and chemistry, matter, qualities, original forces, and laws of nature; in botany and zoology, the difference of species and life itself; in history, the human race with all its peculiarities of thinking and willing; and in all of them, the principle of sufficient reason in the form applicable each time.

What is peculiar to *philosophy* is that it presupposes nothing whatever as known. Everything is equally foreign to it and a problem: not only the relations of appearances, but these appearances themselves, and indeed the principle of sufficient reason itself, to which the other sciences are satisfied to reduce everything. In philosophy nothing would be gained by such a reduction, since one member of the series is as foreign to it as another; moreover, that kind of connection itself is just as much a problem for philosophy as what is connected by it, and this again remains a problem after the connection has been shown just as much as before.

For, as I have said, precisely what the sciences presuppose and lay at the basis and limit of their explanations is the real problem of philosophy. In this respect philosophy begins where the sciences stop. Proofs cannot be its foundation, for they derive unknown propositions from known ones; but for philosophy everything is equally unknown and foreign.

There can be no proposition in consequence of which the world with all its appearances would first exist. Therefore a philosophy cannot, as Spinoza wished, be demonstratively derived `ex firmis principiis` (from firm principles). Philosophy is also the most universal knowledge; its chief propositions therefore cannot be consequences of another knowledge still more universal.

The principle of contradiction merely establishes the agreement of concepts; it does not itself give concepts. The principle of sufficient reason explains connections among appearances, not the appearances themselves. Therefore philosophy cannot aim at seeking a `causa efficiens` (efficient cause) or a `causa finalis` (final cause) of the whole world. The present philosophy, at least, in no way asks *from where* or *for what purpose* the world exists, but only *what* the world is.

Here the why is subordinate to the what. For the why already belongs to the world, since it arises only through the form of the world's appearance, the principle of sufficient reason, and has meaning and validity only insofar as it does so. One might indeed say that everyone knows what the world is without further help, since each person is himself the subject of cognition whose representation it is; and this would be true to that extent.

But that cognition is intuitive; it is `in concreto` (in the concrete). To reproduce it `in abstracto` (in the abstract), to raise successive, changing intuition, and in general everything that the broad concept *feeling* includes and designates merely negatively as what is not abstract, distinct knowledge, precisely into such knowledge, into lasting knowledge: this is the task of philosophy.

Accordingly, philosophy must be a statement `in abstracto` of the essence of the whole world, of the whole as well as of all its parts. Yet in order not to lose itself in an endless multitude of individual judgments, it must use abstraction and think everything individual in the universal, and its differences again in the universal. It will therefore partly separate and partly unite, in order to hand over to knowledge, gathered into a few abstract concepts, all the manifoldness of the world in general according to its essence.

Through those concepts in which philosophy fixes the essence of the world, however, the wholly individual must be cognized just as much as the universal; cognition of both must therefore be connected with the greatest exactness. Hence the capacity for philosophy consists precisely in what Plato placed it in: recognizing the one in the many and the many in the one.

Philosophy will accordingly be a sum of very universal judgments whose ground of knowing is immediately the world itself in its entirety, excluding nothing: thus everything found in human consciousness. It will be *a complete repetition, as it were a reflection, of the world in abstract concepts*. This is possible only by uniting what is essentially identical in *one* concept and separating what is different into another.

Bacon of Verulam already set this task for philosophy when he said:

> `ea demum vera est philosophia, quae mundi ipsius voces fidelissime reddit, et veluti dictante mundo conscripta est, et nihil aliud est, quam ejusdem simulacrum et reflectio, neque addit quidquam de proprio, sed tantum iterat et resonat. (de augm. scient., L. 2, c. 13).`
> Translation: That alone is true philosophy which most faithfully renders the voices of the world itself, and is written as if from the world's dictation; it is nothing other than the world's image and reflection, adding nothing of its own, but only repeating and echoing.

We take this, however, in a broader sense than Bacon could then have imagined.

The agreement that all sides and parts of the world have with one another, precisely because they belong to one whole, must also be found again in that abstract image of the world. Accordingly, in that sum of judgments, one could in a certain sense be derived from another, and indeed always reciprocally. But for this they must first exist, and thus must first be set up as immediately grounded by cognition of the world `in concreto`; all the more so because every immediate grounding is more certain than a mediated one.

Their harmony with one another, by virtue of which they even flow together into the unity of *one* thought, and which springs from the harmony and unity of the intuitive world itself, their common ground of knowing, will therefore not be used as the first thing for grounding them, but will only be added as confirmation of their truth. This task itself can become completely clear only through its solution. [^3]

[^2]: Spinoza, who always boasts of proceeding `more geometrico` (in the geometric manner), in fact did this even more than he himself knew. But he obtains the intended result, already certain for him beforehand, only by arbitrarily taking self-made concepts, such as `substantia` (substance), `causa sui` (cause of itself), and so on, as his starting point, and by allowing himself in proof all those arbitrarinesses for which the nature of wide concept-spheres gives convenient opportunity.
