# §. 31.

Before proceeding, however, the following very essential remark is still needed. I hope that in the preceding book I succeeded in producing the conviction that what in Kant's philosophy is called the *thing in itself*, and appears there as such a significant but obscure and paradoxical doctrine, especially because of the way Kant introduced it, namely by inference from the grounded to the ground, so that it was found to be a stumbling block, indeed the weak side of his philosophy, is, when reached by the entirely different path we have taken, nothing other than the *will*, in the expanded and determinate sphere of this concept that has been specified.

I further hope that, after what has been set out, no one will hesitate to recognize, in the determinate grades of the objectivation of that will which constitutes the in-itself of the world, what Plato called the *eternal Ideas*, or the unchanging forms (ειδῆ). These have been acknowledged as the chief, but at the same time the darkest and most paradoxical dogma of his doctrine, and have been an object of reflection, dispute, mockery, and reverence for so many minds of different temperaments through a series of centuries.

If, then, for us the will is the *thing in itself*, and the Idea is the immediate objecthood of that will at a determinate grade, we find Kant's thing in itself and Plato's Idea, which alone is for him οντως ον (that which truly is), these two great obscure paradoxes of the two greatest philosophers of the West, to be not identical, but very closely related, distinguished by only a single determination. Indeed, precisely because, despite all their inner agreement and kinship, they sound so very different through the extraordinarily different individualities of their authors, these two great paradoxes are the best mutual commentary on each other. They are like two very different roads that lead to *one* goal.

This can be made clear in a few words. What Kant says, in essentials, is the following: "Time, space, and causality are not determinations of the thing in itself; rather, they belong only to its appearance, since they are nothing but forms of our cognition. But since all multiplicity and all coming-to-be and passing-away are possible only through time, space, and causality, it follows that these too attach only to appearance, and by no means to the thing in itself. Since our cognition, however, is conditioned by these forms, all experience is cognition only of appearance, not of the thing in itself. Therefore its laws also cannot be made valid for the thing in itself. Even our own I is included in what has been said, and we know it only as appearance, not according to what it may be in itself." This, in the important respect under consideration, is the meaning and content of Kant's doctrine.

Plato, however, says: "The things of this world, which our senses perceive, have no true being at all: *they are always becoming, but never are*. They have only a relative being; all of them are only in and through their relation to one another. Therefore their whole existence can just as well be called a non-being. Consequently they are also not objects of genuine cognition (επιστημη), for such cognition can exist only of what is in and for itself and always in the same way. They, by contrast, are only the object of an opinion occasioned by sensation (δοξα μετ' αισϑησεως αλογον).

"So long as we are restricted to their perception, we are like people who sit in a dark cave, so firmly bound that they cannot even turn their heads, and who see nothing, by the light of a fire burning behind them, except on the wall opposite them the shadow-images of real things carried past between them and the fire; even of one another, indeed each of himself, they see only the shadows on that wall. Their wisdom, however, would consist in predicting the sequence of those shadows learned from experience.

"By contrast, what alone can be called truly being (οντως ον), because it *always is, but never becomes or passes away*, are the real archetypes of those shadow-images: the eternal Ideas, the original forms of all things. *No multiplicity* belongs to them, for each is by its essence only one, since it is the archetype itself whose copies, or shadows, are all individual, transient things of the same kind and bearing the same name. Nor does *coming-to-be and passing-away* belong to them, for they truly are, but never become or perish like their vanishing copies." In these two negative determinations, however, it is necessarily presupposed that time, space, and causality have no meaning or validity for them, and that they do not exist in these forms.

"Of them alone, therefore, there is genuine cognition, since the object of such cognition can only be what always and in every respect is, and therefore is in itself; not what is, but also again is not, depending on how one looks at it." This is Plato's doctrine.

It is obvious, and needs no further proof, that the inner meaning of both doctrines is entirely the same: both declare the visible world to be an appearance that is in itself nothing, and has meaning and borrowed reality only through what expresses itself in it, which for one is the thing in itself and for the other the Idea. According to both doctrines, however, all forms of that appearance, even the most universal and essential, are entirely foreign to this latter, truly existing reality.

In order to deny these forms, Kant grasped them immediately in abstract expressions, and directly denied time, space, and causality, as mere forms of appearance, of the thing in itself. Plato, by contrast, did not reach the highest expression, and denied those forms of his Ideas only indirectly, by denying of the Ideas what is possible only through those forms, namely multiplicity of what is homogeneous, coming-to-be, and passing-away.

For completeness, however, I will make that remarkable and important agreement intuitive through an example. Suppose an animal stands before us in full activity of life. Plato will say: "This animal has no true existence, but only an apparent one, a constant becoming, a relative existence that can just as well be called a non-being as a being. Truly existing is only the Idea that is imaged in that animal, or the animal in itself, *αυτο το ϑηϱιον* (the animal itself), which depends on nothing, but is in and for itself, *ϰαϑ' ἑαυτο, αει ὡς αυτω* (in itself, always as itself), not having come to be, not ending, but always in the same way, *αει ον ϰαι μηδεποτε ουτε γιγνομενον, ουτε απολλυμενον* (always being and never either coming to be or passing away).

"Insofar as we now recognize its Idea in this animal, it is entirely indifferent and without significance whether we have this animal before us now or its ancestor that lived a thousand years ago; whether it is here or in a distant country; whether it presents itself in this or that manner, posture, or action; whether, finally, it is this individual or any other individual of its kind. All this is nothing and concerns only appearance. The Idea of the animal alone has true being and is the object of actual cognition." So Plato.

Kant would say something like this: "This animal is an appearance in time, space, and causality, all of which are the `a priori` (prior to experience) conditions of the possibility of experience lying in our faculty of cognition, not determinations of the thing in itself. Therefore this animal, as we perceive it at this determinate time, in this given place, as an individual that has come to be in the connection of experience, that is, in the chain of causes and effects, and is just as necessarily perishable, is not a thing in itself, but an appearance valid only in relation to our cognition. To know it according to what it may be in itself, and therefore independently of all determinations lying in time, space, and causality, would require a different mode of cognition from the only one possible to us through senses and understanding."

To bring Kant's expression still closer to the Platonic one, one could also say: time, space, and causality are that arrangement of our intellect by virtue of which the one being of every kind that alone truly exists presents itself to us as a multiplicity of homogeneous beings, constantly arising anew and passing away, in endless succession. The apprehension of things by means of and in accordance with that arrangement is the *immanent* apprehension. By contrast, the apprehension that becomes conscious of how matters stand with this arrangement is the *transcendental* apprehension. One receives the latter `in abstracto` (in the abstract) through the *Critique of Pure Reason*; but exceptionally it can also arise intuitively. This last point is my addition, which I am now trying to explain through the present third book.

If Kant's doctrine had ever been understood, if since Kant Plato had really been understood and grasped, if people had faithfully and earnestly thought through the inner meaning and content of the doctrines of both great masters, instead of tossing around the technical expressions of one and parodying the style of the other, then it could not have failed that people would long ago have found how greatly the two great sages agree, and that the pure meaning, the goal of both doctrines, is entirely the same.

Not only would people then not have constantly compared Plato with Leibniz, on whom his spirit certainly did not rest, or even with a certain well-known gentleman still living,[^1] as if they wished to mock the shades of the great thinker of antiquity; in general, they would then have got much further than they have. Or rather, they would not have slipped so shamefully far back as they have in these last forty years. They would not have let themselves be led around by the nose today by this windbag, tomorrow by another, and they would not have opened the nineteenth century, which announced itself so significantly in Germany, with philosophical farces performed over Kant's grave, as the ancients sometimes performed games at the funeral rites of their own, under the justified mockery of other nations, since nothing of this sort suits the serious and even stiff Germans least of all.

But so small is the real public of genuine philosophers that even the students who understand them are brought by the centuries only sparingly:

> Εισι δη ναϱϑηϰοφοϱοι μεν πολλοι, βαϰχοι δε γε πανϱοι.
> `Thyrsigeri quidem multi, Bacchi vero pauci.`
> Translation: There are many thyrsus-bearers, but few Bacchants.

> Η ατιμια φιλοσοφια διϰ ταυτα πϱοδπεπτωϰεν, ὁτι ου ϰατ' αξιαν αυτης ἁπτονται ου γαϱ νοϑους εδει ἁπτεσϑαι, αλλα γνησιους.
> `(Eam ob rem philosophia in infamiam incidit; quod non pro dignitate ipsam attingunt: neque enim a spuriis, sed a legitimis erat attrectanda.) Plat.`
> Translation: Philosophy has fallen into disrepute for this reason, that people do not take hold of it according to its dignity; for it should not be handled by illegitimate children, but by legitimate ones. Plato.

People followed the words, the words: "representations `a priori`, forms of intuition and thought conscious independently of experience, original concepts of the pure understanding," and so on. They then asked whether Plato's Ideas, which are supposed also to be original concepts and also memories from an intuition of truly existing things prior to life, might perhaps be the same as Kant's forms of intuition and thought, which lie `a priori` in our consciousness.

These two wholly heterogeneous doctrines, the Kantian doctrine of the forms that restrict the cognition of the individual to appearance, and the Platonic doctrine of the Ideas, whose cognition expressly denies precisely those forms, doctrines that are to this extent diametrically opposed, were carefully compared because their expressions somewhat resemble each other. People consulted and disputed about their sameness, then finally found that they were not the same after all, and concluded that Plato's doctrine of Ideas and Kant's critique of reason had no agreement at all.[^2] But enough of this.

[^2]: See, for example, *Immanuel Kant, A Memorial* by F. Bouterweck, p. 49, and Buhle's *History of Philosophy*, vol. 6, pp. 802-815 and 823.
