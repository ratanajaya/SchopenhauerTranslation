# §. 13.

All these considerations, both of the use and of the disadvantage of applying reason, are meant to make clear that although abstract knowledge is the reflection of intuitive representation and is grounded on it, it by no means fits it so congruently that it could everywhere take its place. Rather, it never corresponds to it with complete exactness. Therefore, as we have seen, many human activities can be accomplished only with the help of reason and deliberate procedure, while some are better accomplished without their application.

This very incongruence between intuitive and abstract cognition, by virtue of which the latter always approaches the former only as mosaic work approaches painting, is also the ground of a very remarkable phenomenon. Like reason itself, this phenomenon belongs exclusively to human nature, yet all the explanations of it that have been attempted again and again have remained inadequate. I mean *laughter*.

Because of its origin, we cannot avoid discussing it here, although it again interrupts our course. *Laughter* always arises from nothing other than the suddenly perceived incongruence between a concept and the real objects that had been thought through it in some relation; and laughter itself is simply the expression of this incongruence.

It often emerges when two or more real objects are thought through *one* concept and the concept's identity is transferred to them; then their complete difference in other respects makes it striking that the concept fitted them only from a one-sided point of view. Just as often, however, it is a single real object whose incongruence with the concept under which, in one respect, it has rightly been subsumed suddenly becomes felt.

Now the more correct the subsumption of such actual things under the concept is on the one hand, and the greater and more glaring their unsuitability to it is on the other, the stronger the effect of the ludicrous that springs from this contrast. All laughter, then, arises on the occasion of a paradoxical and therefore unexpected subsumption, whether this is expressed in words or in deeds. This, in brief, is the correct explanation of the ludicrous.

I will not linger here by telling anecdotes as examples in order to illustrate my explanation; for it is so simple and comprehensible that it does not need them, and any ludicrous thing the reader remembers is equally suitable as evidence for it. But our explanation is both confirmed and clarified by unfolding the two kinds of the ludicrous into which it divides, and which follow precisely from that explanation.

Either two or more very different real objects, intuitive representations, have preceded in cognition, and one has arbitrarily identified them through the unity of a concept that includes both: this kind of the ludicrous is called wit. Or, conversely, the concept is first present in cognition, and one now passes from it to reality and to acting upon reality, to action. Objects that are otherwise fundamentally different, but are all thought in that concept, are now regarded and treated in the same way, until their great difference in other respects emerges to the surprise and astonishment of the acting person: this kind of the ludicrous is called *foolishness*.

Accordingly, everything ludicrous is either a witty idea or a foolish action, depending on whether one has moved from the discrepancy of the objects to the identity of the concept, or conversely. The former is always voluntary; the latter always involuntary and forced on one from outside.

To reverse this starting point in appearance, and to mask wit as foolishness, is the art of the court fool and the buffoon. Such a person, well aware of the diversity of the objects, unites them with concealed wit under one concept; then, starting from that concept, he receives from the subsequently discovered diversity of the objects the very surprise he had prepared for himself.

From this short but sufficient theory of the ludicrous it follows that, setting aside this last case of the joker, wit must always show itself in words, while foolishness usually shows itself in actions. It can also show itself in words, however, when it merely states its intention instead of actually carrying it out, or when it expresses itself in mere judgments and opinions.

*Pedantry* also belongs to foolishness. It arises from having little confidence in one's own understanding, and therefore not wanting to leave it to the understanding to recognize immediately what is right in the individual case. One therefore places the understanding entirely under the guardianship of reason and wants to make use of reason everywhere: that is, always to proceed from general concepts, rules, and maxims, and to hold to them exactly, in life, in art, and even in ethical conduct.

Hence the clinging to form, manner, expression, and word that is peculiar to pedantry; these take the place of the essence of the matter. Soon the incongruence of the concept with reality shows itself. It becomes clear that the concept never descends to the individual case, and that its universality and rigid determinacy can never fit exactly the fine nuances and manifold modifications of actuality.

The pedant, therefore, with his general maxims, almost always comes up short in life; he shows himself imprudent, tasteless, and useless. In art, for which the concept is unfruitful, he produces lifeless, stiff, mannered monstrosities. Even in ethical respects, the resolve to act rightly or nobly cannot everywhere be carried out according to abstract maxims. For in many cases the infinitely finely nuanced character of the circumstances requires a choice of the right thing that proceeds immediately from character.

The application of merely abstract maxims partly gives false results because they fit only halfway, and partly cannot be carried through because they are foreign to the individual character of the acting person, and this character can never wholly deny itself. Inconsistencies then follow.

We cannot entirely acquit *Kant* of the charge of having occasioned moral pedantry, insofar as he makes it a condition of the moral worth of an action that it be done from purely rational abstract maxims, without any inclination or momentary impulse. This charge is also the meaning of Schiller's epigram entitled "Scruples of Conscience."

When, especially in political matters, people speak of doctrinaires, theorists, scholars, and so on, what they mean are pedants: people who know things well `in abstracto` (in the abstract), but not `in concreto` (in the concrete). Abstraction consists in thinking away the closer determinations; yet precisely on these a very great deal depends in practice.

To complete the theory, a spurious kind of wit still has to be mentioned: wordplay, the `calembourg` (pun), to which ambiguity, `l'equivoque` (equivocation), whose chief use is the obscene joke, can also be added.

Just as wit forces two very different real objects under one concept, so wordplay, by making use of accident, brings two different concepts under one word. The same contrast arises again, but much more weakly and superficially, because it springs not from the essence of things but from the accident of naming. In wit, the identity is in the concept and the difference is in actuality; in wordplay, however, the difference is in the concepts and the identity in actuality, since the wording belongs to actuality.

It would be only a somewhat too elaborate comparison if one said that wordplay stands to wit as the parabola of the upper inverted cone stands to that of the lower one. But the misunderstanding of the word, or the `quid pro quo` (one thing for another), is the involuntary `calembourg` (pun), and stands to it just as foolishness stands to wit. For this reason, the hard of hearing person, just like the fool, must often provide material for laughter, and bad comedy writers use the former instead of the latter in order to provoke laughter.
