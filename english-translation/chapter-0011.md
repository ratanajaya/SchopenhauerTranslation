# §. 9.

Concepts form a peculiar class, different `toto genere` (in kind altogether) from the intuitive representations considered so far, and present only in the human mind. We can therefore never obtain an intuitive, properly evident cognition of their essence, but only an abstract and discursive one.

It would therefore be absurd to demand that they be demonstrated in experience, if by experience one understands the real external world, which is precisely intuitive representation, or that they be brought before the eyes, or before the imagination, like intuitive objects. They can only be thought, not intuited; and only the effects that the human being produces through them are objects of genuine experience. Such effects are language, deliberate and methodical action, and science; then what results from all of these.

Clearly speech, as an object of outer experience, is nothing other than a very perfect telegraph, communicating arbitrary signs with the greatest speed and the finest nuance. But what do these signs mean? How are they interpreted? While another person speaks, do we perhaps at once translate his speech into images of the imagination, which flash past us, move, connect, transform, and paint themselves out in accordance with the incoming words and their grammatical inflections? What a tumult there would be in our head while listening to a speech or reading a book!

That is not at all how it happens. The meaning of speech is immediately apprehended, grasped exactly and determinately, without phantasms as a rule mixing themselves in. It is reason speaking to reason, remaining within its own domain; and what it communicates and receives are abstract concepts, non-intuitive representations that, once formed and relatively few in number, nevertheless comprise, contain, and represent all the innumerable objects of the actual world.

From this alone it is explicable that no animal can ever speak and apprehend speech, although it has the organs of language and also the intuitive representations in common with us. But precisely because words designate that wholly peculiar class of representations whose subjective correlate is reason, they are without sense and meaning for the animal.

Thus language, like every other appearance that we ascribe to reason, and like everything that distinguishes the human being from the animal, is to be explained from this one simple source: concepts, the abstract, non-intuitive, universal representations that are not individual in time and space.

Only in particular cases do we pass from concepts to intuition, forming phantasms for ourselves as intuitive *representatives of concepts*, to which, however, they are never adequate. These have been discussed especially in the essay on the principle of sufficient reason, §. 28; therefore I will not repeat the same thing here. What is said there should be compared with what Hume says in the twelfth of his *Philosophical Essays*, p. 244, and what Herder says in the *Metacritique* (an otherwise bad book), part 1, p. 274. The Platonic Idea, made possible by the union of imagination and reason, constitutes the chief subject of the third book of the present work.

Although, then, concepts are fundamentally different from intuitive representations, they nevertheless stand in a necessary relation to them; without that relation they would be nothing, and it therefore constitutes their whole essence and existence. Reflection is necessarily a copy, a repetition, of the archetypal intuitive world, though a copy of a quite special kind, in a wholly heterogeneous material. For this reason concepts may quite fittingly be called representations of representations.

Here too the principle of sufficient reason has a special form. And just as the form under which it governs a class of representations always really makes up and exhausts the whole essence of that class, insofar as they are representations, so that, as we have seen, time is through and through succession and nothing else, space through and through position and nothing else, matter through and through causality and nothing else: so too the whole essence of concepts, or of the class of abstract representations, consists solely in the relation expressed in them by the principle of sufficient reason.

Since this relation is the relation to the ground of knowing, the abstract representation has its entire essence solely and exclusively in its relation to another representation that is its ground of knowing. This may indeed at first be another concept, or abstract representation; and this too may have only another such abstract ground of knowing. But this cannot go on to infinity. In the end the series of grounds of knowing must close with a concept that has its ground in intuitive cognition. For the whole world of reflection rests on the intuitive world as its ground of knowing.

The class of abstract representations is therefore distinguished from the others by this: in the others, the principle of sufficient reason always demands only a relation to another representation of the *same* class; in abstract representations, however, it finally demands a relation to a representation from a *different* class.

Those concepts that, as just indicated, relate to intuitive cognition not immediately but only through the mediation of one or even several other concepts have been called, in the narrower sense, `abstracta` (abstracts); by contrast, those that have their ground immediately in the intuitive world have been called `concreta` (concretes). This latter designation, however, fits the concepts it designates only quite improperly, since they too are still always `abstracta` and by no means intuitive representations.

Those names arose only from a very indistinct consciousness of the difference meant by them; nevertheless, with the interpretation given here, they may remain. Examples of the first kind, therefore `abstracta` in the eminent sense, are concepts such as "relation," "virtue," "investigation," "beginning," and so on. Examples of the latter kind, or of what are improperly called `concreta`, are the concepts "human being," "stone," "horse," and so on. If it were not a somewhat too pictorial comparison, and thereby one that falls into playfulness, one could very aptly call the latter the ground floor, and the former the upper stories, of the building of reflection. That a concept comprehends many things under itself, that is, that many intuitive representations, or even again many abstract representations, stand in relation to it as their ground of knowing, that is, are thought through it, is not, as is usually stated, an essential property of the concept, but only a derived and secondary one. Indeed, it need not always exist in actuality, though it must always exist as a possibility.

That property flows from the fact that the concept is the representation of a representation, that is, that it has its whole essence solely in its relation to another representation. But since it is not this representation itself, and since the latter usually belongs to an entirely different class of representations, namely to intuitive representations, it can have temporal, spatial, and other determinations, and in general many further relations, that are not thought along with the concept. Therefore several representations that differ in what is inessential can be thought through the same concept, that is, can be subsumed under it.

But this validity for several things is not an essential property of the concept; it is only accidental. There can therefore be concepts through which only one single real object is thought, yet which are still abstract and universal, and by no means individual and intuitive representations. Such is, for example, the concept someone has of a particular city that he knows only from geography. Although only this one city is thought through it, several cities differing in some respects would still be possible to all of which it would apply.

Thus a concept does not have universality because it has been abstracted from several objects; rather, conversely, because universality, that is, non-determination of the individual, is essential to it as an abstract representation of reason, different things can be thought through the same concept.

From what has been said it follows that every concept, precisely because it is an abstract and not an intuitive representation, and therefore not determined through and through, has what is called an extension or sphere, even in the case where only one single real object exists that corresponds to it.

Now we find throughout that the sphere of every concept has something in common with the spheres of others: that is, in it there is thought in part the same thing that is thought in those others, and in them again in part the same thing that is thought in the first; although, if they are really different concepts, each, or at least one of the two, contains something that the other does not. Every subject stands in this relation to its predicate. To recognize this relation is called *judging*.

The representation of those spheres by spatial figures is an exceedingly fortunate idea. Gottfried Plouquet was probably the first to have it; he used squares for this purpose. Lambert, though after him, still used mere lines that he placed beneath one another. Euler was the first to carry it out completely with circles.

I cannot say on what this very exact analogy between the relations of concepts and those of spatial figures ultimately rests. Meanwhile, it is a very favorable circumstance for logic that all relations of concepts, even according to their possibility, that is, `a priori` (prior to experience), can be intuitively represented by such figures, in the following way:

1. The spheres of two concepts are wholly equal: for example, the concept of necessity and the concept of consequent from a given ground; likewise that of `Ruminantia` and `Bisulca` (ruminants and split-hoofed animals); also that of vertebrates and red-blooded animals, although something could be objected here because of the annelids. These are coextensive concepts. A single circle then represents them, signifying both one and the other.

2. The sphere of one concept wholly includes that of another:

3. One sphere includes two or more that exclude one another and at the same time fill the sphere:

4. Two spheres each include a part of the other:

5. Two spheres lie within a third, though they do not fill it:

This last case holds for all concepts whose spheres have no immediate community, since a third, though often very wide, will always include both.

All combinations of concepts may be reducible to these cases, and the entire doctrine of judgments, their conversion, contraposition, reciprocation, and disjunction (the latter according to the third figure), can be derived from them. So too can the properties of judgments on which Kant founded the alleged categories of the understanding, with the exception, however, of the hypothetical form, which is no longer a combination of mere concepts but of judgments; and then with the exception of modality, about which, as about every property of judgments that is laid at the basis of the categories, the appendix gives detailed account.

With regard to the possible concept-combinations indicated above, it remains only to note that they can also be variously combined with one another, for example, the fourth figure with the second. Only when a sphere that wholly or partly contains another is itself again wholly included by a third do these together represent the syllogism in the first figure: that is, the combination of judgments through which it is cognized that a concept wholly or partly contained in another is also contained in a third that in turn contains this other. Or they represent the converse of this, negation; its pictorial representation can naturally consist only in two connected spheres not lying within a third. If many spheres enclose one another in this way, long chains of syllogisms arise.

This schematism of concepts, which has already been worked out fairly well in several textbooks, can be made the basis of the doctrine of judgments as well as of the whole of syllogistic, whereby the presentation of both becomes very easy and simple. For all their rules can be understood, derived, and explained from it according to their origin.

It is not necessary, however, to load these rules upon the memory, since logic can never be of practical use, but only of theoretical interest for philosophy. For although one might say that logic stands to rational thinking as thoroughbass stands to music, and also, if we take it less exactly, as ethics stands to virtue, or aesthetics to art, one must consider in reply that no artist has yet become one through the study of aesthetics, nor has a noble character become so through the study of ethics; that long before Rameau people composed correctly and beautifully; and also that one does not need to possess thoroughbass in order to notice disharmonies. Just as little does one need to know logic in order not to be deceived by fallacies.

Yet it must be admitted that, even if not for judgment, thoroughbass is nevertheless of great use for the exercise of musical composition. And aesthetics and even ethics may also, although to a much lesser degree, have some use for exercise, though chiefly a negative one; therefore not all practical value is to be denied them. But not even this much can be praised in logic. For it is merely knowledge `in abstracto` (in the abstract) of what everyone knows `in concreto` (in the concrete). Hence, just as little as one needs it in order not to assent to a false piece of reasoning, just as little does one call its rules to help in order to produce a correct one; and even the most learned logician sets them entirely aside in his actual thinking.

This is explained as follows. Every science consists of a system of universal, hence abstract, truths, laws, and rules with regard to some kind of object. The individual case that subsequently occurs under these is then determined each time according to that universal knowledge, which holds once for all, because such application of the universal is infinitely easier than investigating the individual case that occurs from the beginning on its own account. For the universal abstract cognition once attained always lies closer to hand for us than the empirical investigation of the individual.

With logic, however, it is just the reverse. It is the universal knowledge of reason's procedure, known through reason's self-observation and abstraction from all content, and expressed in the form of rules. But that procedure is necessary and essential to reason; therefore reason will deviate from it in no case, so long as it is left to itself.

It is therefore easier and safer to let reason proceed in accordance with its essence in each particular case than to hold before it knowledge first abstracted from that procedure, in the form of an alien law given from outside. It is easier because, although in all other sciences the universal rule lies closer to us than the investigation of the individual case alone and by itself, conversely, in the use of reason, the procedure of reason required in the given case always lies closer to us than the universal rule abstracted from it, since what thinks in us is itself that reason.

It is safer because an error can much more easily occur in such abstract knowledge, or in its application, than a procedure of reason can arise that would run contrary to reason's essence and nature. From this comes the strange fact that, while in other sciences one tests the truth of the individual case by the rule, in logic, conversely, the rule must always be tested by the individual case; and even the most practiced logician, if he notices that in an individual case he infers otherwise than a rule states, will always sooner look for an error in the rule than in the inference he has actually made.

To want to make practical use of logic would therefore mean wanting first to derive from universal rules, with unspeakable effort, what in the individual case is immediately conscious to us with the greatest certainty. It would be just as if one wanted first to consult mechanics for one's movements and physiology for digestion; and whoever learns logic for practical purposes is like someone who wants to train a beaver to build its lodge.

Although logic is thus without practical use, it must nonetheless be retained, because it has philosophical interest as a special knowledge of the organization and action of reason. As a closed discipline, standing on its own, complete in itself, rounded off and perfectly certain, it is entitled to be treated scientifically on its own and independently of everything else, and likewise to be taught at universities. But it receives its real value only in connection with the whole of philosophy, in the consideration of cognition, and specifically of rational or abstract cognition.

Accordingly, its presentation should not so much have the form of a science directed toward practice, nor contain merely naked rules laid down for the correct conversion of judgments, inference, and so forth. Rather, it should be directed more toward the cognition of the essence of reason and of the concept, and toward a detailed consideration of the principle of sufficient reason of knowing. For logic is a mere paraphrase of this principle, and indeed really only for the case in which the ground that gives truth to judgments is not empirical or metaphysical, but logical or metalogical.

Alongside the principle of sufficient reason of knowing, the three other fundamental laws of thought that are so closely related to it, or judgments of metalogical truth, are therefore to be presented; from these the whole technique of reason then gradually grows. The essence of thinking proper, that is, of judging and inferring, is to be represented from the connection of the concept-spheres according to the spatial schema in the way indicated above, and from this all the rules of judging and inferring are to be derived by construction.

The only practical use that can be made of logic is this: in disputation, one demonstrates to one's opponent, by naming them technically, not so much his actual false inferences as his intentional sophisms. By pushing back this practical orientation and emphasizing the connection of logic with the whole of philosophy, as one chapter of it, knowledge of it should nevertheless not become rarer than it is now. For today everyone who does not want to remain crude in the main matter, and to be counted among the ignorant multitude sunk in dullness, must have studied speculative philosophy. This is because this nineteenth century is a philosophical one; by which it is not meant so much that it possesses philosophy, or that philosophy is dominant in it, as rather that it is ripe for philosophy and precisely for that reason urgently needs it. This is a sign of highly advanced cultivation, indeed a fixed point on the scale of the culture of the ages. [^2]

As little practical use as logic can have, it is nevertheless probably not to be denied that it was invented for practical purposes. I explain its origin to myself in the following way. When among the Eleatics, Megarians, and Sophists the pleasure in disputation developed more and more and gradually rose almost to an addiction, the confusion into which almost every disputation fell must soon have made them feel the necessity of a methodical procedure; and for guidance in this a scientific dialectic had to be sought.

The first thing that had to be noticed was that both contending parties always had to agree about some proposition to which the disputed points were to be traced back in disputation. The beginning of methodical procedure consisted in formally stating these commonly acknowledged propositions as such and placing them at the head of the inquiry. At first, however, these propositions concerned only the material aspect of the inquiry.

Soon it was perceived that, also in the manner in which one returned to the commonly acknowledged truth and sought to derive one's assertions from it, certain forms and laws were followed about which, even without previous agreement, people nevertheless never disagreed. From this it was seen that they must be the peculiar course of reason itself, lying in its own essence: the formal aspect of the inquiry.

Although this was not exposed to doubt and disagreement, some mind systematic to the point of pedantry nevertheless hit on the thought that it would look very fine, and would be the completion of methodical dialectic, if this formal element of all disputation too, this procedure of reason itself that is always law-governed, were likewise expressed in abstract propositions. These could then be placed, just like the commonly acknowledged propositions concerning the material aspect of the inquiry, at the head of the investigation as the fixed canon of disputation itself, to which one would always have to look back and appeal.

In this way, what had previously been followed as if by tacit agreement, or carried out as if instinctively, was now to be consciously acknowledged as law and formally expressed. Gradually, more or less complete expressions were found for logical principles, such as the principle of contradiction, of sufficient reason, and of excluded middle; the `dictum de omni et nullo` (the dictum concerning all and none); and then the more specific rules of syllogistic, such as `ex meris particularibus aut negativis nihil sequitur` (from merely particular or negative premises nothing follows), `a rationato ad rationem non valet consequentia` (from the consequent to the ground the inference is not valid), and so on.

That this was accomplished only slowly and with great effort, and that before Aristotle everything remained very incomplete, we see partly from the awkward and prolix manner in which logical truths are brought to light in some Platonic dialogues, but still better from what Sextus Empiricus reports to us about the disputes of the Megarians over the easiest and simplest logical laws, and the laborious way in which they brought such laws to clarity (`Sext. Emp. adv. Math. L. 8. p. 112 seqq.`). Aristotle, however, collected, ordered, and corrected what he found already at hand, and brought it to incomparably greater perfection.

If one observes in this way how the course of Greek culture prepared and brought about Aristotle's work, one will have little inclination to believe the claim of Persian writers, which *Jones*, very much taken with it, communicates to us: namely, that Callisthenes found a completed logic among the Indians and sent it to his uncle Aristotle (`Asiatic Researches`, vol. 4, p. 163).

It is easy to understand that in the sorrowful Middle Ages the Aristotelian logic had to be extremely welcome to the disputation-addicted spirit of the Scholastics, which, lacking all real knowledge, fed on formulas and words alone; that even in its Arabic mutilation it was eagerly seized upon and soon elevated to the center of all knowledge. Although its prestige has since fallen, it has nevertheless maintained down to our time the credit of being an independent, practical, and highly necessary science. Indeed, in our day Kantian philosophy, which actually took its cornerstone from logic, has again awakened a new interest in it, which in this respect, that is, as a means to cognition of the essence of reason, it certainly deserves.

Just as correct, strict syllogisms come about by considering exactly the relation of the concept-spheres, and only when one sphere lies exactly within another and this one again wholly within a third does one also acknowledge the first to be wholly contained in the third; so, by contrast, the *art of persuasion* rests on subjecting the relations of concept-spheres only to a superficial consideration, and then determining them one-sidedly according to one's aims. This happens chiefly by presenting the sphere of a concept under consideration, when it lies only partly within another sphere but partly also within an entirely different one, as lying wholly in the first or wholly in the second, according to the speaker's intention.

For example, when passion is being discussed, one may subsume it at will under the concept of the greatest strength, the mightiest agency in the world, or under the concept of unreason, and this under that of impotence and weakness. The same procedure can then be continued and applied anew to every concept to which the speech leads.

Almost always several others share in the sphere of a concept, each of which contains on its own territory a part of the domain of the first, while itself also comprising still more beyond that. Of these latter concept-spheres, however, one allows only the one under which one wants to subsume the first concept to be illuminated, while leaving the others unnoticed or keeping them concealed.

All arts of persuasion and all subtler sophisms really rest on this artifice; for the logical sophisms, such as the `mentiens`, `velatus`, `cornutus` (liar, veiled man, horned man), and so forth, are obviously too crude for actual use. Since I do not know that anyone has so far traced the essence of all sophistry and persuasion back to this ultimate ground of its possibility, and shown it in the peculiar constitution of concepts, that is, in reason's way of knowing, I will, since my presentation has led me to it, illustrate the matter, easy as it is to see, by a schema on the accompanying plate. This is meant to show how the concept-spheres variously intersect one another and thereby give arbitrary choice room to pass from any concept to this or that other one.

I only hope that the plate will not lead anyone to attach more importance to this small incidental discussion than it can have by its nature. For the explanatory example I have chosen the concept of *traveling*. Its sphere intersects with the territory of four others, from each of which the persuader can pass over at will. These again intersect with other spheres, some of them with two or more at once, through which the persuader arbitrarily takes his path, always as if it were the only one, and then finally arrives, according to his intention, at good or evil.

Only, in following the spheres, one must always keep the direction from the center (the given main concept) toward the periphery, and not go backward. Such a sophistical maneuver can be clothed in continuous speech, or also in the strict form of inference, depending on the weak side of the listener. At bottom, most scientific proofs, especially philosophical ones, are not much differently constituted. How else would it have been possible for so much, at different times, not only to be erroneously accepted (for error itself has another origin), but to be demonstrated and proved, and yet later found to be fundamentally false: for example, Leibniz-Wolffian philosophy, Ptolemaic astronomy, Stahlian chemistry, Newton's theory of colors, and so on and so on.
