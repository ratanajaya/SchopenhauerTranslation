# §. 19.

In the first book, with inward resistance, we declared our own body, like all the other objects of this intuitive world, to be mere representation of the knowing subject. Now it has become clear to us what distinguishes, in everyone's consciousness, the representation of one's own body from all others, though otherwise it is entirely like them. The distinction is this: the body also occurs in consciousness in an entirely different way, different `toto genere` (in kind altogether), which is designated by the word *will*. And precisely this double cognition that we have of our own body gives us the clarification about the body itself, about its acting and moving on motives, and also about its suffering through external influence; in a word, about what it is not as representation, but besides this, and therefore what it is *in itself*. This is the clarification we do not immediately have about the essence, acting, and suffering of all other real objects.

The knowing subject is an individual precisely through this special relation to one body, which, considered apart from that relation, is only a representation like all the rest. But the relation by virtue of which the knowing subject is an *individual* exists for just this reason only between it and a single one among all its representations. Therefore it is conscious of this one representation not merely as a representation, but at the same time in an entirely different way, namely as a will.

If, however, the subject abstracts from that special relation, from that double and wholly heterogeneous cognition of one and the same thing, then that one thing, the body, is a representation like all others. So, in order to orient itself here, the knowing individual must assume one of two things.

Either it must assume that what distinguishes that one representation lies merely in the fact that its cognition stands only to this one representation in this double relation; that insight into only this *one* intuitive object is open to it in two ways at once; and that this is to be explained not by a difference between this object and all others, but only by a difference between the relation of its cognition to this one object and the relation it has to all the others.

Or else it must assume that this one object is essentially different from all others, that it alone among all objects is at once will and representation, while the rest are mere representation, that is, mere phantoms; thus that its body is the only real individual in the world, that is, the only appearance of will and the only immediate object of the subject.

That the other objects, considered as mere *representations*, are like its body, that is, that like it they fill space, which itself can possibly exist only as representation, and also act in space as it does: this is indeed demonstrably certain from the law of causality, which is secure `a priori` (prior to experience) for representations and admits no effect without a cause. Yet apart from the fact that from an effect one may infer only a cause in general, not an identical cause, one still remains here within the field of mere representation, for which alone the law of causality holds and beyond which it can never lead.

Whether, however, the objects known to the individual only as representations are nevertheless, like his own body, appearances of a will: this, as was already stated in the previous book, is the real meaning of the question concerning the reality of the external world. To deny it is the meaning of *theoretical egoism*, which thereby takes all appearances except its own individual to be phantoms, just as practical egoism does precisely the same thing in a practical respect, namely by seeing and treating only its own person as truly real and all others as mere phantoms.

Theoretical egoism can indeed never be refuted by proofs. Still, in philosophy it has surely never been used otherwise than as a skeptical sophism, that is, for appearance's sake. As a serious conviction, by contrast, it could be found only in a madhouse; and as such it would need not so much a proof against it as a cure. We therefore will not concern ourselves further with it in this respect, but will regard it only as the last fortress of skepticism, which is always polemical.

Our cognition is always bound to individuality, and has its limitation precisely in this. It therefore necessarily brings with it that each person can only *be* one thing, but can *know* everything else; and this limitation is exactly what creates the need for philosophy. Since we, for just this reason, strive through philosophy to widen the limits of our cognition, we will regard the skeptical argument of theoretical egoism that confronts us here as a small border fortress. It is indeed forever unconquerable, but its garrison can also never come out of it; hence one may pass it by and leave it in the rear without danger.

Accordingly, the double cognition we now have of the essence and action of our own body, a cognition brought to clarity and given in two wholly heterogeneous ways, will be used from here on as a key to the essence of every appearance in nature. All objects that are not our own body, and are therefore not given to our consciousness in two ways but only as representations, we will judge by analogy with that body. We will therefore assume that, just as on one side they are representation, entirely as it is, and are homogeneous with it in that respect, so on the other side, if one sets aside their existence as representation of the subject, what then remains must, in its inner essence, be the same as what we call *will* in ourselves.

For what other kind of existence or reality could we attribute to the rest of the corporeal world? Where would we take the elements from which to compose such a reality? Apart from will and representation, nothing at all is known to us or even thinkable. If we want to attribute to the corporeal world, which stands there immediately only in our representation, the greatest reality known to us, then we give it the reality that each person's own body has for him; for this body is what is most real to everyone.

But if we now analyze the reality of this body and its actions, then, apart from the fact that it is our representation, we find nothing in it except the will. With this, even its reality is exhausted. We can therefore nowhere find some other kind of reality to attribute to the corporeal world.

If, then, the corporeal world is to be something more than merely our representation, we must say that apart from representation, and therefore in itself and according to its innermost essence, it is what we find immediately in ourselves as will. I say: according to its innermost essence. But first we must come to know this essence of the will more closely, so that we may know how to distinguish from it what does not belong to the will itself, but already to its appearance, which has many grades. Such, for example, is its being accompanied by cognition, and the determination by motives that is conditioned by cognition. As we will see in what follows, this belongs not to the essence of the will, but only to its distinct appearance as animal and human being.

Therefore, when I say that the force which drives the stone to the earth is, according to its essence, in itself and apart from all representation, will, one must not attribute to this proposition the absurd opinion that the stone moves according to a known motive, because will appears in this way in human beings.[^1]

Now, however, we want to show, ground, and develop in its whole scope, more fully and clearly, what has been presented up to this point only provisionally and in general terms.[^2]

[^1]: We will therefore by no means agree with Bacon of Verulam when he (`de augm. scient. L. 4 in fine`; On the Advancement of Learning, book 4, at the end) thinks that all mechanical and physical movements of bodies occur only after a preceding perception in those bodies, although an intimation of the truth gave rise even to this false proposition. The same is true of Kepler's claim, in his treatise `de planeta Martis` (On the Planet Mars), that the planets must have cognition in order to find their elliptical paths so accurately and to measure the speed of their movement so that the triangles within the area of their orbit always remain proportional to the time in which they traverse their base.
[^2]: On this, chapter 19 of the second volume.
