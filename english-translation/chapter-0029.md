# §. 27.

From all the preceding considerations about the forces of nature and their appearances, it has become clear how far explanation from causes can go, and where it must stop if it is not to fall into the foolish attempt to reduce the content of all appearances to their mere form, in which case nothing but form would remain in the end. We can now determine, in general terms, what is to be demanded of all aetiology.

Aetiology has to seek out the causes of all appearances in nature, that is, the circumstances under which they always occur. But then it must trace the many-shaped appearances that arise under manifold circumstances back to what is active in all appearance and presupposed in the cause: to original forces of nature. It must correctly distinguish whether a difference in appearance comes from a difference in the force, or only from a difference in the circumstances under which the force expresses itself. It must guard just as carefully against taking for the appearance of different forces what is only the expression of one and the same force under different circumstances, as against taking for expressions of one force what belongs originally to different forces.

This directly requires power of judgment. That is why so few people are able to extend insight in physics, although everyone can extend experience. Laziness and ignorance make people inclined to appeal too early to original forces. This appears, with an exaggeration that borders on irony, in the entities and quiddities of the Scholastics. Nothing could be further from my wish than to have favored their reintroduction.

Instead of giving a physical explanation, one may appeal to the objectivation of the will just as little as to the creative power of God. For physics demands causes; but the will is never a cause. Its relation to appearance is not at all according to the principle of sufficient reason. Rather, what in itself is will is, from another side, there as representation, that is, as appearance. As appearance it obeys the laws that make up the form of appearance. Thus every movement, for example, although it is always an appearance of will, must nevertheless have a cause from which it is to be explained with reference to a determinate time and place; that is, it is to be explained not in general, according to its inner essence, but as an *individual* appearance. This cause is mechanical in the case of a stone; it is a motive in the case of a human being's movement. But it can never be absent.

By contrast, the universal element, the common essence of all appearances of a determinate kind, that without whose presupposition explanation from causes would have neither sense nor meaning, is the universal natural force. In physics this must remain as `qualitas occulta` (occult quality), precisely because here aetiological explanation ends and metaphysical explanation begins.

The chain of causes and effects, however, is never broken by an original force to which one would have to appeal; nor does it run back to such a force as its first link. Rather, the nearest link in the chain, just as much as the most remote, already presupposes the original force, and without it could explain nothing. A series of causes and effects can be the appearance of the most diverse forces, whose successive entrance into visibility is guided by that series, as I explained above with the example of a metal machine. But the difference among these original forces, which cannot be derived from one another, in no way interrupts the unity of that chain of causes or the connection among all its links.

The aetiology of nature and the philosophy of nature never undermine one another. Rather, they go side by side, considering the same object from different points of view. Aetiology gives an account of the causes that necessarily brought about the individual appearance to be explained, and it points out, as the basis of all its explanations, the universal forces active in all these causes and effects. It determines these forces exactly, their number, their differences, and then all the effects in which each force appears differently according to the difference of circumstances, always in accordance with its own peculiar character, which it unfolds according to an infallible rule called a *law of nature*.

As soon as physics has completely achieved all this in every respect, it has reached its completion. Then no force in inorganic nature is still unknown, and no effect remains that has not been shown to be the appearance of one of those forces under determinate circumstances, according to a law of nature. Yet a law of nature remains nothing but the rule observed from nature, according to which nature proceeds every time, under determinate circumstances, as soon as they occur. One may therefore certainly define a law of nature as a universally expressed fact, `un fait généralisé` (a generalized fact); a complete exposition of all laws of nature would accordingly be only a complete register of facts.

The consideration of nature as a whole is then completed by *morphology*, which enumerates, compares, and orders all the enduring forms of organic nature. It has little to say about the cause of the entrance of individual beings, since in all of them this cause is generation, whose theory is a matter of its own, and in rare cases `generatio aequivoca` (spontaneous generation). Strictly speaking, however, this latter also includes the way in which all lower grades of the objecthood of the will, that is, physical and chemical appearances, emerge individually; and the statement of the conditions for this emergence is precisely the task of aetiology.

Philosophy, by contrast, everywhere, and so also in nature, considers only the universal. The original forces themselves are its object here, and in them it recognizes the different grades of the objectivation of the will, which is the inner essence, the in-itself, of this world. If philosophy abstracts from that will, it declares the world to be the mere representation of the subject.

But if aetiology, instead of preparing the way for philosophy and supplying application for its doctrines through evidence, rather thinks that its aim is to explain away all original forces, perhaps down to *one*, the most universal, for example impenetrability, which it imagines it understands from the ground up and to which it therefore tries forcibly to reduce all the others, then it removes its own foundation and can yield only error instead of truth. The content of nature is then displaced by form; everything is attributed to the circumstances acting upon things, and nothing to the inner essence of things. If this path really succeeded, then, as already said, a problem in arithmetic would finally solve the riddle of the world.

This is the path taken when, as already mentioned, every physiological effect is to be reduced to form and mixture, perhaps to electricity, electricity again to chemism, and chemism to mechanism. This last was, for example, the error of Descartes and all atomists, who reduced the movement of the heavenly bodies to the impact of a fluid, and qualities to the connection and shape of atoms, and who worked toward explaining all appearances of nature as mere phenomena of impenetrability and cohesion.

Although people have retreated from that view, the electrical, chemical, and mechanical physiologists of our own day are doing the same thing when they stubbornly try to explain all life and all functions of the organism from the "form and mixture" of its components. That the aim of physiological explanation is the reduction of organic life to the universal forces considered by physics is still stated in *Meckel's Archive for Physiology*, 1820, vol. 5, p. 185.

Lamarck too, in his *Zoological Philosophy*, vol. 2, chap. 3, declares life to be a mere effect of heat and electricity: `le calorique et la matière électrique suffisent parfaitement pour composer ensemble cette cause essentielle de la vie` (caloric and electrical matter are perfectly sufficient to compose together this essential cause of life) (p. 16). According to this, heat and electricity would really be the thing in itself, and the animal and plant world its appearance. The absurdity of this opinion stands out glaringly on p. 306 ff. of that work.

It is well known that in the most recent period all those views, so often exploded, have reappeared with renewed boldness. If one looks at them closely, they ultimately rest on the presupposition that the organism is only an aggregate of appearances of physical, chemical, and mechanical forces, which here, having accidentally come together, bring about the organism as a play of nature without further significance.

Accordingly, the organism of an animal or of a human being would, philosophically considered, not be the presentation of an Idea of its own, that is, not itself immediate objecthood of the will at a determinate higher grade. Rather, only those Ideas would appear in it which objectify the will in electricity, chemism, and mechanism. The organism would therefore have been blown together by the concurrence of these forces as accidentally as the shapes of human beings and animals in clouds or stalactites, and thus would in itself be no more interesting.

We will see immediately, however, to what extent that application of physical and chemical modes of explanation to the organism may still, within certain limits, be allowed and useful. For I will show that the vital force certainly uses and employs the forces of inorganic nature, but by no means consists of them, any more than the blacksmith consists of hammer and anvil. Thus not even the very simple life of plants will ever be explained from them, for instance from capillary force and endosmosis, much less animal life. The following consideration prepares the way for that rather difficult discussion.

According to all that has been said, natural science goes astray when it tries to reduce the higher grades of the objecthood of the will to lower ones, since the failure to recognize and the denial of original natural forces existing in their own right is just as mistaken as the groundless assumption of peculiar forces where there is only a special mode of appearance of forces already known. Kant is therefore right to say that it is absurd to hope for a Newton of the blade of grass, that is, for someone who would reduce the blade of grass to appearances of physical and chemical forces, of which it would then be an accidental concretion, a mere play of nature, in which no peculiar Idea appeared; that is, in which the will did not immediately reveal itself at a higher and special grade, but only as it does in the appearances of inorganic nature, and accidentally in this form.

The Scholastics, who would never have permitted anything of this kind, would have been quite right to say that it would be a complete denial of the `forma substantialis` (substantial form) and a degradation of it to `forma accidentalis` (accidental form). For Aristotle's `forma substantialis` designates exactly what I call the grade of the objectivation of the will in a thing.

On the other hand, it must not be overlooked that in all Ideas, that is, in all forces of inorganic nature and all forms of organic nature, it is *one and the same will* that reveals itself, that is, enters into the form of representation, into *objecthood*. Its unity must therefore also make itself known through an inner kinship among all its appearances.

At the higher grades of its objecthood, where the whole appearance is clearer, that is, in the plant and animal kingdoms, this kinship reveals itself through the universally pervasive analogy of all forms, the basic type that recurs in all appearances. For this reason it has become the guiding principle of the excellent zoological systems that came from the French in this century, and it is demonstrated most completely in comparative anatomy as `l'unité de plan, l'uniformité de l'élément anatomique` (the unity of plan, the uniformity of the anatomical element).

To discover this basic type was also a chief concern, or at least certainly the most praiseworthy effort, of the philosophers of nature of the Schellingian school, who even have some merit in this respect, although in many cases their hunt for analogies in nature degenerates into mere clever trifling. Rightly, however, they demonstrated that universal kinship and family resemblance even among the Ideas of inorganic nature, for example between electricity and magnetism, whose identity was later established, between chemical attraction and gravity, and the like.

They especially drew attention to the fact that *polarity*, that is, the division of one force into two qualitatively different, opposed activities that strive toward reunification, and that usually also reveals itself spatially by separating into opposed directions, is a basic type of almost all appearances of nature, from magnet and crystal to the human being. In China, however, this cognition has been current from the oldest times in the doctrine of the opposition of *Yin* and *Yang*.

Indeed, precisely because all things in the world are the objecthood of one and the same will, and therefore identical according to their inner essence, there must not only be that unmistakable analogy among them, and in everything less perfect there must already appear the trace, hint, and disposition of the next more perfect thing; but also, since all those forms nevertheless belong only to the world as *representation*, one may even assume that already in the most universal forms of representation, in this actual basic framework of the appearing world, namely in space and time, the basic type, the hint, the disposition of everything that fills the forms, can be found and demonstrated.

It seems to have been a dim cognition of this that gave rise to the Kabbalah and to the whole mathematical philosophy of the Pythagoreans, as well as of the Chinese in the *I Ching*. And in that Schellingian school too, among its many efforts to bring to light the analogy among all appearances of nature, we also find some attempts, although unsuccessful ones, to derive laws of nature from the mere laws of space and time. Still, one cannot know how far some genius may one day realize both efforts.

Although the distinction between appearance and thing in itself must never be lost from sight, and therefore the identity of the will objectified in all Ideas must never, because it has determinate grades of its objecthood, be distorted into an identity of the individual Ideas themselves in which it appears, chemical or electrical attraction, for example, must never be reduced to attraction by gravity, even if their inner analogy is recognized and the former can be regarded, so to speak, as higher powers of the latter. Just as little does the inner analogy of the structure of all animals entitle us to mix up and identify the species, perhaps declaring the more perfect to be varieties of the less perfect.

If, finally, the physiological functions are also never to be reduced to chemical and physical processes, then the following can nevertheless be assumed with great probability in order to justify this procedure within certain limits.

When, among the appearances of the will at the lower grades of its objectivation, that is, in the inorganic realm, several come into conflict with one another, each wanting, along the guiding thread of causality, to take possession of the existing matter, then from this conflict there emerges the appearance of a higher Idea, which overpowers all the less perfect Ideas that were previously present, but in such a way that it allows their essence to persist in a subordinate manner by taking an analogue of them up into itself. This process is intelligible only from the identity of the will appearing in all Ideas, and from its striving toward ever higher objectivation.

We therefore see, for example, in the hardening of bones an unmistakable analogue of crystallization, which originally ruled the lime, although ossification can never be reduced to crystallization. The analogy shows itself more weakly in the hardening of flesh. So too the mixture of fluids in animal bodies and secretion are analogues of chemical mixture and separation. Indeed, the laws of chemical mixture and separation still continue to operate there, but in a subordinate, highly modified way, overpowered by a higher Idea. Therefore merely chemical forces outside the organism will never produce such fluids. Rather:

> `Encheiresin naturae` (operation of nature) chemistry calls it;<br>
> It mocks itself and does not know how.

The more perfect Idea that emerges from such a victory over several lower Ideas, or objectivations of the will, gains, precisely by taking up into itself from each one it overpowers an analogue raised to a higher power, an entirely new character. The will objectifies itself in a new, clearer way. Originally through `generatio aequivoca` (spontaneous generation), and afterward through assimilation to the existing germ, there arise organic fluid, plant, animal, human being. Thus from the conflict of lower appearances the higher one emerges, swallowing them all up, but also realizing the striving of all at a higher grade. Already here, then, the law holds: `serpens, nisi serpentem comederit, non fit draco` (a serpent, unless it has eaten a serpent, does not become a dragon).

I wish it had been possible for me, through clarity of presentation, to overcome the obscurity that clings to the material of these thoughts. But I see very well that the reader's own reflection must come greatly to my aid if I am not to remain misunderstood, or be misunderstood.

According to the view just given, one will indeed be able to demonstrate traces of chemical and physical modes of action in the organism, but never to explain the organism from them. For it is by no means a phenomenon accidentally produced by the combined action of such forces. It is a higher Idea that has subordinated those lower ones to itself by *overpowering assimilation*, because the *one* will that objectifies itself in all Ideas, in striving toward the highest possible objectivation, here gives up the lower grades of its appearance after a conflict among them, in order to appear all the more powerfully at a higher grade.

No victory without struggle. Since the higher Idea, or objectivation of will, can emerge only by overpowering the lower ones, it suffers resistance from them. Although brought into servitude, they still always strive to reach the independent and complete expression of their essence.

Just as a magnet that has lifted a piece of iron maintains a constant struggle with gravity, which, as the lowest objectivation of the will, has a more original right to the matter of that iron, and just as in this constant struggle the magnet even strengthens itself, since resistance stimulates it, as it were, to greater exertion, so every appearance of will, including the one that presents itself in the human organism, maintains a lasting struggle against the many physical and chemical forces which, as lower Ideas, have an earlier right to that matter.

This is why the arm that one has held raised for a while, overcoming gravity, sinks down. This is also why the comfortable feeling of health, which expresses the victory of the Idea of the self-conscious organism over the physical and chemical laws that originally rule the fluids of the body, is so often interrupted, indeed is really always accompanied by a certain greater or lesser discomfort, arising from the resistance of those forces. In this way even the vegetative part of our life is constantly bound up with a slight suffering.

This too is why digestion depresses all animal functions: it claims the whole vital force for the overcoming of chemical natural forces through assimilation. Hence, in general, the burden of physical life, the necessity of sleep, and finally death. For at last, favored by circumstances, those subjugated natural forces win back from the organism, itself exhausted by constant victory, the matter that had been wrested from them, and arrive at the unhindered presentation of their essence.

One can therefore also say that every organism presents the Idea of which it is the image only after subtracting that part of its force which is used to overpower the lower Ideas that contest its matter. This seems to have hovered before Jakob Boehme when he says somewhere that all bodies of human beings and animals, indeed all plants, are actually half dead.

According as the organism succeeds more or less in overpowering those natural forces that express the lower grades of the objecthood of the will, it becomes a more perfect or less perfect expression of its Idea; that is, it stands nearer to or farther from the *ideal* to which beauty belongs in its species.

Thus in nature we everywhere see conflict, struggle, and alternation of victory, and in this we will later recognize more clearly the division of the will against itself that is essential to it. Each grade of the objectivation of the will contests matter, space, and time with every other. Persisting matter must continually change its form, as mechanical, physical, chemical, and organic appearances, guided by causality and greedily pressing forward to emerge, tear matter from one another, since each wants to reveal its Idea.

This conflict can be followed throughout all of nature; indeed, nature exists only through it: ει γαϱ μη ην τo νειϰoς εν τoις πϱαγμασιν, εν αν ην ἁπαντα ὡς φησιν Εμπεδoϰλης (`nam si non inesset in rebus contentio, unum omnia essent, ut ait Empedocles. Arist. Metaph. B., 5`) (for if there were not conflict in things, all things would be one, as Empedocles says). For this conflict itself is only the revelation of the division of the will against itself that is essential to it.

This universal struggle reaches its clearest visibility in the animal world, which has the plant world for its nourishment, and in which again each animal becomes the prey and nourishment of another. That is, the matter in which its Idea presented itself must surrender itself to the presentation of another Idea, since each animal can preserve its existence only through the constant cancellation of another's. Thus the will to life everywhere feeds on itself and, in different forms, is its own nourishment, until finally the human race, because it overpowers all the others, regards nature as a product manufactured for its use. Yet this same race, as we will find in the fourth book, reveals in itself that conflict, that self-division of the will, with the most terrible clarity, and becomes `homo homini lupus` (man is a wolf to man).

Meanwhile, we will recognize the same conflict, the same overpowering, just as much at the lower grades of the objecthood of the will. Many insects, especially the ichneumonids, lay their eggs on the skin, indeed in the body, of the larvae of other insects, whose slow destruction is the first work of the brood that hatches. The young hydra, which grows out of the old one as a dwarf and later separates from it, already struggles with it for the prey that offers itself while it is still attached, so that one tears it from the other's mouth (`Trembley, Polypod. II, p. 110, and III, p. 165`).

The most glaring example of this kind, however, is supplied by the bulldog ant (`bulldog-ant`) in Australia. When it is cut in two, a fight begins between the head part and the tail part. The former attacks the latter with its jaws, and the latter defends itself bravely by stinging the former. The fight usually lasts half an hour, until they die or are dragged away by other ants. The event occurs every time. (From a letter by Howitt in the `W. Journal`, reprinted in Galignani's `Messenger`, November 17, 1855.)

On the banks of the Missouri one sometimes sees a mighty oak so wound around, bound, and constricted on its trunk and all its branches by a gigantic wild grapevine that it must wither as if suffocated. The same thing appears even at the lowest grades, for example where, through organic assimilation, water and carbon are transformed into plant sap, or plants or bread into blood, and everywhere animal secretion takes place with the chemical forces restricted to a subordinate mode of action.

It appears also in inorganic nature: when, for example, crystals shooting forth meet one another, cross one another, and mutually disturb one another so that they cannot show the purely crystallized form, almost every geode is the image of such a conflict of the will at that very low grade of its objectivation. Or again when a magnet forces magnetism upon iron in order to present its Idea there too; or when galvanism overpowers elective affinities, decomposes the firmest compounds, and suspends the chemical laws to such a degree that the acid of a salt decomposed at the negative pole must go to the positive pole without combining with the alkalis through which it passes on the way, or even being allowed to redden the litmus it encounters.

On a large scale it appears in the relation between central body and planet. The planet, although in decided dependence, still resists, just like the chemical forces in the organism. From this arises the constant tension between centripetal and centrifugal force, which keeps the structure of the world in motion and is itself already an expression of that universal conflict essential to the appearance of the will which we are considering.

For since every body must be regarded as the appearance of a will, and will necessarily presents itself as a striving, the original state of every heavenly body gathered into a sphere cannot be rest, but motion, a striving onward into infinite space, without rest and without goal. Neither the law of inertia nor the law of causality stands opposed to this. For according to the former, matter as such is indifferent to rest and motion, and therefore motion, just as much as rest, can be its original state. Consequently, if we find it in motion, we are no more entitled to presuppose that a state of rest preceded this motion and to ask for the cause of the entrance of motion, than, conversely, if we found it at rest, we would be entitled to presuppose a motion that preceded this rest and to ask for the cause of its cessation.

Therefore no first impulse is to be sought for centrifugal force. In the case of the planets, according to Kant's and Laplace's hypothesis, it is the remnant of the original rotation of the central body from which they separated during its contraction. Motion, however, is essential to this body itself: it still rotates and at the same time flies onward in endless space, or perhaps circles around a larger central body invisible to us.

This view agrees completely with the astronomers' conjecture of a central sun, and also with the observed advance of our whole solar system, perhaps also of the whole star cluster to which our sun belongs. From this one may finally infer a universal advance of all fixed stars, together with the central sun. In infinite space, to be sure, this loses all meaning, since motion in absolute space is not distinguished from rest. Precisely thereby, as already directly through striving and flying without goal, it becomes the expression of that nothingness, that lack of an ultimate purpose, which at the end of this book we will have to acknowledge in the striving of the will in all its appearances. For this reason, too, endless space and endless time had to be the most universal and essential forms of its whole appearance, as that which exists in order to express its whole essence.

Finally, we can recognize the conflict of all appearances of will against one another even in mere matter considered as such, insofar as Kant rightly expressed the essence of its appearance as repulsive and attractive force. Thus matter itself has its existence only in a conflict of opposed forces.

If we abstract from all chemical difference in matter, or think ourselves back along the chain of causes and effects to the point where there is as yet no chemical difference, what remains is mere matter, the world gathered into a sphere, whose life, that is, objectivation of the will, now consists in that conflict between attractive and repulsive force. The former, as gravity, presses from all sides toward the center; the latter, as impenetrability, resists it, whether through rigidity or elasticity. This constant pressure and resistance may be regarded as the objecthood of the will at the very lowest grade, and even there already expresses its character.

Here, then, at the lowest grade, we would see the will present itself as a blind drive, an obscure, dull impulse, far from all immediate knowability. This is the simplest and weakest kind of its objectivation. As such blind drive and cognitionless striving it appears throughout inorganic nature, in all the original forces whose search and whose laws are the concern of physics and chemistry. Each of these forces presents itself to us in millions of entirely homogeneous and law-governed appearances, announcing no trace of individual character, but merely multiplied by time and space, that is, by the `principium individuationis` (principle of individuation), as an image is multiplied by the facets of a glass.

Objectifying itself more clearly from grade to grade, the will nevertheless still works entirely without cognition in the plant kingdom, where the connection among its appearances is no longer made by causes in the strict sense but by stimuli, as an obscure driving force. It does so finally even in the vegetative part of animal appearance, in the production and development of every animal and in the maintenance of its inner economy, where mere stimuli still necessarily determine its appearance.

The ever higher grades of the objecthood of the will finally lead to the point where the individual that presents the Idea could no longer obtain the nourishment it had to assimilate by mere movement in response to stimuli. Such a stimulus would have to be awaited, whereas here nourishment is more specifically determined; and with the ever greater multiplicity of appearances, the crowding and confusion have become so great that they disturb one another, and the chance on which the individual moved merely by stimuli would have to wait for its nourishment would be too unfavorable. From the point when the animal has freed itself from the egg or womb in which it vegetated without cognition, nourishment must therefore be sought out and selected.

Here, then, movement according to motives becomes necessary, and because of this cognition becomes necessary. Cognition therefore enters as an aid required at this grade of the objectivation of the will, a μηχανη (contrivance), for the preservation of the individual and the propagation of the species. It emerges represented by the brain or a larger ganglion, just as every other striving or determination of the will objectifying itself is represented by an organ, that is, presents itself for representation as an organ.[^1]

But with this aid, this μηχανη (contrivance), the *world as representation* suddenly stands there, with all its forms: object and subject, time, space, multiplicity, and causality. The world now shows its second side. Previously merely *will*, it is now at the same time *representation*, object of the knowing subject. The will, which until now had pursued its drive in the dark with the greatest certainty and infallibility, has at this grade lit a lamp for itself. This became necessary as a means of removing the disadvantage that would arise precisely for the most perfect of its appearances from the crowded and complicated character of those appearances.

The previous infallible certainty and lawfulness with which it worked in inorganic and merely vegetative nature rested on the fact that it alone was active in its original essence, as blind drive, as will, without assistance, but also without disturbance from a second and wholly different world, the world as representation. This world is indeed only the copy of its own essence, but is nevertheless of an entirely different nature and now intervenes in the connection among its appearances.

Through this, the infallible certainty of those appearances now ceases. Animals are already exposed to semblance, to deception. They have, however, only intuitive representations, no concepts, no reflection; hence they are bound to the present and cannot take the future into account.

It seems as if this cognition without reason were not in all cases sufficient for its purpose, and sometimes needed something like supplementary help. For there appears to us the very remarkable phenomenon that the blind working of the will and the working illuminated by cognition cross over into one another's domain, in two kinds of appearances, in a most surprising way.

First, in the midst of the actions of animals guided by intuitive cognition and its motives, we find an action carried out without these, and therefore with the necessity of blindly working will: the constructive drives. These are guided by no motive and no cognition, yet they have the look of producing their works even according to abstract, rational motives. The other, opposite case is the one in which, conversely, the light of cognition penetrates into the workshop of the blindly working will and illuminates the vegetative functions of the human organism: magnetic clairvoyance.

Finally, where the will has reached the highest grade of its objectivation, the cognition of the understanding that dawned in animals, to which the senses supply the data from which mere intuition arises, bound to the present, is no longer enough. The complicated, many-sided, pliable being, the human being, so full of needs and exposed to innumerable injuries, had to be illuminated by a double cognition in order to exist. A higher power of intuitive cognition, as it were, had to be added to it, a reflection of it: reason, as the faculty of abstract concepts.

With reason, reflective awareness was present, containing an overview of future and past, and, as a result, deliberation, care, the capacity for premeditated action independent of the present, and finally also fully distinct consciousness of one's own decisions of will as such.

If the possibility of semblance and deception already entered with merely intuitive cognition, thereby cancelling the previous infallibility in the cognitionless drive of the will, so that instinct and constructive drive, as cognitionless expressions of will, had to come to its aid in the midst of actions guided by cognition, then with the entrance of reason that certainty and unerring character of the expressions of will, which at the other extreme, in inorganic nature, even appears as strict lawfulness, is almost entirely lost.

Instinct recedes completely. Deliberation, which is now supposed to replace everything, produces, as was shown in the first book, vacillation and uncertainty. Error becomes possible, and in many cases prevents the adequate objectivation of the will through deeds. For although the will has already taken its determinate and unchangeable direction in the character, in accordance with which willing itself unfailingly occurs on the occasion of motives, error can still falsify its expressions, since illusory motives then enter in just like real ones and cancel them.[^2] So, for example, when superstition interposes imaginary motives that compel a human being to a course of action directly opposed to the way his will would otherwise express itself under the existing circumstances: Agamemnon slaughters his daughter; a miser gives alms out of pure egoism, in the hope of a hundredfold repayment someday, and so on.

Cognition in general, rational as well as merely intuitive, thus originally arises from the will itself. It belongs to the essence of the higher grades of its objectivation as a mere μηχανη (contrivance), a means for preserving the individual and the species, just like every organ of the body. Originally destined to serve the will and to accomplish its purposes, it also remains almost entirely serviceable to the will throughout: so in all animals and in almost all human beings.

Yet in the third book we will see how, in individual human beings, cognition can withdraw from this servitude, throw off its yoke, and, free from all purposes of willing, exist purely for itself as the mere clear mirror of the world, from which art arises. Finally, in the fourth book, we will see how, through this kind of cognition, when it acts back upon the will, the will's self-cancellation can occur: that is, resignation, which is the final goal, indeed the innermost essence, of all virtue and holiness, and redemption from the world.
