# §. 20.

As the essence in itself of one's own body, as that which this body is besides being an object of intuition, a representation, the *will*, as was said, first makes itself known in the voluntary movements of this body. For these movements are nothing other than the visibility of the individual acts of will. They enter immediately and completely at the same time as those acts, as one and the same thing with them, distinguished from them only by the form of knowability into which they have passed, that is, by the fact that they have become representation.

These acts of will, however, still always have a ground outside themselves, in motives. Yet motives never determine more than what I will at *this* time, in *this* place, under *these* circumstances. They do not determine *that* I will at all, nor *what* I will in general, that is, the maxim that characterizes my entire willing.

My willing, therefore, cannot be explained in its whole essence from motives. Motives determine only its expression at the given moment; they are merely the occasion on which my will shows itself. The will itself, by contrast, lies outside the domain of the law of motivation. Only its appearance at each moment is necessarily determined by that law.

Only on the presupposition of my empirical character is the motive the sufficient ground of explanation for my action. But if I abstract from my character and then ask why I will this in general and not that, no answer is possible. For only the *appearance* of the will is subject to the principle of sufficient reason, not the will itself, which in this respect is to be called *groundless*.

Here I partly presuppose Kant's doctrine of the empirical and intelligible character, as well as the relevant discussions in my *Two Fundamental Problems of Ethics*, pp. 48-58, and again pp. 178 ff. of the first edition [2nd ed., pp. 46-57 and 174 ff.]. We will also have to speak of this more fully in the fourth book. For now I need only draw attention to this: that one appearance is grounded by another, here the deed by the motive, in no way conflicts with the fact that its essence in itself is will, which itself has no ground. For the principle of sufficient reason, in all its forms, is merely the form of cognition. Its validity therefore extends only to representation, to appearance, to the visibility of the will, not to the will itself that becomes visible.

Now if every action of my body is the appearance of an act of will, in which, under given motives, my will itself as a whole, and therefore my character, expresses itself again, then the indispensable condition and presupposition of every action must also be appearance of the will. For the will's appearing cannot depend on something that is not immediately and solely through it, something that would therefore be merely accidental for it; otherwise its appearing itself would become merely accidental. But that condition is the whole body itself.

Therefore the body itself must already be appearance of the will. It must stand to my will as a whole, that is, to my intelligible character, whose appearance in time is my empirical character, in the same relation as the individual action of the body stands to the individual act of will. Thus the whole body must be nothing other than my will become visible. It must be my will itself insofar as this will is an intuitive object, a representation of the first class.

As confirmation of this, it has already been mentioned that every effect upon my body at once and immediately affects my will as well, and in this respect is called pain or pleasure, or in a lower degree an agreeable or disagreeable sensation. Conversely, every violent movement of the will, and so every affect and passion, shakes the body and disturbs the course of its functions.

To be sure, one can give an aetiological account, though only very imperfectly, of the origin of my body, and somewhat better of its development and preservation; this is precisely physiology. But physiology explains its subject only in the same way that motives explain action. Therefore, just as the grounding of an individual action by the motive, and the necessary consequence of that action from the motive, in no way conflicts with the fact that the action in general and according to its essence is only the appearance of a will that is groundless in itself, so too the physiological explanation of the functions of the body does not detract from the philosophical truth that the whole existence of this body and the entire series of its functions are only the objectivation of that same will which appears in the body's external actions according to motives.

Indeed, physiology even seeks to trace these external actions themselves, the immediately voluntary movements, back to causes in the organism. For example, it explains the movement of the muscle from an influx of fluids, "like the contraction of a cord that becomes wet," as Reil says in his *Archive for Physiology*, vol. 6, p. 153. But suppose one really did arrive at a thorough explanation of this kind: this would still never abolish the immediately certain truth that every voluntary movement (`functiones animales`; animal functions) is the appearance of an act of will.

In just the same way, no physiological explanation of vegetative life (`functiones naturales, vitales`; natural, vital functions), however far it might advance, can ever abolish the truth that this whole animal life itself, developing in this way, is appearance of the will.

In general, as was discussed above, every aetiological explanation can never state more than the necessarily determined place in time and space of an individual appearance, its necessary occurrence there according to a fixed rule. By contrast, the inner essence of every appearance always remains unfathomable along this path. It is presupposed by every aetiological explanation and merely indicated by the names force, or law of nature, or, where actions are in question, character, will.

Thus, although every individual action, on the presupposition of the determinate character, necessarily follows when the motive is presented, and although growth, the process of nutrition, and all changes in the animal body proceed according to necessarily acting causes, namely stimuli, nevertheless the whole series of actions, and consequently each individual action, and likewise their condition, the whole body itself that performs them, and consequently also the process through which and in which the body exists, are nothing other than the appearance of the will, the becoming-visible, the *objecthood of the will*.

On this rests the complete appropriateness of the human and animal body to the human and animal will in general. This appropriateness is similar to, but far surpasses, the fit that an intentionally made tool has to the will of its maker, and for this reason it appears as purposiveness, that is, as the teleological explicability of the body.

The parts of the body must therefore correspond perfectly to the chief desires through which the will manifests itself; they must be their visible expression. Teeth, throat, and intestinal canal are objectified hunger; the genitals are the objectified sexual drive; the grasping hands and swift feet correspond to the will's already more mediate striving, which they represent.

As the general human form corresponds to the general human will, so the individual corporeal form corresponds to the individually modified will, the character of the individual. This corporeal form is therefore thoroughly characteristic and expressive, in all its parts.

It is very remarkable that Parmenides already expressed this in the following verses cited by Aristotle (*Metaphysics*, III, 5):

> `ὡς γὰρ ἕκαστος ἔχει κρᾶσιν μελέων πολυκάμπτων,`
> `τώς νόος ἀνθρώποισι παρέστηκεν· τὸ γὰρ αὐτό`
> `ἔστιν ὅπερ φρονέει μελέων φύσις ἀνθρώποισιν,`
> `καὶ πᾶσιν καὶ παντί· τὸ γὰρ πλέον ἐστὶ νόημα.`
>
> `Ut enim cuique complexio membrorum flexibilium se habet, ita mens hominibus adest: idem namque est, quod sapit, membrorum natura hominibus, et omnibus et omni: quod enim plus est, intelligentia est.`
>
> Translation: For as each person has the constitution of his many-jointed limbs, so mind is present to human beings; for the nature of the limbs in human beings is the same as what thinks, both in all and in each; for what is more is thought.[^1]
