# §. 8.

As from the immediate light of the sun into the borrowed reflection of the moon, so we pass from intuitive, immediate representation, which represents and vouches for itself, over to reflection: to the abstract, discursive concepts of reason, which have all their content only from that intuitive cognition and in relation to it.

So long as we conduct ourselves purely intuitively, everything is clear, firm, and certain. There are no questions, no doubts, no errors. One does not want to go further, cannot go further; one has rest in intuition, satisfaction in the present. Intuition is sufficient to itself. Therefore what has sprung purely from it and remained faithful to it, such as the genuine work of art, can never be false, nor can it be refuted by any time; for there is no opinion there, but the thing itself.

But with abstract cognition, with reason, doubt and error have entered in theoretical matters, and care and remorse in practical matters. If in intuitive representation *semblance* momentarily distorts actuality, then in abstract representation *error* can rule for millennia, cast its iron yoke over whole peoples, stifle the noblest stirrings of humanity, and even have those whom it cannot deceive bound in chains by its slaves, its deceived followers.

Error is the enemy against which the wisest minds of all ages have waged the unequal struggle; and only what they have wrested from it has become the property of humanity. It is therefore good to draw attention to it at once, as we enter the ground on which its domain lies.

It has often been said that one should pursue truth even where no use from it can be foreseen, since that use may be indirect and emerge where one does not expect it. I find, however, that I must add here that one should also strive just as much to uncover and eradicate every error, even where no harm from it can be foreseen, since that harm too may be very indirect and one day emerge where one does not expect it. For every error carries a poison within it.

If it is spirit, if it is cognition, that makes the human being lord of the earth, then there are no harmless errors, still less venerable, holy errors. And for the consolation of those who devote strength and life to the noble and difficult struggle against error, in whatever kind or matter, I cannot refrain from adding this: so long as truth is not yet present, error can play its game, like owls and bats in the night. But one might sooner expect owls and bats to chase the sun back into the east than that truth, once recognized and clearly and completely expressed, should be displaced again so that the old error may once more occupy its broad place undisturbed. Such is the power of truth: its victory is difficult and laborious, but once won, it cannot be taken from it again.

Besides the representations considered up to this point, which in their composition could be traced back to time, space, and matter if we look to the object, or to pure sensibility and understanding, that is, cognition of causality, if we look to the subject, there has entered in the human being alone, among all the inhabitants of the earth, another power of cognition. An entirely new consciousness has dawned, very aptly and with anticipatory correctness called *reflection*.

For reflection is indeed a reflected light, something derived from intuitive cognition. Yet it has taken on a nature and character fundamentally different from that cognition; it does not know its forms, and even the principle of sufficient reason, which governs every object, has here an entirely different form. This new consciousness, raised to a higher power, this abstract reflection of everything intuitive in the non-intuitive concept of reason, is what alone gives the human being that reflective awareness which so thoroughly distinguishes his consciousness from that of animals, and through which his whole conduct on earth turns out so differently from that of his non-rational brothers.

He surpasses them equally in power and in suffering. They live in the present alone; he lives at the same time in future and past. They satisfy the need of the moment; he makes the most elaborate arrangements for his future, indeed for times he cannot live to see. They are wholly given over to the impression of the moment, to the effect of the intuitive motive; he is determined by abstract concepts independently of the present.

Therefore he carries out deliberate plans or acts according to maxims, without regard to his surroundings and the accidental impressions of the moment. Thus, for example, he can calmly make artificial arrangements for his own death; he can dissemble to the point of inscrutability and take his secret with him to the grave. Finally, he has a real choice between several motives. For only `in abstracto` (in the abstract) can such motives, present side by side in consciousness, bring with them the cognition that one excludes the other, and so measure their power over the will against one another. Then the motive that predominates, by giving the deciding stroke, is the deliberate decision of the will and makes known its character as a sure sign.

The animal, by contrast, is determined by the present impression. Only fear of present compulsion can tame its desire, until that fear has finally become habit and now determines it as such: this is training. The animal senses and intuits; the human being also *thinks* and *knows*. Both *will*.

The animal communicates its sensation and mood by gesture and sound. The human being communicates thoughts to another by language, or conceals thoughts by language. Language is the first product and the necessary instrument of his reason. Hence in Greek and Italian, language and reason are designated by the same word: `ὁ λόγος` and `il discorso` (discourse). `Vernunft`, reason, comes from `Vernehmen`, to apprehend, which is not synonymous with hearing, but means becoming aware of thoughts communicated through words.

Only with the help of language does reason bring about its most important achievements: the concordant action of several individuals, the planned cooperation of many thousands, civilization, the state; further, science, the preservation of earlier experience, the gathering of what is common into one concept, the communication of truth, the spread of error, thinking and poetry, dogmas and superstitions.

The animal first comes to know death in death. The human being consciously approaches his death every hour, and this sometimes makes life questionable even for the one who has not already recognized this character of constant annihilation in life as a whole. Chiefly for this reason the human being has philosophies and religions; yet whether what we rightly value above all in his action, voluntary right-doing and nobility of disposition, has ever been the fruit of either is uncertain. By contrast, as secure products belonging to them alone, and as productions of reason along this path, there stand the strangest and most adventurous opinions of philosophers of different schools, and the oddest, sometimes also cruel, practices of priests of different religions.

That all these very diverse and far-reaching manifestations spring from one common principle, from that special power of mind that the human being has over the animal and that has been called *reason*, `ὁ λόγος`, `τὸ λογιστικόν`, `τὸ λόγιμον`, `ratio`, is the unanimous opinion of all ages and peoples.

All human beings also know very well how to recognize the manifestations of this faculty, and how to say what is rational and what is irrational, where reason appears in contrast with other capacities and qualities of the human being, and finally what, because of the lack of reason, is never to be expected even from the cleverest animal.

The philosophers of all ages also speak, on the whole, in agreement with that common knowledge of reason, and in addition emphasize several especially important manifestations of it, such as mastery over affects and passions, the capacity to make inferences and to set up universal principles, even principles certain prior to all experience, and so on. Yet all their explanations of the proper essence of reason are wavering, not sharply determined, diffuse, without unity or center, now emphasizing this manifestation, now that one, and therefore often diverging from one another.

In addition, many of them start from the opposition between reason and revelation, which is entirely foreign to philosophy and serves only to increase the confusion. It is highly striking that until now no philosopher has strictly traced all those manifold manifestations of reason back to one simple function, recognizable in them all, from which they could all be explained and which would therefore constitute the proper inner essence of reason.

To be sure, the excellent Locke, in *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding*, Book 2, chapter 11, §§. 10 and 11, quite correctly gives abstract universal concepts as the distinguishing character between animal and human being; and Leibniz repeats this in complete agreement in the *New Essays on Human Understanding*, Book 2, chapter 11, §§. 10 and 11.

But when Locke, in Book 4, chapter 17, §§. 2 and 3, comes to the actual explanation of reason, he entirely loses sight of that simple chief characteristic of it, and likewise falls into a wavering, indeterminate, incomplete account of its fragmented and derivative manifestations. Leibniz, in the corresponding passage of his work, behaves on the whole in the same way, only with more confusion and obscurity.

I have spoken at length in the appendix about how greatly Kant confused and falsified the concept of the essence of reason. But anyone who takes the trouble to go through, from this point of view, the mass of philosophical writings that have appeared since Kant will recognize that just as the errors of princes are paid for by whole peoples, so the errors of great minds spread their harmful influence over whole generations, even over centuries, indeed growing and propagating themselves until they finally degenerate into monstrosities. All this follows from the fact that, as Berkeley says:

> `Few men think; yet all will have opinions.` [^1]

Just as the understanding has only *one* function, immediate cognition of the relation of cause and effect, and just as intuition of the actual world, as well as all prudence, sagacity, and inventiveness, however varied their application may be, are quite obviously nothing other than manifestations of that simple function, so reason too has *one* function: formation of the concept. From this one function all the phenomena mentioned above, which distinguish the life of the human being from that of the animal, are very easily and entirely self-evidently explained. Everything that has everywhere and always been called rational or irrational points simply to the application or non-application of that function. [^2]

[^1]: Few human beings think, but all want to have opinions.

[^2]: Compare with this section §§. 26 and 27 of the second edition of the essay on the principle of sufficient reason.
