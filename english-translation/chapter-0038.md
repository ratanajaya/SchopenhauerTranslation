History follows the thread of events. It is pragmatic insofar as it derives them according to the law of motivation, the law that determines the appearing will where it is illuminated by cognition.

At the lower grades of the will's objecthood, where it still works without cognition, natural science considers the laws governing the changes of its appearances as aetiology, and what is enduring in them as morphology. Morphology makes its almost infinite theme easier for itself with the help of concepts, comprehending the universal in order to derive the particular from it.

Finally, mathematics considers the mere forms in which, for the cognition of the subject as individual, the Ideas appear drawn apart into multiplicity: time and space. All these, whose common name is science, therefore follow the principle of sufficient reason in its different forms, and their theme remains appearance, its laws, its connection, and the relations arising from it.

What kind of cognition, then, considers that which exists outside and independently of all relation, the only thing truly essential in the world, the true content of its appearances, that which is subject to no change and is therefore cognized with equal truth for all time? In a word, what cognition considers the *Ideas*, which are the immediate and adequate objecthood of the thing in itself, the will?

It is *art*, the work of genius. Art repeats the eternal Ideas apprehended through pure contemplation, the essential and enduring element in all appearances of the world; and according to the material in which it repeats them, it is plastic art, poetry, or music. Its sole origin is cognition of the Ideas; its sole goal is communication of this cognition.

Science follows the restless and unstable stream of grounds and consequents in their fourfold form. At every goal it reaches, it is always directed further onward again, and can never find a final goal or complete satisfaction, any more than one can reach, by running, the point where the clouds touch the horizon. Art, by contrast, is everywhere at its goal. For it tears the object of its contemplation out of the stream of the world's course and has it isolated before itself. This individual thing, which in that stream was a vanishingly small part, becomes for art a representative of the whole, an equivalent of the infinitely many in space and time.

Art therefore stops at this individual thing. It halts the wheel of time. Relations vanish for it. Only the essential, the Idea, is its object.

We can therefore designate art quite directly as *the way of considering things independently of the principle of sufficient reason*, in contrast to the consideration that follows precisely this principle, which is the path of experience and science. This latter kind of consideration may be compared to an infinite horizontal line; the former to the vertical line that cuts it at any chosen point.

The consideration that follows the principle of sufficient reason is the rational way of considering things, which alone has validity and use in practical life and in science. The consideration that looks away from the content of that principle is the way of considering things proper to genius, which alone has validity and use in art. The former is Aristotle's way of considering things; the latter is, on the whole, Plato's.

The former is like the mighty storm that rushes on without beginning or goal, bending, moving, and carrying everything away with it. The latter is like the calm ray of sunlight that cuts through the path of this storm, wholly unmoved by it. The former is like the countless drops of a waterfall, violently moved, constantly changing, not resting for a moment. The latter is like the rainbow resting quietly on this raging turmoil.

Ideas are apprehended only through the pure contemplation described above, the contemplation that is wholly absorbed in the object; and the essence of *genius* consists precisely in the preponderant capacity for such contemplation. Since this contemplation demands a complete forgetting of one's own person and its relations, *genius* is nothing other than the most complete *objectivity*, that is, the objective direction of the mind, opposed to the subjective direction toward one's own person, that is, toward the will.

Accordingly, genius is the capacity to behave purely intuitively, to lose oneself in intuition, and to withdraw from the service of the will the cognition that originally exists only for that service. This means losing sight entirely of one's interest, one's willing, and one's purposes, and thus for a time completely divesting oneself of one's personality, in order to remain as *pure knowing subject*, as clear eye of the world. And this must happen not for moments only, but so persistently and with as much reflective awareness as is needed in order to repeat, through deliberate art, what has been apprehended, and "to fix in enduring thoughts what hovers in wavering appearance."

It is as if, in order for genius to emerge in an individual, a measure of cognitive power must have fallen to him that far exceeds what is required for the service of an individual will. This surplus of cognition, now set free, becomes the subject pure of will, the bright mirror of the essence of the world.

This explains the liveliness, even to the point of unrest, in individuals of genius. The present can rarely satisfy them, because it does not fill their consciousness. This gives them that restless striving, that unceasing search for new objects worthy of contemplation, and also that almost never satisfied longing for beings like themselves, equal to them, to whom they could communicate themselves. Meanwhile the ordinary child of earth, entirely filled and satisfied by the ordinary present, is absorbed in it; and since he finds his like everywhere, he has that special comfort in everyday life which is denied to genius.

Imagination has been recognized as an essential component of genius; indeed, it has sometimes even been taken as identical with it. The former is right; the latter is wrong. Since the objects of genius as such are the eternal Ideas, the enduring essential forms of the world and all its appearances, and since cognition of the Idea is necessarily intuitive, not abstract, the cognition of genius would be limited to the Ideas of the objects actually present to his person, and would depend on the chain of circumstances that brought them to him, if imagination did not extend his horizon far beyond the actuality of his personal experience. It enables him, from the little that has entered his actual apperception, to construct all the rest, and thus to let almost all possible images of life pass before him.

Moreover, actual objects are almost always only very defective specimens of the Idea presenting itself in them. Genius therefore needs imagination in order to see in things not what nature has actually formed, but what nature strove to form and did not bring about because of the conflict among its forms mentioned in the preceding book. We will return to this below when considering sculpture.

Imagination therefore extends the horizon of genius beyond the objects that present themselves in actuality to his person, both in quality and in quantity. For this reason, unusual strength of imagination accompanies genius; indeed, it is a condition of it. But conversely, imagination does not testify to genius. On the contrary, even very ungifted people can have a great deal of imagination.

For just as one can consider an actual object in two opposed ways, either purely objectively, as genius does, grasping its Idea, or commonly, merely in its relations to other objects and to one's own will according to the principle of sufficient reason, so too one can intuit a phantasm in both ways. Considered in the first way, it is a means to cognition of the Idea, whose communication is the work of art. In the second case, the phantasm is used to build castles in the air that suit self-interest and one's own whim, momentarily deceiving and amusing; in this case, what is really cognized in the phantasms thus connected is always only their relations.

The person who carries on this game is a fantasist. He will easily mix the images with which he amuses himself in solitude into actuality, and thereby become unfit for it. Perhaps he will write down the illusions of his imagination, where they produce the ordinary novels of every kind, which entertain his like and the great public, since the readers dream themselves into the place of the hero and then find the presentation very "cozy."

The ordinary human being, this factory product of nature as she brings it forth daily by the thousand, is, as I have said, not capable, at least not persistently, of a wholly disinterested consideration in every sense, which is genuine contemplation. He can direct his attention to things only insofar as they have some relation, however indirect, to his will.

Since in this respect, which always requires only cognition of relations, the abstract concept of the thing is sufficient and usually even more useful, the ordinary human being does not dwell long with mere intuition, and therefore does not fasten his gaze on an object for long. Instead, with everything that presents itself to him, he quickly seeks only the concept under which it is to be brought, as the lazy person seeks a chair; and then it interests him no further.

That is why he is so quickly finished with everything: with works of art, beautiful natural objects, and the genuinely significant sight of life in all its scenes. He does not linger. He seeks only his path in life, and at most everything that could ever become his path, that is, topographical notes in the broadest sense. He loses no time in contemplating life itself as such.

The person of genius, by contrast, whose cognitive power, through its preponderance, withdraws itself from the service of his will for part of his time, lingers with the contemplation of life itself. He strives to grasp the Idea of each thing, not its relations to other things. In doing this, he often neglects consideration of his own path in life, and therefore usually follows it awkwardly enough.

For the ordinary human being, his faculty of cognition is the lantern that lights his path. For the person of genius, it is the sun that makes the world manifest. This very different way of looking into life soon becomes visible even outwardly in both. The gaze of the human being in whom genius lives and works easily distinguishes him, since, lively and firm at once, it bears the character of contemplation; as we can see in the portraits of the few heads of genius that nature has now and then produced among countless millions. By contrast, in the gaze of the others, if it is not, as is usually the case, dull or sober, the true opposite of contemplation, namely spying, easily becomes visible.

Accordingly, the "expression of genius" in a head consists in the fact that a decided predominance of cognition over willing is visible in it, and consequently that a cognition without any relation to willing, that is, a *pure cognition*, expresses itself there. By contrast, in heads such as they usually are, the expression of willing predominates, and one sees that cognition first becomes active only at the impulse of willing, and is therefore directed merely toward motives.

Since cognition of genius, or cognition of the Idea, is the cognition that does not follow the principle of sufficient reason, while the cognition that follows this principle confers prudence and rationality in life and brings about the sciences, individuals of genius will be afflicted with the defects entailed by neglect of the latter mode of cognition.

However, one must note the following limitation: what I will mention in this respect applies to them only insofar as, and while, they are actually engaged in the mode of cognition proper to genius. This is by no means the case at every moment of their lives, since the great, though spontaneous, exertion required for the will-free apprehension of the Ideas necessarily slackens again and leaves large intervals in which, both with respect to advantages and defects, they stand fairly close to ordinary human beings.

For this reason, the working of genius has always been regarded as inspiration; indeed, as the name itself indicates, as the working of a superhuman being different from the individual himself, which periodically takes possession of him.

The reluctance of individuals of genius to direct attention to the content of the principle of sufficient reason will first show itself with regard to the ground of being, as an aversion to mathematics. Mathematical consideration concerns the most universal forms of appearance, space and time, which themselves are only forms of the principle of sufficient reason, and is therefore the complete opposite of that consideration which seeks precisely only the content of appearance, the Idea expressing itself in it, while looking away from all relations.

In addition, the logical treatment of mathematics will resist genius, since, by blocking genuine insight, it does not satisfy, but offers a mere chain of inferences according to the principle of the ground of knowing. Among all mental powers it makes the greatest claim on memory, since one must always keep present all the earlier propositions to which one appeals.

Experience too has confirmed that great geniuses in art have no aptitude for mathematics: never was a human being very distinguished in both. Alfieri says that he was never able to grasp even the fourth proposition of Euclid. Goethe's lack of mathematical knowledge has been reproached often enough by the unintelligent opponents of his theory of colors. Of course, where the issue was not calculation and measurement according to hypothetical data, but immediate cognition by the understanding of cause and effect, this reproach was so completely askew and out of place that those opponents displayed their total lack of power of judgment by it just as much as by their other Midas-pronouncements.

That even today, almost half a century after the appearance of Goethe's theory of colors, the Newtonian flimflam remains undisturbed in possession of the professorial chairs, even in Germany, and people continue, quite seriously, to speak of the seven homogeneous lights and their different refrangibility, will someday be counted among the great intellectual character traits of humanity in general and of Germanness in particular.

The same reason given above also explains the equally well-known fact that, conversely, distinguished mathematicians have little receptivity for works of fine art. This is expressed with particular naivety in the well-known anecdote about the French mathematician who, after reading Racine's *Iphigenia*, shrugged and asked: `Qu'est-ce-que cela prouve?` (What does that prove?)

Furthermore, since sharp apprehension of relations according to the law of causality and motivation is what actually constitutes prudence, while cognition of genius is not directed to relations, a prudent person, insofar as and while he is prudent, will not be a genius, and a genius, insofar as and while he is a genius, will not be prudent.

Finally, intuitive cognition in general, in whose domain the Idea lies throughout, stands in direct opposition to rational or abstract cognition, which is guided by the principle of the ground of knowing. It is also well known that great genius is rarely found paired with predominant rationality. On the contrary, individuals of genius are often subject to violent affects and irrational passions.

The reason for this is nevertheless not weakness of reason, but partly the unusual energy of the whole appearance of will that is the individual of genius, an energy that expresses itself through the vehemence of all acts of will; and partly the predominance of intuitive cognition through senses and understanding over abstract cognition. Hence there is a decided direction toward the intuitive, whose highly energetic impression in such people so outshines the colorless concepts that their action is guided no longer by these concepts, but by that impression, and thereby becomes irrational.

Accordingly, the impression of the present is very powerful in them, carrying them away into rashness, affect, and passion. For this reason too, and in general because their cognition has in part withdrawn itself from the service of the will, in conversation they will not think so much of the person to whom they are speaking as of the matter of which they speak, which hovers vividly before them. They will therefore judge or narrate too objectively for their own interest, will not keep silent about what it would be wiser to keep silent about, and so on. Finally, they are inclined to monologues and can in general show several weaknesses that really approach madness.

That genius and madness have one side where they border on one another, indeed pass into one another, has often been observed; and even poetic inspiration has been called a kind of madness. Horace calls it `amabilis insania` (lovely madness) in *Odes* III, 4, and Wieland calls it "sweet madness" at the beginning of *Oberon*. Even Aristotle is said, according to Seneca's citation in *On Tranquility of Mind* 15, 16, to have said: `Nullum magnum ingenium sine mixtura dementiae fuit` (There has been no great genius without some admixture of madness).

Plato expresses this in the myth of the dark cave cited above, in *Republic* VII, by saying that those who have seen the true sunlight and the really existing things outside the cave, the Ideas, later cannot see anymore in the cave because their eyes are no longer accustomed to darkness. They no longer recognize the shadow-images down there correctly, and for this reason, because of their mistakes, they are mocked by the others, who never left this cave and these shadow-images. In the *Phaedrus* he also says quite directly that without a certain madness there can be no genuine poet, indeed that anyone who recognizes the eternal Ideas in perishable things appears mad.

Cicero too reports: `Negat enim, sine furore, Democritus, quemquam poëtam magnum esse posse; quod idem dicit Plato (de divin. I, 37).` (Democritus denies that anyone can be a great poet without frenzy; Plato says the same.) And finally Pope says:

> `Great wits to madness sure are near allied,`<br>
> `And thin partitions do their bounds divide`[^1]

Especially instructive in this respect is Goethe's *Torquato Tasso*, in which he sets before our eyes not only the suffering, the essential martyrdom of genius as such, but also its steady transition into madness.

Finally, the fact of immediate contact between genius and madness is confirmed by the biographies of highly gifted human beings, for example Rousseau, Byron, and Alfieri, and by anecdotes from the lives of others. On the other hand, I must mention that, in frequent visits to asylums, I have found individual subjects of unmistakably great gifts, whose genius clearly showed through their madness, though madness had here completely gained the upper hand.

This cannot be attributed to chance, because on the one hand the number of the insane is relatively very small, while on the other hand an individual of genius is a phenomenon rare beyond all ordinary estimation, and appears in nature only as the greatest exception. One can convince oneself of this simply by counting the truly great geniuses whom all cultivated Europe has produced throughout the whole of ancient and modern times, including only those who produced works that retain lasting value for humanity through all ages, and then comparing these few individuals with the 250 million people who, renewing themselves every thirty years, live constantly in Europe.

Indeed, I will not omit mentioning that I have known some people of spiritual superiority that was not significant, but was nevertheless decided, who at the same time betrayed a slight tinge of derangement. According to this, it might seem that every increase of intellect beyond the ordinary measure already disposes one to madness as an abnormality.

Meanwhile, I will present as briefly as possible my view of the purely intellectual ground of that kinship between genius and madness, since this discussion will contribute to explaining the proper essence of genius, that is, of the quality of mind that alone can create genuine works of art. But this makes a brief discussion of madness itself necessary.[^2]

To my knowledge, a clear and complete insight into the essence of madness, a correct and distinct concept of what actually distinguishes the mad person from the healthy one, has still not been found.

Neither reason nor understanding can be denied to the mad, for they speak and understand, and often infer very correctly. As a rule they also intuit the present quite correctly and grasp the connection between cause and effect. Visions, like fever fantasies, are not a common symptom of madness. Delirium falsifies intuition; madness falsifies thoughts.

For the most part, the mad do not err at all in their cognition of what is immediately *present*. Rather, their mad talk always relates to what is *absent* and *past*, and only through this to its connection with the present. Therefore their illness seems to me to affect especially the *memory*. Not, to be sure, so that memory is entirely lacking to them; for many know a great deal by heart and sometimes recognize people whom they have not seen for a long time. Rather, the thread of memory is torn, its continuous connection abolished, and no uniformly coherent recollection of the past is possible.

Individual scenes from the past stand there correctly, as does the individual present; but in their recollection there are gaps, which they then fill with fictions. These fictions either remain always the same and become fixed ideas, in which case it is fixed delusion, melancholy; or they are different each time, momentary notions, in which case it is called folly, `fatuitas` (foolishness).

For this reason it is so difficult, when a mad person enters the asylum, to ask him about the course of his earlier life. More and more, truth and falsehood become mixed in his memory. Although the immediate present is cognized correctly, it is falsified by the fictitious connection with a supposed past. Thus they take themselves and others to be identical with persons who exist only in their fictitious past, fail to recognize some acquaintances at all, and so, while having a correct representation of the present individual thing, have only false relations of it to what is absent.

If madness reaches a high degree, complete loss of memory arises. The mad person is then entirely incapable of regard for anything absent or past, and is determined solely by the whim of the moment in connection with the fictions that fill the past in his head. One is then not safe from mistreatment or murder for a single moment unless one constantly keeps superior force before his eyes.

The cognition of the mad person has this in common with that of the animal: both are restricted to the present. But what distinguishes them is this: the animal actually has no representation of the past as such, although the past works on the animal through the medium of habit. Thus, for example, the dog recognizes his former master even after years, that is, receives the accustomed impression from seeing him; but he nevertheless has no recollection of the time that has passed since then.

The mad person, by contrast, always carries around in his reason a past `in abstracto` (in the abstract), but a false one, which exists only for him, either at all times or only just now. The influence of this false past then also prevents the use of the correctly cognized present, which the animal does make.

That violent mental suffering, unexpected and terrible events, frequently occasion madness, I explain in the following way. Every such suffering, as an actual event, is always restricted to the present, and is therefore only transitory; to that extent it is still not excessively severe. It becomes overwhelmingly great only insofar as it is lasting pain. But as such it is again solely a thought, and therefore lies in the *memory*.

If such grief, such painful knowledge or recollection, is so tormenting that it becomes absolutely unbearable and the individual would succumb to it, then nature, so terribly anxious, resorts to *madness* as the last means of saving life. The mind, so greatly tormented, now tears, as it were, the thread of its memory, fills the gaps with fictions, and thus flees from mental pain exceeding its strength into madness, just as one amputates a limb seized by fire and replaces it with a wooden one.

As examples, consider the raging Ajax, King Lear, and Ophelia; for the creatures of genuine genius, to which alone we can appeal here as universally known, are to be set equal to actual persons in truth. Moreover, frequent actual experience shows exactly the same thing here.

A weak analogue of this kind of transition from pain to madness is the fact that all of us often try, almost mechanically, to drive away a tormenting memory that suddenly occurs to us by some loud utterance or movement, to divert ourselves from it, to distract ourselves from it by force.

If, then, as indicated, we see that the mad person correctly cognizes the individual present and also many individual past things, but fails to recognize the connection, the relations, and therefore errs and speaks madness, this is precisely the point at which he touches the individual of genius.

For the individual of genius too, since he leaves behind cognition of relations, which is cognition according to the principle of sufficient reason, in order to see and seek in things only their Ideas, and to grasp their proper essence as it expresses itself intuitively, in relation to which *one* thing represents its whole species and therefore, as Goethe says, one case counts for thousands: the individual of genius too, in doing this, loses sight of cognition of the connection among things. The individual object of his contemplation, or the present grasped by him with excessive vividness, appears in such bright light that, as it were, the other members of the chain to which it belongs recede into darkness; and this gives rise to phenomena that have a long-recognized resemblance to those of madness.

What exists in the individual thing only imperfectly and weakened by modifications, the way of considering things proper to genius heightens into the Idea of that thing, into perfection. He therefore sees extremes everywhere, and for that very reason his actions run to extremes. He does not know how to hit the right measure; sobriety is lacking to him, and the result is what has been described.

He cognizes the Ideas perfectly, but not individuals. Hence, as has been observed, a poet can know *the* human being deeply and thoroughly, while knowing *human beings* very badly; he is easily deceived and is a plaything in the hands of the cunning.[^3]
