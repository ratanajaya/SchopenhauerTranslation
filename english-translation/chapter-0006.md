# §. 4.

Whoever has recognized the form of the principle of sufficient reason that appears in pure time as such, and on which all counting and calculation rests, has thereby recognized the whole essence of time. Time is nothing more than precisely that form of the principle of sufficient reason, and has no other property. Succession is the form of the principle of sufficient reason in time; succession is the whole essence of time.

Whoever, further, has recognized the principle of sufficient reason as it governs mere, purely intuited space has thereby exhausted the whole essence of space; for space is, through and through, nothing other than the possibility of the reciprocal determinations of its parts by one another, which is called *position*. The detailed consideration of this, and the laying down of the results that follow from it in abstract concepts for more convenient application, is the content of the whole of geometry.

In just the same way, whoever has recognized that form of the principle of sufficient reason which governs the content of those forms, time and space, their perceptibility, that is, matter, and therefore has recognized the law of causality, has thereby recognized the whole essence of matter as such. For matter is, through and through, nothing but causality, as everyone immediately sees as soon as he reflects.

The being of matter is its acting; no other being for it is even thinkable. Only as acting does it fill space and time. Its action upon the immediate object, which is itself matter, conditions the intuition in which alone matter exists. The consequence of the action of any other material object upon another is known only insofar as the latter now acts differently than before upon the immediate object; indeed, it consists only in this. Cause and effect, therefore, are the whole essence of matter: its being is its acting. More on this may be found in the essay on the principle of sufficient reason, §. 21, p. 77.

It is therefore highly apt that in German the totality of everything material is called *Wirklichkeit*, actuality, [^1] a word much more expressive than "reality." That upon which matter acts is always again matter. Its whole being and essence therefore consists only in the lawful alteration that one part of matter produces in another. It is consequently entirely relative, according to a relation valid only within its own limits, just like time and just like space.

Time and space, each by itself, can be intuitively represented even without matter; but matter cannot be represented without them. Already the form inseparable from matter presupposes *space*, and its acting, in which its whole existence consists, always concerns an alteration, hence a determination of *time*. But time and space are not merely presupposed by matter, each on its own. Rather, their union constitutes the essence of matter, precisely because that essence, as has been shown, consists in acting, in causality.

All conceivable, innumerable appearances and states could lie side by side in infinite space without crowding one another; or they could follow one another in infinite time without disturbing one another. In that case, a necessary relation of them to one another, and a rule that determined them according to that relation, would be by no means necessary, indeed not even applicable. Consequently, with all coexistence in space and all change in time, so long as each of these two forms had its existence and course by itself, without connection with the other, there would still be no causality at all; and since causality makes up the true essence of matter, there would also be no matter.

The law of causality receives its meaning and necessity only because the essence of alteration does not consist in the mere change of states in themselves. Rather, it consists in the fact that at *the same place* in space there is now *one* state and then *another*, and that at *one and the same* determinate time there is *here* this state and *there* that one. Only this reciprocal limitation of time and space by one another gives meaning, and at the same time necessity, to a rule according to which alteration must proceed.

What is determined by the law of causality, then, is not the succession of states in mere time, but this succession with reference to a determinate space; and not the existence of states at a determinate place, but their existence at this place at a determinate time. Alteration, that is, the change that occurs according to the causal law, therefore always concerns a determinate part of space and a determinate part of time *at once* and in union. Accordingly, causality unites space with time.

But we have found that the whole essence of matter consists in acting, and therefore in causality. Consequently, space and time must also be united in matter. Matter must bear in itself, at once, the properties of time and of space, however much the two conflict with each other. What is impossible in each of those two forms by itself, matter must unite in itself: the unstable flight of time with the rigid, unchanging persistence of space. Infinite divisibility it has from both.

In accordance with this, we find that matter first brings about *coexistence*, which could occur neither in mere time, which has no side-by-side, nor in mere space, which knows no before, after, or now. But the *coexistence* of many states is what really constitutes the essence of actuality. For through coexistence, *duration* first becomes possible, since duration is knowable only in the change of what exists simultaneously with the enduring thing. Yet only by means of what endures in change does change itself receive the character of *alteration*, that is, of the transformation of quality and form while *substance*, that is, *matter*, persists. [^2]

In mere space the world would be rigid and motionless: no succession, no alteration, no acting. But once acting is removed, the representation of matter is removed as well. In mere time, again, everything would be fleeting: no persistence, no side-by-side and therefore no coexistence, consequently no duration; and so once again no matter. Only through the union of time and space does matter arise, that is, the possibility of coexistence, and thereby of duration, and through duration in turn the persistence of substance amid the alteration of states. [^3]

Since matter has its essence in the union of time and space, it everywhere bears the stamp of both. It attests its origin from space partly through form, which is inseparable from it, but especially through its persistence, or substance, since change belongs to time alone, while in time alone and by itself there is nothing enduring. The certainty of this persistence `a priori` (prior to experience) is therefore to be derived entirely from the certainty of space. [^4] Matter reveals its origin from time, however, in quality, or accident, without which it never appears, and which is simply always causality: acting upon other matter, and therefore alteration, a concept of time.

The lawfulness of this acting, however, always refers to space and time together, and has meaning only thereby. What state must occur at *this time* in *this place* is the determination to which alone the legislation of causality extends. On this derivation of the basic determinations of matter from the forms of our cognition of which we are conscious `a priori` rests the fact that we recognize certain properties of matter `a priori`: filling space, that is, impenetrability, that is, efficacy; then extension, infinite divisibility, persistence, that is, indestructibility; and finally mobility. By contrast, gravity, despite its exceptionlessness, must probably be counted as belonging to cognition `a posteriori` (after experience), although *Kant*, in the *Metaphysical Foundations of Natural Science*, p. 71 (Rosenkranz edition, p. 372), presents it as knowable `a priori`.

Just as the object in general exists only for the subject, as its representation, so every particular class of representations exists only for an equally particular determination in the subject, which is called a faculty of cognition. Kant called the subjective correlate of time and space by themselves, as empty forms, pure sensibility. Since Kant broke the path here, this expression may be retained, although it is not quite fitting, since sensibility already presupposes matter.

The subjective correlate of matter, or of causality, for the two are one, is the *understanding*, and it is nothing besides this. To know causality is its only function, its sole power. This is a great power, embracing much, varied in application, yet unmistakably identical in all its manifestations. Conversely, all causality, hence all matter, and therefore the whole of actuality, exists only for the understanding, through the understanding, in the understanding.

The first, simplest, ever-present manifestation of the understanding is intuition of the actual world. This is, through and through, cognition of the cause from the effect; hence all intuition is intellectual. Yet intuition could never arise if some effect were not known immediately and thereby served as the starting point. This effect is the effect upon animal bodies. In this respect they are the *immediate objects* of the subject; the intuition of all other objects is mediated through them.

The alterations that every animal body undergoes are known immediately, that is, sensed. As soon as this effect is referred to its cause, there arises the intuition of that cause as an *object*. This reference is not an inference in abstract concepts. It does not occur through reflection, nor arbitrarily, but immediately, necessarily, and securely. It is the mode of cognition of the *pure understanding*, without which intuition would never arise. There would remain only a dull, plant-like consciousness of the alterations of the immediate object, following one another in complete meaninglessness, unless perhaps as pain or pleasure they had significance for the will.

But just as the visible world stands there with the entrance of the sun, so the understanding, with *one* stroke, through its single, simple function, transforms dull, meaningless sensation into intuition. What the eye, the ear, and the hand sense is not intuition; these are mere data. Only when the understanding passes from the effect to the cause does the world stand there as intuition, spread out in space, changing in form, and persisting through all time in matter. For the understanding unites space and time in the representation *matter*, that is, efficacy. This world as representation exists only through the understanding, and also only for the understanding.

In the first chapter of my essay *On Vision and Colors*, I have already explained how, from the data supplied by the senses, the understanding creates intuition; how, by comparing the impressions that the different senses receive from the same object, the child learns intuition; and how this alone explains so many sensory phenomena: single vision with two eyes, double vision when squinting, or when objects standing one behind another at unequal distances are simultaneously fixed by the eye, and every illusion produced by a sudden alteration in the sense organs.

I treated this important subject much more fully and thoroughly, however, in the second edition of the essay on the principle of sufficient reason, §. 21. Everything said there would have its necessary place here, and so would really have to be said here again. Since, however, I have almost as much reluctance to copy myself as to copy others, and since I am also not able to present it better than was done there, I refer to it instead of repeating it here, but now presuppose it as known.

Children learning to see, people born blind who have been operated on learning to see, single vision of what is sensed doubly with two eyes, double vision and double touch when the sense organs are displaced from their ordinary position, the upright appearance of objects while their image in the eye stands inverted, the transfer of color, which is merely an inner function, a polar division of the eye's activity, onto external objects, and finally the stereoscope: all these are firm and irrefutable proofs that all *intuition* is not merely sensory but intellectual. It is pure *cognition by the understanding of the cause from the effect*. It therefore presupposes the law of causality, on cognition of which all intuition, and hence all experience, depends according to its first and whole possibility, not the reverse, cognition of the causal law depending on experience. The latter was the Humean skepticism, which is only now refuted.

For the independence of cognition of causality from all experience, that is, its apriority, can be demonstrated only from the dependence of all experience upon it. This, in turn, can be done only by showing, in the manner indicated here and developed in the passages just cited, that cognition of causality is already contained in intuition in general, within whose domain all experience lies; that it therefore exists wholly `a priori` with respect to experience, is presupposed by experience as its condition, and does not presuppose experience. It cannot be demonstrated in the way Kant attempted, which I criticized in the essay on the principle of sufficient reason, §. 23.
