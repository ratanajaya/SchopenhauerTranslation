# §. 28.

We have considered the great manifoldness and difference of the appearances in which the will objectifies itself; indeed, we have seen their endless and irreconcilable conflict with one another. Yet, according to our whole account so far, the will itself, as thing in itself, is by no means included in that multiplicity, that change.

The difference of the Platonic Ideas, that is, of the gradations of objectivation; the multitude of individuals in which each of these presents itself; the conflict of forms over matter: all this does not affect the will itself. It is only the way and manner of its objectivation, and has only through this an indirect relation to the will, by virtue of which it belongs to the expression of its essence for representation.

Just as a magic lantern shows many and various images, while it is only one and the same flame that gives visibility to them all, so in all the manifold appearances that fill the world side by side, or displace one another in succession as events, it is still only the *one will* that appears. All this is its visibility, its objecthood, while it remains unmoved in the midst of that change. It alone is the thing in itself; every object, however, is appearance, phenomenon, to speak in Kant's language.

Although the will finds its clearest and most complete objectivation in the human being, as Platonic Idea, this alone could not express its essence. In order to appear with its proper significance, the Idea of the human being could not present itself alone and torn away from everything else. It had to be accompanied by the descending sequence through all the forms of animals, through the plant kingdom, down to the inorganic. Only all of these together complete the full objectivation of the will. They are presupposed by the Idea of the human being as the blossoms of a tree presuppose leaves, branches, trunk, and root. They form a pyramid whose apex is the human being.

If one enjoys comparisons, one may also say that their appearance accompanies the appearance of the human being as necessarily as full light is accompanied by the gradual gradations of all half-shadows through which it loses itself in darkness. Or one may call them the echo of the human being and say: animal and plant are the descending fifth and third of the human being; the inorganic realm is the lower octave.

The whole truth of this last comparison, however, will become clear to us only when, in the following book, we try to fathom the deep significance of music. Then it will appear to us how melody, advancing in connected sequence through high, easily mobile tones, is to be regarded in a certain sense as presenting the life and striving of the human being, which has coherence through reflection. By contrast, the disconnected accompanying parts and the slow-moving bass, from which the harmony necessary to the completeness of music arises, represent the rest of animal and cognitionless nature. But more on this in its place, where it will no longer sound so paradoxical.

We also find that *inner necessity* in the sequence of the will's appearances, inseparable from the adequate objecthood of the will, expressed in the whole of these appearances themselves by an *outer necessity*: namely the necessity by virtue of which the human being needs animals for his preservation, these need one another by grades, then also plants, which in turn need soil, water, chemical elements and their mixtures, the planet, the sun, rotation and revolution around it, the obliquity of the ecliptic, and so on.

At bottom, this springs from the fact that the will must feed on itself, because there is nothing outside it and it is a hungry will. Hence the hunt, anxiety, and suffering.

Just as cognition of the unity of the will, as thing in itself, in the infinite difference and manifoldness of appearances alone gives the true explanation of that wonderful and unmistakable analogy among all productions of nature, that family resemblance which lets us regard them as variations of the same theme, not given along with them, so in the same way a clearly and deeply grasped cognition of that harmony, that essential connection among all parts of the world, that necessity of their gradation which we have just considered, will open to us a true and sufficient insight into the inner essence and significance of the undeniable *purposiveness* of all organic products of nature, which we even presuppose `a priori` (prior to experience) when considering and judging them.

This *purposiveness* is of two kinds. It is partly *inner*, that is, such an ordered agreement of all parts of an individual organism that the preservation of that organism and of its species results from it, and therefore presents itself as the purpose of that arrangement.

But purposiveness is also partly *outer*: namely, a relation of inorganic nature to organic nature in general, or of individual parts of organic nature to one another, which makes possible the preservation of organic nature as a whole, or even of individual animal species, and therefore presents itself to our judgment as a means to that end.

*Inner purposiveness* enters the connection of our consideration in the following way. If, according to what has been said so far, all differences of forms in nature and all multiplicity of individuals belong not to the will, but only to its objecthood and the form of that objecthood, then it necessarily follows that the will is indivisible and wholly present in every appearance, although the grades of its objectivation, the Platonic Ideas, are very different.

For easier comprehension, we can regard these different Ideas as individual and in themselves simple acts of will, in which its essence expresses itself more or less. The individuals, however, are again appearances of the Ideas, and therefore of those acts, in time, space, and multiplicity.

At the lowest grades of objecthood, such an act, or an Idea, retains its unity even in appearance. At the higher grades, by contrast, in order to appear, it needs a whole series of states and developments in time, all of which together first complete the expression of its essence. Thus, for example, the Idea that reveals itself in any universal natural force always has only one simple expression, even if this expression presents itself differently according to external relations. Otherwise its identity could not be demonstrated at all, which is done precisely by separating off the difference that arises only from external relations.

Likewise, the crystal has only *one* expression of life, its shooting into crystal, which afterward has its fully sufficient and exhaustive expression in the solidified form, the corpse of that momentary life. The plant, however, already does not express the Idea whose appearance it is all at once and through one simple expression, but in a succession of developments of its organs in time.

The animal develops its organism in the same way, in a succession of often very different forms, metamorphosis. But this form itself, although already objecthood of the will at this grade, is still not sufficient for the complete presentation of its Idea. Rather, the Idea is first completed by the animal's actions, in which its empirical character, which is the same throughout the whole species, expresses itself. Only this is the full revelation of the Idea, while it presupposes the determinate organism as its basic condition.

In the human being, the empirical character is already peculiar in each individual; indeed, as we will see in the fourth book, it can go as far as the complete cancellation of the character of the species, namely through the self-cancellation of the whole of willing. What is recognized as empirical character through the necessary development in time, and the resulting falling apart into individual actions, is, with abstraction from this temporal form of appearance, the *intelligible character*, in Kant's expression. Kant's immortal merit appears especially splendid in his demonstration of this distinction and presentation of the relation between freedom and necessity, that is, really between the will as thing in itself and its appearance in time.[^1]

The intelligible character thus coincides with the Idea, or, still more properly, with the original act of will that reveals itself in it. To this extent, not only the empirical character of every human being, but also that of every animal species, every plant species, and even every original force of inorganic nature is to be regarded as the appearance of an intelligible character, that is, of an extra-temporal, indivisible act of will.

In passing, I would like to draw attention here to the naivete with which every plant expresses and openly displays its whole character by its mere shape, revealing its whole being and willing. This is what makes the physiognomies of plants so interesting. The animal, by contrast, must already be observed in its action and activity if it is to be known according to its Idea. The human being must be thoroughly investigated and tested, since reason makes him capable of dissimulation to a high degree.

The animal is just as much more naive than the human being as the plant is more naive than the animal. In the animal we see the will to life, as it were, more nakedly than in the human being, where it is clothed with so much cognition and, in addition, veiled by the capacity for dissimulation, so that its true essence comes to light almost only by accident and in places.

In the plant the will shows itself entirely naked, but also much more weakly, as a mere blind drive to existence, without purpose or aim. For the plant reveals its whole essence to the first glance and with perfect innocence, which does not suffer from the fact that it displays at its summit the genitals that, in all animals, have been given the most hidden place. This innocence of the plant rests on its cognitionlessness: guilt lies not in willing, but in willing with cognition.

Every plant first tells of its home, of its climate and the nature of the soil from which it has sprung. Even the little practiced observer therefore easily recognizes whether an exotic plant belongs to the tropical or the temperate zone, and whether it grows in water, in marshes, on mountains, or on the heath. Beyond this, however, every plant also expresses the special will of its species and says something that cannot be expressed in any other language.

But now to the application of what has been said to the teleological consideration of organisms, insofar as it concerns their inner purposiveness. If in inorganic nature the Idea, which everywhere is to be regarded as one single act of will, reveals itself only in one single and always identical expression, so that one can say that here the empirical character immediately participates in the unity of the intelligible character, indeed, as it were, coincides with it, and for this reason no inner purposiveness can show itself there; if, by contrast, all organisms present their Idea through a succession of developments one after another, conditioned by a manifoldness of different parts side by side, so that the sum of the expressions of their empirical character first becomes the expression of the intelligible character when taken together, then this necessary coexistence of parts and succession of development still does not cancel the unity of the appearing Idea, of the act of will expressing itself. Rather, this unity now finds its expression in the necessary relation and linkage of those parts and developments with one another, according to the law of causality.

Since it is the one and indivisible will, and for that very reason the will wholly in agreement with itself, that reveals itself in the whole Idea as in one act, its appearance, although it divides into a diversity of parts and states, must nevertheless show that unity again in the thorough agreement among them. This occurs through a necessary relation and dependence of all parts on one another, by which the unity of the Idea is restored even in appearance.

Accordingly, we now recognize those different parts and functions of the organism reciprocally as means and end of one another, while the organism itself is the final end of them all. Consequently, both the division of the Idea, simple in itself, into the multiplicity of the parts and states of the organism on the one hand, and the restoration of its unity through the necessary connection of those parts and functions, by which they are cause and effect, and therefore means and end, of one another on the other hand, are proper and essential not to the appearing will as such, the thing in itself, but only to its appearance in space, time, and causality, all of them forms of the principle of sufficient reason, the form of appearance.

They belong to the world as representation, not to the world as will. They belong to the way in which the will becomes object, that is, representation, at this grade of its objecthood.

Whoever has penetrated the meaning of this perhaps somewhat difficult discussion will now properly understand Kant's doctrine that both the purposiveness of the organic and the lawfulness of the inorganic are first brought into nature by our understanding, and therefore both belong only to appearance, not to the thing in itself. The astonishment mentioned above at the infallible constancy of the lawfulness of inorganic nature is essentially the same as astonishment at the purposiveness of organic nature. For in both cases what surprises us is only the sight of the original unity of the Idea, which, for appearance, had assumed the form of multiplicity and difference.[^2]

Now as for the second kind of purposiveness, according to the division made above, namely *outer* purposiveness, which shows itself not in the inner economy of organisms but in the support and help they receive from outside, both from inorganic nature and from one another: this too finds its general explanation in the discussion just set out. For the whole world, with all its appearances, is the objecthood of the one and indivisible will, the Idea that is related to all other Ideas as harmony is related to the individual voices. Thus that unity of the will must also show itself in the agreement of all its appearances with one another.

But we can bring this insight to much greater clarity if we go somewhat more closely into the appearances of that outer purposiveness and agreement of the different parts of nature with one another. This discussion will at the same time throw light back on the preceding one. We will reach it best by considering the following analogy.

The character of each individual human being can, insofar as it is thoroughly individual and not wholly contained in the character of the species, be regarded as a special Idea, corresponding to a peculiar act of objectivation of the will. This act itself would then be his intelligible character, while his empirical character would be its appearance. The empirical character is wholly and completely determined by the intelligible character, which is groundless will, that is, will as thing in itself, not subject to the principle of sufficient reason, the form of appearance.

The empirical character must provide in the course of a life the image of the intelligible character, and it cannot turn out otherwise than the essence of the latter requires. But this determination extends only to what is essential, not to what is inessential in the course of life that accordingly appears. What belongs to this inessential element is the more precise determination of the events and actions that are the material in which the empirical character shows itself.

These events and actions are determined by external circumstances, which provide the motives to which the character reacts according to its nature. Since these circumstances can be very different, the outer shape of the appearance of the empirical character, that is, the determinate factual or historical shape of the course of life, must be governed by their influence. This shape can turn out very differently, even though what is essential in this appearance, its content, remains the same. Thus, for example, it is inessential whether one plays for nuts or crowns; but whether one cheats at play or proceeds honestly is the essential matter. The latter is determined by the intelligible character, the former by external influence.

As the same theme can present itself in a hundred variations, so the same character can present itself in a hundred very different courses of life. Yet however varied the external influence may be, however it turns out, the empirical character expressing itself in the course of life must still exactly objectify the intelligible character, adapting its objectivation to the material of factual circumstances it finds before it.

We must assume something analogous to that influence of external circumstances on a course of life determined in its essence by character, if we want to think how the will, in the original act of its objectivation, determines the different Ideas in which it objectifies itself, that is, the different forms of natural beings of every kind among which it distributes its objectivation, and which therefore must necessarily have a relation to one another in appearance.

We must assume that among all those appearances of the *one* will a universal reciprocal adaptation and accommodation to one another took place. But here, as we will soon see more clearly, every determination of time must be omitted, since the Idea lies outside time. Accordingly, every appearance had to adapt itself to the surroundings into which it entered, and these surroundings in turn had to adapt themselves to it, even if that appearance occupies a much later place in time. Everywhere we see this `consensus naturae` (consensus of nature).

Thus every plant is suited to its soil and climate, every animal to its element and to the prey that is to become its food, and is also in some way protected to a certain extent against its natural pursuer. The eye is suited to light and its refrangibility; the lungs and blood to air; the swim bladder to water; the eye of the seal to the change of its medium; the water-holding cells in the camel's stomach to the drought of African deserts; the sail of the nautilus to the wind that is to drive its little boat; and so on down to the most special and astonishing cases of outer purposiveness.[^3]

Here, however, one must abstract from all relations of time, since these can concern only the appearance of the Idea, not the Idea itself. Accordingly, this way of explaining things is also to be used backward, and we must assume not only that every species adapted itself to the circumstances it found, but also that these circumstances, which preceded it in time, had just as much regard for the beings that were someday to come.

For it is one and the same will that objectifies itself in the whole world. It knows no time, since this form of the principle of sufficient reason belongs neither to it nor to its original objecthood, the Ideas, but only to the way in which these are known by the individuals that are themselves transitory, that is, to the appearance of the Ideas.

Therefore, in our present consideration of the way in which the objectivation of the will distributes itself into the Ideas, temporal sequence has no significance at all. The Ideas whose *appearances*, according to the law of causality to which they as such are subject, entered the temporal sequence earlier, thereby have no privilege over those whose appearance enters later. On the contrary, the latter are precisely the most perfect objectivations of the will, to which the earlier had to adapt themselves just as much as these had to adapt themselves to the earlier.

Thus the course of the planets, the inclination of the ecliptic, the rotation of the earth, the distribution of land and sea, the atmosphere, light, heat, and all similar appearances, which are in nature what the thoroughbass is in harmony, adapted themselves, as if with foreboding, to the coming races of living beings whose support and sustainer they were to become. In the same way the soil adapted itself to the nourishment of plants, plants to the nourishment of animals, animals to the nourishment of other animals, just as conversely all of these again adapted themselves to those.

All parts of nature meet one another halfway, because it is *one* will that appears in them all, while temporal sequence is wholly foreign to its original and alone *adequate objecthood*, the Ideas; this expression will be explained in the following book.

Even now, when species only have to preserve themselves and no longer come into being, we occasionally see such provision of nature extending into the future, really abstracting, as it were, from temporal sequence: an adaptation of what is there to what is still to come. Thus the bird builds the nest for young it does not yet know; the beaver erects a structure whose purpose is unknown to it; the ant, the hamster, and the bee gather stores for a winter unknown to them; the spider and antlion set traps, as if with deliberate cunning, for future prey unknown to them; insects lay their eggs where the future brood will find future nourishment.

When, around flowering time, the female flower of the dioecious *Vallisneria* uncoils the spiral turns of its stem, by which it had until then been held at the bottom of the water, and thereby rises to the surface, at exactly the same time the male flower, growing on the bottom of the water on a short stem, tears itself loose from this stem and reaches the surface, sacrificing its life. There, floating around, it seeks the female flower, which then, after fertilization has occurred, withdraws again to the bottom by contracting its spiral, and there the fruit develops.[^4]

Here too I must again mention the larva of the male stag beetle, which bites the hole in the wood for its metamorphosis twice as large as the female does, in order to gain room for its future horns. In general, then, the instinct of animals gives us the best explanation for the rest of nature's teleology. For just as instinct is an action like one performed according to a concept of an end, and yet wholly without such a concept, so all formation in nature is like formation according to a concept of an end, and yet wholly without such a concept.

For in the outer as in the inner teleology of nature, what we must think as means and end is everywhere only the *appearance of the unity of the one will, so far as it is in agreement with itself*, unfolded for our mode of cognition in space and time.

Meanwhile, the reciprocal adaptation and accommodation of appearances that springs from this unity still cannot cancel the inner conflict, essential to the will, that appears in the general struggle of nature described above. That harmony goes only so far as to make possible the *continued existence* of the world and its beings, which without it would long ago have perished. It therefore extends only to the continued existence of species and of the general conditions of life, not to that of individuals.

Thus, by virtue of that harmony and accommodation, the *species* in organic nature and the *universal natural forces* in inorganic nature exist alongside one another and even mutually support one another. By contrast, the inner conflict of the will objectified through all those Ideas shows itself in the unceasing war of annihilation among the *individuals* of those species and in the constant struggle of the *appearances* of those natural forces with one another, as was explained above.

The battlefield and object of this struggle is matter, which they strive to wrest from one another, as well as space and time, whose union through the form of causality is really matter, as was shown in the first book.[^5]
