# §. 22.

This *thing in itself* (we will keep the Kantian expression as a fixed formula), which as such is never an object, precisely because every object is already only its appearance and no longer the thing itself, had to borrow the name and concept of an object if it was nevertheless to be thought objectively. It had to borrow them from something given objectively in some way, and therefore from one of its appearances.

But in order to serve as a point of reference, this appearance could be no other than the most perfect among all its appearances, that is, the clearest, the most fully unfolded, the one immediately illuminated by cognition. This, however, is precisely the human being's *will*.

One must note carefully, however, that here we are indeed using only a `denominatio a potiori` (a naming from the predominant case), through which the concept of will receives a greater extension than it has had until now.

As Plato so often remarks, cognition of the identical in different appearances, and of the different in similar appearances, is the condition of philosophy. Until now, however, the identity between the essence of every striving and acting force in nature and the will had not been recognized. Hence the many appearances that are only different species of the same genus were not regarded as such, but were taken to be heterogeneous. For this reason, no word could exist to designate the concept of this genus.

I therefore name the genus after its preeminent species, whose nearer and immediate cognition leads us to the mediate cognition of all the others. Yet anyone who is unable to carry out the expansion of the concept required here would remain caught in a permanent misunderstanding. Such a person would continue to understand by the word *will* only the one species that alone has been designated by it until now: the will guided by cognition and expressing itself exclusively according to motives, indeed perhaps only according to abstract motives, and therefore under the guidance of reason. As has been said, however, this is only the clearest appearance of the will.

We must now isolate in thought the innermost essence of this very appearance, which is immediately known to us, and then transfer it to all the weaker, less distinct appearances of the same essence. In this way we accomplish the required expansion of the concept of will.

But I would also be misunderstood in the opposite way by someone who thought it was ultimately all the same whether we designate that essence in itself of all appearance by the word will or by some other word. This would be the case if that thing in itself were something whose existence we merely *inferred*, and if we therefore knew it only mediately and only `in abstracto` (in the abstract). Then one could indeed call it whatever one wished; the name would stand as a mere sign for an unknown quantity.

But this is not so. The word *will*, which is meant to unlock for us, like a magic word, the innermost essence of every thing in nature, by no means designates an unknown quantity, something reached by inferences. Rather, it designates something known entirely immediately, and so familiar that we know and understand what will is much better than we understand anything else whatever.

Until now, the concept *will* has been subsumed under the concept *force*. I, by contrast, do exactly the reverse, and want every force in nature to be thought as will. One should not suppose that this is a dispute about words, or something indifferent. On the contrary, it is of the very highest significance and importance.

For the concept *force*, like all other concepts, is ultimately grounded in intuitive cognition of the objective world, that is, in appearance, in representation, and it is drawn from this. It is abstracted from the domain in which cause and effect rule, and therefore from intuitive representation. It signifies precisely the cause's being-a-cause, at the point where aetiological explanation can go no further, and where force is instead the necessary presupposition of all aetiological explanation.

By contrast, the concept *will* is the only one among all possible concepts that has its origin *not* in appearance, *not* in mere intuitive representation, but comes from within. It proceeds from the most immediate consciousness of each person, in which he knows his own individual, according to its essence, immediately, without any form, even without the form of subject and object; and in which, at the same time, he himself is this individual, since here what knows and what is known coincide.

If, therefore, we refer the concept of *force* back to that of *will*, we have in fact referred something more unknown back to something infinitely better known, indeed to the only thing really known to us immediately and entirely. We have thereby very greatly expanded our cognition.

If, on the other hand, we subsume the concept *will* under that of *force*, as has been done until now, then we give up the only immediate cognition we have of the inner essence of the world, by letting it disappear into a concept abstracted from appearance, with which we can therefore never get beyond appearance.
