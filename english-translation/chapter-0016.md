# §. 14.

From all these varied considerations, by which I hope the distinction and relation between the mode of cognition belonging to reason, namely knowledge and the concept, on the one hand, and immediate cognition in pure sensible, mathematical intuition and in apprehension through the understanding, on the other, have been brought to complete clarity; and also from the episodic discussions of feeling and laughter, to which the consideration of that remarkable relation among our modes of cognition almost unavoidably led us: I now return to the further discussion of science as the third advantage, alongside language and deliberate action, that reason gives to the human being. The general consideration of science that concerns us here will deal partly with its form, partly with the grounding of its judgments, and finally also with its content.

We have seen that, apart from the foundation of pure logic, all knowledge in general has its origin not in reason itself, but elsewhere. Gained as intuitive cognition, it is deposited in reason by passing over into an entirely different mode of cognition, the abstract one.

All *knowledge*, that is, cognition raised to consciousness `in abstracto` (in the abstract), stands to genuine *science* as a fragment stands to the whole. Every human being has acquired knowledge about many things through experience, through observation of the individual case that presents itself. But only the person who makes it his task to attain complete cognition `in abstracto` of some kind of objects strives after science.

Only through the concept can he separate out that kind. Therefore at the head of every science stands a concept through which the part is thought out of the whole of all things about which that science promises complete cognition `in abstracto`: for example, the concept of spatial relations; or of the reciprocal action of inorganic bodies; or of the constitution of plants, animals, the successive changes of the earth's surface, the changes of the human race as a whole, the structure of a language, and so on.

If science tried to attain knowledge of its object by investigating individually all the things thought through the concept, until it had gradually come to know the whole, then no human memory would suffice, and no certainty of completeness could be obtained. Therefore science makes use of the peculiarity of concept-spheres discussed above, namely that they include one another. It is chiefly directed toward the wider spheres that lie within the concept of its object in general. Once it has determined their relations to one another, everything thought in them is thereby also determined in general, and can now be determined more and more exactly by separating out ever narrower concept-spheres.

In this way it becomes possible for a science to embrace its object as a whole. This path by which science proceeds to cognition, namely from the universal to the particular, distinguishes it from ordinary knowledge. Therefore systematic form is an essential and characteristic mark of science.

The connection of the most universal concept-spheres of every science, that is, knowledge of its highest propositions, is an indispensable condition for learning it. How far one wants to go from these to the more particular propositions is optional, and increases not the thoroughness but the extent of one's learnedness.

The number of upper propositions to which all the others are subordinate differs greatly in the different sciences, so that in some there is more subordination, in others more coordination. In this respect, the former make greater demands on the power of judgment, the latter on memory. It was already known to the Scholastics [^1] that, because an inference requires two premises, no science can proceed from a single highest proposition that cannot be further derived; rather, it must have several, at least two.

The properly classifying sciences, such as zoology and botany, and also physics and chemistry insofar as these latter reduce all inorganic action to a few fundamental forces, have the most subordination. History, by contrast, really has none at all, since the universal in it consists merely in the overview of the main periods. The particular events cannot be derived from these periods; they are subordinate to them only in time, while in concept they are coordinated. Therefore history, strictly speaking, is indeed knowledge, but not science.

In mathematics, according to the Euclidean treatment, the axioms are the only indemonstrable highest propositions, and all demonstrations are strictly subordinated to them step by step. Yet this treatment is not essential to mathematics. In fact, every theorem begins a new spatial construction that is in itself independent of the preceding ones, and can really be cognized entirely independently of them, from itself, in the pure intuition of space, in which even the most intricate construction is really as immediately evident as the axiom. But of this more fully below.

Meanwhile, every mathematical proposition still remains a universal truth that holds for innumerable individual cases. A gradual progression from simple propositions to complicated ones that can be reduced to them is also essential to it. Thus mathematics is in every respect science.

The perfection of a science as such, that is, in point of form, consists in there being as much subordination and as little coordination of propositions as possible. The general scientific talent is therefore the capacity to subordinate concept-spheres according to their different determinations, so that, as Plato repeatedly recommends, science is not made up merely of a universal with an unmanageable multiplicity placed immediately beneath it side by side. Rather, cognition should descend gradually from the most universal to the particular through intermediate concepts and through divisions made according to ever closer determinations.

In Kant's expressions, this means satisfying equally the law of homogeneity and the law of specification. But precisely from the fact that this constitutes properly scientific perfection, it follows that the purpose of science is not greater certainty; for even the most isolated individual cognition can possess certainty just as fully. Rather, the purpose of science is the easing of knowledge through its form, and the possibility of completeness of knowledge thereby given.

It is therefore a common but mistaken opinion that the scientific character of cognition consists in greater certainty. Just as false is the assertion that arose from this, that only mathematics and logic are sciences in the proper sense because only in them, owing to their complete apriority, is there unshakable certainty of cognition. This latter advantage itself cannot be denied them. But it gives them no special claim to scientificity, which lies not in certainty but in the systematic form of cognition grounded in the stepwise descent from the universal to the particular.

This path of cognition peculiar to the sciences, from the universal to the particular, entails that much in them is grounded by derivation from preceding propositions, and thus by proofs. This has given rise to the old error that only what is proved is completely true, and that every truth requires a proof. On the contrary, every proof requires an unproved truth that ultimately supports it, or supports its own proofs in turn. Therefore an immediately grounded truth is to be preferred to one grounded by proof as water from the spring is to water from the aqueduct.

Intuition, partly pure `a priori` (prior to experience), as it grounds mathematics, and partly empirical `a posteriori` (after experience), as it grounds all other sciences, is the source of all truth and the foundation of all science. The sole exception is logic, which is grounded on reason's non-intuitive but still immediate knowledge of its own laws.

It is not the proved judgments, nor their proofs, but those judgments drawn immediately from intuition and grounded on it instead of on any proof, that are in science what the sun is in the structure of the world. From them all light proceeds; illuminated by it, the others shine in turn.

To ground the truth of such first judgments immediately from intuition, to lift such foundation stones of science out of the unmanageable multitude of real things: this is the work of the *power of judgment*. It consists in the capacity to transfer what is intuitively cognized correctly and exactly into abstract consciousness, and is therefore the mediator between understanding and reason.

Only outstanding strength of this power, exceeding the ordinary measure in an individual, can really advance the sciences. But anyone with sound reason can infer propositions from propositions, prove, and draw conclusions. By contrast, to deposit and fix what is intuitively cognized in concepts suitable for reflection, so that on the one hand what is common to many real objects is thought through *one* concept, while on the other hand what is different in them is thought through just as many concepts; so that the different, despite partial agreement, is nevertheless cognized and thought as different, and again the identical, despite partial difference, is nevertheless cognized and thought as identical, all in accordance with the purpose and point of view prevailing each time: all this is done by the *power of judgment*.

Lack of it is *simple-mindedness*. The simple-minded person now fails to recognize the partial or relative difference in what is identical in one respect, now the identity in what is relatively or partially different.

Moreover, Kant's division of the power of judgment into reflective and subsumptive can also be applied to this explanation, according as it goes from intuitive objects to the concept, or from the concept to those objects. In both cases it always mediates between the intuitive cognition of the understanding and the reflective cognition of reason.

There can be no truth that would have to be brought out unconditionally by inferences alone. Rather, the necessity of grounding it merely by inferences is always only relative, indeed subjective. Since all proofs are inferences, one should seek for a new truth not first a proof, but immediate evidence; and only so long as this is lacking should the proof be set up provisionally.

No science can be demonstrable through and through, any more than a building can stand in the air. All its proofs must lead back to something intuitive and therefore no longer demonstrable. For the whole world of reflection rests on and is rooted in the intuitive world. All ultimate, that is, original *evidence*, is *intuitive*: the word itself already betrays this. Accordingly, it is either empirical, or else grounded on the `a priori` intuition of the conditions of possible experience. In both cases, therefore, it yields only immanent, not transcendent cognition.

Every concept has its value and its existence solely in its relation, however mediated, to an intuitive representation. What holds for concepts also holds for the judgments composed from them, and for whole sciences. Therefore it must somehow be possible to cognize every truth that is found by inferences and communicated by proofs immediately as well, without proofs and inferences.

This is certainly most difficult with some complicated mathematical propositions, to which we arrive only by chains of inference: for example, the calculation of chords and tangents for all arcs by means of inferences from the Pythagorean theorem. Yet even such a truth cannot rest essentially and solely on abstract propositions. The spatial relations lying at its basis must also be capable of being brought out for pure intuition `a priori` in such a way that their abstract statement is immediately grounded. Mathematical proof, however, will be discussed in detail shortly.

One often hears, in a lofty tone, of sciences that rest throughout on correct inferences from secure premises and are therefore unshakably true. But through purely logical chains of inference, however true the premises may be, one will never obtain more than a clarification and explication of what already lies complete in the premises. One will only set out `explicite` (explicitly) what had been understood there `implicite` (implicitly).

By those much-praised sciences people especially mean the mathematical sciences, above all astronomy. The certainty of astronomy, however, comes from the fact that it is grounded on the `a priori` given, and therefore infallible, intuition of space. All spatial relations follow from one another with a necessity, the ground of being, that yields certainty `a priori`; therefore they can be derived from one another with certainty.

To these mathematical determinations there is added here only a single natural force, gravity, which acts exactly in proportion to the masses and to the square of the distance; and finally the law of inertia, secured `a priori` because it follows from the law of causality, together with the empirical datum of the motion impressed once for all on each of these masses. This is the whole material of astronomy. Both through its simplicity and through its certainty, it leads to firm results that, because of the greatness and importance of the objects, are very interesting.

For example, if I know the mass of a planet and the distance of its satellite from it, I can infer the orbital period of the latter with certainty according to Kepler's second law. But the ground of this law is that, at this distance, only this velocity both binds the satellite to the planet and keeps it from falling into it.

Only on such a geometrical basis, that is, by means of an intuition `a priori`, and in addition under application of a law of nature, can one go far with inferences. For here they are, as it were, merely bridges from *one* intuitive apprehension to another. The same is not true of mere and pure inferences on an exclusively logical path.

The origin of the first fundamental truths of astronomy is really induction, that is, the gathering together of what is given in many intuitions into a correct, immediately grounded judgment. From this, hypotheses are afterward formed; and their confirmation by experience, as induction approaching completeness, gives the proof for that first judgment.

For example, the apparent motion of the planets is empirically cognized. After many false hypotheses about the spatial connection of this motion, the planetary orbit, the correct one was finally found; then the laws it follows, the Keplerian laws; and finally also its cause, universal gravitation. The empirically cognized agreement of all occurring cases with these hypotheses and with the consequences drawn from them, that is, induction, gave all those hypotheses complete certainty.

The discovery of the hypothesis was the work of the power of judgment, which correctly apprehended the given fact and expressed it accordingly. Induction, that is, manifold intuition, confirmed its truth. But this truth could even be grounded immediately by a single empirical intuition, if only we could freely traverse the spaces of the universe and had telescopic eyes. Consequently, inferences are not even here the essential and sole source of cognition; they are always really only a makeshift.

Finally, to present a third, heterogeneous example, let us note that the so-called metaphysical truths, that is, truths of the kind Kant sets out in the *Metaphysical Foundations of Natural Science*, also do not owe their evidence to proofs. What is certain `a priori` we cognize immediately: as the form of all cognition, it is conscious to us with the greatest necessity.

For example, that matter persists, that is, can neither come into being nor pass away, we know immediately as a negative truth. For our pure intuition of space and time gives the possibility of motion; the understanding gives, in the law of causality, the possibility of change of form and quality. But for the coming into being or disappearance of matter, we lack forms of representability.

Therefore that truth has at all times, everywhere, and to everyone been evident, and has never seriously been doubted. This could not be the case if its ground of knowing were nothing other than a Kantian proof so difficult that it walks on needle points. Indeed, moreover, I have found Kant's proof false, as is set out in the appendix, and have shown above that the persistence of matter is to be derived not from the share time has in the possibility of experience, but from the share space has in it.

The real grounding of all truths called metaphysical in this sense, that is, abstract expressions of the necessary and universal forms of cognition, cannot itself lie again in abstract propositions. It can lie only in the immediate consciousness of the forms of representation, which announces itself through apodictic `a priori` assertions that fear no refutation.

If one nevertheless wants to give a proof of them, it can consist only in showing that, in some undoubted truth, the truth to be proved is already contained as part or presupposition. Thus, for example, I have shown that all empirical intuition already contains the application of the law of causality; therefore cognition of that law is the condition of all experience, and cannot first be given and conditioned by experience, as Hume claimed.

Proofs are in general less for those who want to learn than for those who want to dispute. These people stubbornly deny immediately grounded insight. Only truth can be consistent on every side; one must therefore show them that under *one* form, and mediately, they concede what under another form, and immediately, they deny: that is, one must show the logically necessary connection between what is denied and what is admitted.

In addition, however, scientific form itself, namely the subordination of everything particular under something universal and so upward without end, brings it about that the truth of many propositions is grounded only logically: namely through their dependence on other propositions, hence through inferences that at the same time appear as proofs. But one should never forget that this whole form is only a means of making cognition easier, not a means to greater certainty.

It is easier to cognize the constitution of an animal from the species to which it belongs, and then upward from the `genus` (genus), family, order, and class, than to investigate the given animal on its own each time. But the truth of all propositions derived by inference is always only conditioned and ultimately dependent on some truth that rests not on inference but on intuition.

If this latter truth always lay as near to us as derivation by inference, it would be absolutely preferable. For all derivation from concepts is exposed to many deceptions, because of the manifold interlacing of spheres shown above and the often wavering determination of their content. So many proofs of false doctrines and sophisms of every kind are examples of this.

Inferences are indeed completely certain in form, but they are very uncertain through their matter, the concepts. For the spheres of these concepts are often not sharply enough determined; and they intersect in such many-sided ways that one sphere is partly contained in many others, so that one can pass arbitrarily from it into one or another of these, and from there onward again, as has already been shown.

Or, in other words, the `terminus minor` (minor term), and also the `medius` (middle term), can always be subordinated to different concepts, from which one chooses the `terminus major` (major term) and the `medius` at will, so that the inference then turns out differently.

Everywhere, therefore, immediate evidence is greatly preferable to proved truth. The latter should be accepted only where the former would have to be brought from too far away, not where it lies just as near, or even nearer, than the proof. For this reason, as we saw above, in logic, where immediate cognition lies nearer to us in every individual case than derived scientific cognition, we always guide our thinking only by the immediate cognition of the laws of thought and leave logic unused.
