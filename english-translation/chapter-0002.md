# [Prefaces]

#### Editor's Preliminary Remarks.

The present collected edition [^1] of the works of *Arthur Schopenhauer* contains, in its six volumes, "everything he ever wrote," arranged according to the plan he himself sketched not long before his death.

It opens, in volumes I and II, with the two volumes of *The World as Will and Representation*, following the "third, improved and considerably enlarged edition" (Leipzig: F. A. Brockhaus, 1859), whose page numbers are inserted in square brackets in our printing. That third edition was the final edition revised by the author, since Schopenhauer died the following year. The additions he wrote into his interleaved copy, or left behind in his manuscript books, have been placed by me beneath the text and marked with a dagger, †. The first volume has only a few additions, which I have reproduced from the fourth edition edited by Dr. Julius Frauenstadt of Berlin, the heir to Schopenhauer's personal copies and manuscripts (Leipzig, 1873), on pages 297, 449, 502, and 527. By contrast, from the relatively numerous additions to the second volume I was able to reproduce several, including some of the longer ones, directly from Schopenhauer's *manuscript*. The relevant passages are all contained in his last manuscript book, the *Senilia*, where they are marked as intended for insertion into a future edition of *The World as Will and Representation*. In the present edition they appear on pages 143, 417, 418, 563, 564, 596, and 757; the remaining additions have been taken from Frauenstadt's fourth edition. For the ways in which, because I have gone back to the original manuscript, *my* text differs from Frauenstadt's, and also for the respects in which this reprint of the "principal work" is distinguished from all posthumous editions, I may refer the reader to the bibliographical appendix in volume VI.

Volume III of the works brings together the shorter writings: "On the Fourfold Root of the Principle of Sufficient Reason," "On the Will in Nature," and the "Ethics." The following two volumes, IV and V, contain *Parerga and Paralipomena*, and in volume VI the essay "On Vision and Colors," together with the `Theoria colorum physiologica` (Physiological Theory of Colors), finds its place. In this final volume I also provide a chronological survey of Schopenhauer's life and writings, as well as the detailed bibliographical account already mentioned, especially concerning my treatment of the text, and, for the first time, an index of names and subjects.

I take this opportunity as well to express publicly my thanks to the Royal Library in Berlin, which for years has allowed me, with the greatest liberality, to study Schopenhauer's handwritten literary remains, bequeathed to it by Dr. Frauenstadt.

*Berlin*, on the thirtieth anniversary<br>
of Arthur Schopenhauer's death.

Eduard Grisebach.

#### Preface to the First Edition.

I have set myself here to say how this book is to be read, if it is possibly to be understood. What is to be communicated through it is a single thought. Yet, despite all my efforts, I have found no shorter way of communicating it than this entire book. I take that thought to be what has long been sought under the name of philosophy, and what, for that very reason, historically educated people consider as impossible to find as the philosopher's stone, although Pliny already said to them: `Quam multa fieri non posse, prisquam sint facta, judicantur? (Hist. nat., 7, 1.)` (How many things are judged impossible before they have been done?)

According as one considers that one thought to be communicated from different sides, it appears as what has been called metaphysics, what has been called ethics, and what has been called aesthetics; and indeed it would have to be all of these, if it is what I have already admitted I take it to be.

A *system of thoughts* must always have an architectonic connection: one in which each part supports another, while the latter does not support the former; in which the foundation stone finally supports all without being supported by them, and the summit is supported without supporting. By contrast, *a single thought*, however comprehensive it may be, must preserve the most perfect unity. If, for the sake of communication, it nevertheless allows itself to be divided into parts, the connection of these parts must again be organic: each part must sustain the whole just as much as it is sustained by the whole; no part may be first or last; the whole thought must gain in clarity through every part; and even the smallest part cannot be fully understood unless the whole has already been understood. Meanwhile a book must have a first and a last line, and in this respect it will always remain very unlike an organism, however much its content may resemble one. Form and matter will therefore stand here in contradiction.

It follows of itself that, under such circumstances, there is no other advice for entering into the thought presented here than to *read the book twice*, and the first time with great patience. That patience can only be drawn from the freely granted belief that the beginning almost as much presupposes the end as the end presupposes the beginning, and likewise that every earlier part almost as much presupposes the later as the later presupposes the earlier. I say "almost," for it is by no means entirely so; and whatever could be done to place first what least needs clarification by what follows, and in general whatever could contribute as much as possible to ease of comprehension and clarity, has been done honestly and conscientiously. Indeed, this might even have succeeded to some degree if the reader, as is very natural, did not think while reading not only of what is said at each moment, but also of the possible consequences of it. In that way, besides the many actual contradictions with the opinions of the age, and probably also with those of the reader, many other anticipated and imaginary contradictions may be added; and then what is merely misunderstanding must present itself as lively disapproval. It is all the harder to recognize it as misunderstanding because the clarity of exposition and definiteness of expression, attained with effort, may leave no doubt about the immediate meaning of what is said, yet cannot at the same time state its relations to everything else. For this reason, as I have said, the first reading requires patience, drawn from the confidence that on the second reading much, or everything, will appear in quite a different light. Moreover, the earnest effort toward complete and even easy intelligibility, in a very difficult subject, must justify an occasional repetition. The organic, not chain-like, structure of the whole already made it necessary to touch the same passage twice at times. This same structure, and the very close connection of all the parts, also prevented me from using the division into chapters and paragraphs which I otherwise value highly; instead it forced me to be content with four main divisions, as it were four points of view on the one thought. In each of these four books, one must take special care not to lose sight, amid the particular details that necessarily have to be treated, of the main thought to which they belong, and of the progress of the whole exposition. With this the first demand, indispensable like those that follow, has been stated to the reader who is not favorably disposed, and who is addressed as a philosopher precisely because the reader himself is one.

The second demand is this: before reading the book, one must read the introduction to it, although that introduction is not included in the book, but appeared five years earlier under the title *On the Fourfold Root of the Principle of Sufficient Reason: A Philosophical Essay*. Without acquaintance with this introduction and propaedeutic, a real understanding of the present work is quite impossible; and the content of that essay is everywhere presupposed here as though it stood within the book. Moreover, if it had not already preceded this work by several years, it would probably not have stood before it as an introduction in the strict sense, but would have been incorporated into the first book. As things are, the first book, lacking what is said in that essay, shows a certain imperfection through these gaps, which it must always fill by referring to that essay. Yet my reluctance to copy myself, or to laboriously say again in other words what had already been said well enough, was so great that I preferred this course, even though I could now give the content of that essay a somewhat better presentation, especially by cleansing it of several concepts that arose from my then excessive entanglement in Kantian philosophy, such as categories, outer and inner sense, and the like. Even there, however, those concepts stand only because I had never yet engaged with them deeply; thus they remain only as a secondary matter, entirely out of contact with the main issue. For that reason, through acquaintance with the present work, the correction of such passages in that essay will occur quite of itself in the reader's mind. But only if one has fully recognized from that essay what the principle of sufficient reason is and means; to what its validity does and does not extend; and that the whole world is not first and foremost that principle, with the world following only as its corollary, in consequence of and in accordance with it, but rather that the principle is nothing more than the form in which the object, always conditioned by the subject, is known everywhere, whatever kind of object it may be, insofar as the subject is a knowing individual: only then will it be possible to enter into the method of philosophizing attempted here for the first time, a method wholly different from all previous ones.

The same reluctance to copy myself literally, or to say exactly the same thing a second time in other and worse words after I had already used the better ones, has caused a second gap in the first book of this work. I have omitted everything that stands in the first chapter of my essay "On Vision and Colors," and that otherwise would have found its place here word for word. Thus acquaintance with that earlier small work is also presupposed here.

The third demand made of the reader could almost be tacitly presupposed: it is nothing other than acquaintance with the most important phenomenon to have appeared in philosophy for two thousand years, and one so close to us: I mean Kant's chief writings. The effect they produce in a mind to which they truly speak seems to me, as I have said elsewhere, very much comparable to the operation for cataracts on a blind person. If we continue the comparison, my aim may be described by saying that I wanted to put cataract glasses into the hands of those on whom that operation has succeeded; the operation itself is therefore the most necessary condition for using them. Thus, much as I proceed from what the great Kant accomplished, the serious study of his writings has nevertheless led me to discover significant errors in them, which I had to separate out and present as objectionable, in order to presuppose and apply what is true and excellent in his teaching in a pure and clarified form. But in order not to interrupt and confuse my own exposition with frequent polemic against Kant, I have placed this in a separate appendix. Just as, according to what has been said, my work presupposes acquaintance with Kantian philosophy, so it also presupposes acquaintance with that appendix. In this respect it would therefore be advisable to read the appendix first, especially since its contents have close relations precisely to the first book of the present work. On the other hand, by the nature of the matter, it could not be avoided that the appendix too should here and there refer to the work itself. From this nothing follows except that the appendix, like the main part of the work, must be read twice.

*Kant's* philosophy, then, is the only philosophy with which a thorough acquaintance is directly presupposed in what is presented here. But if, in addition, the reader has spent time in the school of the divine *Plato*, he will be all the better prepared and more receptive to hearing me. If he has even received and inwardly absorbed the benefit of the *Vedas*, access to which, opened to us through the Upanishads, is in my eyes the greatest advantage this still young century has over earlier ones, since I suspect that the influence of Sanskrit literature will reach no less deeply than the revival of Greek literature in the fifteenth century; if, I say, the reader has also already received and taken in the consecration of ancient Indian wisdom, then he is prepared in the very best way to hear what I have to present. To him it will not speak as something strange or hostile, as it does to many others. For, if it did not sound too proud, I might assert that every one of the individual and disconnected utterances that make up the Upanishads can be derived as a consequence from the thought I am to communicate, although the reverse is by no means true: that thought is not already to be found there.

But already most readers have sprung up impatiently and burst into the reproach that I have held back with such effort for so long: how can I dare to present the public with a book under conditions and demands, of which the first two are arrogant and altogether immodest, and this at a time when there is such a general wealth of original thoughts that in Germany alone three thousand substantial, original, and wholly indispensable works of this kind are made common property each year by the printing press, besides countless periodicals and even daily papers? At a time, in particular, when there is not the slightest lack of entirely original and profound philosophers, but in Germany alone more of them are living at once than several centuries in succession have otherwise been able to show? How, asks the indignant reader, is one to get to the end if one had to proceed so laboriously with a book?

Since I have not the least thing to offer against such reproaches, I only hope for some gratitude from these readers for having warned them in time, so that they lose no hour on a book whose reading could bear no fruit without the fulfillment of the stated demands, and which should therefore simply be left unread; especially since there is much else to wager that it will not please them, and that, rather, it will always be only `paucorum hominum` (for the few) and must therefore wait calmly and modestly for the few whose unusual cast of mind would find it enjoyable. For, quite apart from the lengthiness and the effort it imposes on the reader, what educated person of this age, whose knowledge has come so close to the splendid point at which paradox and falsehood are exactly the same thing, could bear to encounter, almost on every page, thoughts that directly contradict what he has once and for all established for himself as true and settled? And then how unpleasantly many a person will find himself disappointed when he meets here no discussion at all of what he believes he absolutely must seek here, because his way of speculating coincides with that of a still-living great philosopher, [^2] who has written truly moving books and has only the small weakness of taking everything he learned and approved before his fifteenth year to be innate fundamental thoughts of the human mind. Who would care to endure all this? Therefore my advice is simply to put the book away.

Yet I fear that even so I shall not get free of him. The reader who has reached the preface that rejects him has bought the book with good money and asks what will make him whole. My last refuge is now to remind him that one can make use of a book in many ways without actually reading it. It can, like many another, fill a gap in his library, where, neatly bound, it will surely look well. Or he can lay it on the dressing table or tea table of his learned lady friend. Or finally, and this is certainly best of all and what I especially advise, he can review it.

And so, after allowing myself the jest for which, in this thoroughly ambiguous life, scarcely any page can be too serious to make room, I now give the book over with inward seriousness, confident that sooner or later it will reach those to whom alone it can be addressed, and otherwise calmly resigned that it too will fully share the fate that has always fallen to truth in every field of knowledge, and therefore all the more in the most important one: a brief victory celebration is granted to it between the two long periods in which it is condemned as paradoxical and then slighted as trivial. The former fate usually overtakes its author as well. But life is short, and truth works far away and lives long: let us speak the truth.

(Written in Dresden, August 1818.)

#### Preface to the Second Edition.

Not to my contemporaries, not to my countrymen, but to humanity I hand over my now completed work, in the confidence that it will not be without value for humanity, even if that value, as is the lot of what is good in every kind, should only be recognized late. For it could only have been for humanity, not for the passing generation occupied with its temporary delusion, that my mind, almost against my will, has devoted itself without interruption to its work throughout a long life. During that time, the lack of interest could not make me uncertain of the work's value, because I continually saw the false, the bad, and finally the absurd and nonsensical [^3] standing in general admiration and reverence. I reflected that, if those capable of recognizing what is genuine and right were not so rare that one can look around for some twenty years in vain for them, then those capable of producing it could not be so few that their works would later form an exception to the transience of earthly things; and then the strengthening prospect of posterity, which everyone who has set himself a high goal needs, would be lost. Anyone who takes seriously and pursues something that does not lead to material advantage must not count on the interest of his contemporaries. In most cases, however, he will see that meanwhile the semblance of such a thing asserts itself in the world and enjoys its day; and this is in order. For the thing itself must also be pursued for its own sake; otherwise it cannot succeed, because everywhere every intention threatens insight. Accordingly, as literary history testifies throughout, everything valuable has needed much time to gain recognition, especially if it belonged to the instructive rather than the entertaining kind; and meanwhile the false shone. For it is difficult, if not impossible, to unite the thing with the semblance of the thing. This is precisely the curse of this world of distress and need: that everything must serve and pay court to distress and need. For that very reason it is not so constituted that any noble and elevated striving, such as the striving for light and truth, may flourish in it unhindered and exist for its own sake. Even if such a striving has once managed to make itself count, and the concept of it has thereby been introduced, material interests and personal aims will at once seize upon it too, in order to make it their tool or their mask. Accordingly, after Kant had brought philosophy into esteem again, it too soon had to become the instrument of aims: of state aims from above, of personal aims from below; though, strictly speaking, not philosophy itself, but its double, which passes for it. This should not surprise us. For the incredibly great majority of human beings are, by their nature, capable of no aims except material ones; indeed, they can understand no others. Therefore the pursuit of truth alone is far too high and eccentric for it to be expected that all, or many, or even a few, should honestly take part in it. If nevertheless one sees, as just now in Germany, a striking activity, a general bustle of doing, writing, and talking in matters of philosophy, one may confidently assume that the actual `primum mobile` (first mover), the hidden mainspring of such movement, despite all solemn expressions and assurances, consists solely in real, not ideal, aims. Personal, official, ecclesiastical, political, in short material interests are what people have in view; and consequently mere party aims set the many pens of alleged sages of the world into such strong motion. Thus intentions, not insights, are the guiding star of these noisy agitators; and truth is certainly the last thing thought of in the matter. It finds no partisans. Rather, through such philosophical battle-noise, it may pass on its way as quietly and unnoticed as through the winter night of the darkest century, one caught in the stiffest church faith, where it is perhaps communicated only as a secret doctrine to a few adepts, or even entrusted to parchment alone. Indeed, I would say that no time can be more unfavorable to philosophy than one in which it is disgracefully misused on the one side as a means of statecraft and on the other as a means of livelihood. Or does anyone perhaps believe that, in such striving and amid such noise, truth, which is not aimed at there at all, will also casually come to light? Truth is no prostitute who throws her arms around those who do not desire her. Rather, she is so coy a beauty that even the one who sacrifices everything to her may still not be sure of her favor.

If governments make philosophy a means for their political purposes, scholars on the other hand see in professorships of philosophy a trade that feeds its man like any other. They therefore press toward it, while assuring everyone of their good disposition, that is, of their intention to serve those purposes. And they keep their word: not truth, not clarity, not Plato, not Aristotle, but the purposes they have been appointed to serve are their guiding star, and at once also become the criterion of the true, the valuable, the noteworthy, and of their opposites. Whatever does not correspond to those purposes, even if it were the most important and extraordinary thing in their field, is therefore either condemned or, where that seems risky, smothered by unanimous ignoring. One need only look at their unanimous zeal against pantheism: will any fool believe that it springs from conviction? And how could philosophy, degraded into a bread-winning trade, fail to degenerate into sophistry? Precisely because this is unavoidable, and because the rule "Whose bread I eat, his song I sing" has always held good, earning money with philosophy was among the ancients the mark of the sophist. In addition, since in this world nothing but mediocrity is everywhere to be expected, demanded, and had for money, one must make do with it here too. Accordingly, at all the German universities we see dear mediocrity straining to produce, from its own resources, the philosophy that does not yet exist at all, and to do so according to prescribed measure and aim; a spectacle it would almost be cruel to mock.

While philosophy had in this way long been forced everywhere to serve as a means, on the one side for public purposes and on the other for private ones, I have, undisturbed by this, followed my train of thought for more than thirty years, simply because I had to and could not do otherwise, from an instinct-like drive, though one supported by the confidence that whatever one person has thought truly and illuminated from concealment will someday be grasped by another thinking mind, will speak to him, please him, and comfort him. To such a mind one speaks, just as those akin to us have spoken to us and have thereby become our comfort in this wilderness of life. Meanwhile one pursues one's matter for its own sake and for oneself. But the strange thing about philosophical meditations is that precisely only what someone has thought through and investigated for himself later benefits others; not what was originally intended for others. The former is recognizable first of all by the character of thorough honesty, because one does not seek to deceive oneself or hand oneself hollow nuts. In this way all sophistry and all empty verbiage fall away, and as a result every sentence written down at once repays the effort of reading it. Correspondingly, my writings bear the stamp of honesty and openness so clearly on their face that this alone makes them stand out sharply from those of the three famous sophists of the post-Kantian period. One always finds me on the standpoint of *reflection*, that is, of rational consideration and honest communication, never on that of *inspiration*, called intellectual intuition or absolute thinking, but by its proper name bombast and charlatanry. Working in this spirit, and meanwhile constantly seeing the false and bad in general esteem, indeed bombast [^4] and charlatanry [^5] in the highest veneration, I long ago renounced the approval of my contemporaries. It is impossible that an age which for twenty years has proclaimed a Hegel, this intellectual Caliban, as the greatest of philosophers, so loudly that it echoed through all Europe, could make anyone who has seen this eager for its approval. It has no more wreaths of honor to bestow: its approval is prostituted, and its censure means nothing. That I am serious about this is evident from the fact that, if I had in any way sought the approval of my contemporaries, I would have had to strike out twenty passages that run completely counter to all their views, and in part must even offend them. But I would count it a crime to sacrifice even a syllable to that approval. My guiding star has quite seriously been truth. Following it, I could at first strive only for my own approval, wholly turned away from an age deeply fallen with respect to all higher intellectual endeavors, and from a national literature demoralized except for its exceptions, in which the art of combining lofty words with low-mindedness has reached its summit. The faults and weaknesses necessarily attached to my nature, as to every nature its own, I can certainly never escape; but I will not increase them through unworthy accommodations.

As far as this second edition is concerned, I am pleased above all that after twenty-five years I find nothing to retract, and that my fundamental convictions have therefore at least stood the test with me myself. The changes in the first volume, which alone contains the text of the first edition, therefore nowhere touch what is essential. They concern partly only secondary matters, and for the most part consist of usually brief explanatory additions inserted here and there. Only the critique of Kantian philosophy has received significant corrections and extensive additions, since these could not be placed here in a supplementary book, as each of the four books that present my own doctrine has received one in the second volume. I chose this latter form of enlargement and improvement for those books because the twenty-five years that had passed since their composition had brought about such a noticeable change in my manner of presentation and tone of exposition that it would not have been advisable to fuse the content of the second volume with that of the first into a single whole; both would have suffered from such a fusion. I therefore present the two works separately, and in the earlier presentation I have often changed nothing even where I would now express myself quite differently, because I wanted to guard against spoiling the work of my younger years through the fault-finding of age. Whatever may need correction in this respect will, with the help of the second volume, set itself right in the reader's mind. The two volumes stand in a complementary relation to one another in the fullest sense of the word, insofar as this relation rests on the fact that one period of a human life, intellectually considered, is the complement of another. Thus one will find not only that each volume contains what the other does not have, but also that the advantages of one lie precisely in what the other lacks. If, then, the first half of my work has over the second what only the fire of youth and the energy of the first conception can bestow, the second will surpass the first through the maturity and complete working-through of the thoughts, which can belong only to the fruits of a long life's course and its labor. For when I had the strength to grasp the fundamental thought of my system originally, to pursue it at once into its four branches, to return from them to the unity of its trunk, and then to present the whole clearly, I could not yet be in a position to work through all the parts of the system with the completeness, thoroughness, and detail that can be attained only through many years of meditation upon it. Such meditation is required in order to test and illustrate it by countless facts, to support it with the most varied evidence, to illuminate it brightly from all sides, boldly to set contrasting points of view against one another, and to separate and arrange the manifold materials cleanly. Therefore, although it would certainly be more pleasant for the reader to have my whole work cast in a single mold, rather than now consisting of two halves that have to be brought together in use, he should consider that this would have required me to accomplish in one period of life what is possible only in two; indeed, it would have required me in one period of life to possess qualities that nature has distributed between two very different ones. Accordingly, the necessity of delivering my work in two mutually supplementary halves may be compared to the necessity by which an achromatic objective lens, because it is impossible to make it from a single piece, is produced by composing it from a convex lens of flint glass and a concave lens of crown glass, whose united effect alone accomplishes what was intended. On the other hand, the reader will find some compensation for the inconvenience of using two volumes at once in the variety and relief that come from the treatment of the same subject by the same mind, in the same spirit, but in very different years. Meanwhile, for anyone not yet acquainted with my philosophy, it is thoroughly advisable first to read the first volume without consulting the supplements, and to use these only on a second reading; otherwise it would be too difficult for him to grasp the system in its coherence, since only the first volume presents it in that coherence, while in the second the main doctrines are individually grounded and developed more fully. Even someone who cannot decide on a second reading of the first volume will do better to read the second only after the first, and by itself, in the direct sequence of its chapters. These chapters do indeed stand in a connection, though a looser one, whose gaps will be completely filled by the memory of the first volume if he has understood it well. Besides this, he will everywhere find references back to the relevant passages of the first volume, in which, for this purpose, I have in the second edition furnished with paragraph numbers the sections that in the first edition were marked merely by dividing lines.

Already in the preface to the first edition I declared that my philosophy proceeds from Kant's and therefore presupposes a thorough knowledge of it: I repeat that here. For Kant's teaching produces in every mind that has grasped it a fundamental change so great that it may count as a spiritual rebirth. It alone is capable of truly removing the realism that is inborn in him and arises from the original constitution of the intellect; neither Berkeley nor Malebranche is sufficient for this, since they remain too much at the general level, whereas Kant goes into particulars, and indeed in a way that knows neither model nor copy and has a wholly distinctive, one might say immediate, effect on the mind. As a result, the mind undergoes a thorough disillusionment and from then on sees all things in another light. Only through this does it become receptive to the more positive disclosures that I have to give. Anyone, on the other hand, who has not mastered Kantian philosophy remains, whatever else he may have studied, as it were in a state of innocence, namely in that natural and childlike realism in which we are all born and which qualifies one for all sorts of things, but not for philosophy. Such a person therefore stands to the former as a minor to an adult. That this truth sounds paradoxical today, which was by no means the case in the first thirty years after the appearance of the *Critique of Pure Reason*, comes from the fact that since then a generation has grown up that does not really know Kant. To know him requires more than a fleeting, impatient reading, or an account at second hand; and this in turn is because, through bad guidance, that generation has wasted its time on the philosophizings of ordinary, and therefore unauthorized, minds, or even of swaggering sophists whom it was irresponsibly urged to admire. Hence the confusion in first concepts, and in general the unspeakable crudity and coarseness that show through the covering of preciousness and pretentiousness in the philosophical attempts of the generation thus educated. But anyone who imagines he can learn Kant's philosophy from others' accounts of it is caught in a disastrous error. Rather, I must seriously warn against such reports, especially from more recent times; and in these very last years I have encountered accounts of Kantian philosophy in the writings of the Hegelians that truly verge on the fabulous. How could minds distorted and ruined in early youth by the nonsense of Hegelianism still be capable of following Kant's profound investigations? They are early accustomed to taking the emptiest verbiage for philosophical thoughts, the most wretched sophisms for acuteness, and silly absurdity for dialectic; and by taking in raging combinations of words over which the mind tortures and exhausts itself in vain in the attempt to think something, their heads are disorganized. For them no critique of reason is suitable; for them no philosophy is suitable. For them what is needed is a `medicina mentis` (medicine of the mind), first as a purgative perhaps `un petit cours de senscommunologie` (a little course in common-sense-ology), and then one may see whether philosophy can ever again be spoken of in their case. Kant's doctrine, then, will be sought in vain anywhere other than in Kant's own works; these are instructive throughout, even where he errs, even where he fails. In consequence of his originality, what is true of all genuine philosophers holds of him in the highest degree: one learns to know them only from their own writings, not from reports by others. For the thoughts of those extraordinary minds cannot bear filtration through an ordinary head. Born behind broad, high, beautifully arched foreheads, beneath which shining eyes look out, they lose all strength and life when moved into the narrow dwelling and low roof of cramped, pressed, thick-walled skulls, from which dull glances aimed at personal ends peer forth; they no longer resemble themselves. Indeed, one may say that such heads act like uneven mirrors in which everything is twisted, distorted, loses the symmetry of its beauty, and appears as a caricature. Only from their originators themselves can philosophical thoughts be received. Therefore anyone who feels drawn to philosophy must seek out its immortal teachers in the quiet sanctuary of their own works. The chief chapters of any one of these genuine philosophers will provide a hundred times more insight into their doctrines than the dragging and squinting reports about them produced by everyday minds, which are usually also deeply caught in the fashionable philosophy of the moment or in their own cherished opinion. Yet it is astonishing how decidedly the public reaches by preference for those presentations at second hand. Here elective affinity really seems to be at work, by virtue of which common nature is drawn to its like and therefore prefers to hear even what a great mind has said from one of its own kind. Perhaps this rests on the same principle as the system of mutual instruction, according to which children learn best from their peers.

Now one more word for the philosophy professors. I have always had to admire the sagacity, the correct and fine tact with which, as soon as my philosophy appeared, they recognized it as something wholly heterogeneous to their own efforts, perhaps even dangerous to them, or, to speak popularly, as something that did not fit their trade; likewise the sure and shrewd policy by which they at once discovered the only right procedure toward it; the complete unanimity with which they put that procedure into practice; and finally the persistence with which they have remained faithful to it. This procedure, which incidentally also recommends itself by being extremely easy to carry out, consists, as is well known, in complete ignoring and thereby in secreting away, in Goethe's malicious expression, which really means suppressing what is important and significant. The effectiveness of this quiet means is heightened by the Corybantic din with which the birth of the intellectual children of those in agreement is mutually celebrated, and which forces the public to look and to notice the important faces with which they greet one another over it. Who could fail to recognize the expediency of this procedure? After all, there is nothing to object to in the principle `primum vivere, deinde philosophari` (first live, then philosophize). The gentlemen want to live, and indeed to live from *philosophy*: they are referred to *it*, with wife and child, and have ventured upon it despite Petrarch's `povera e nuda vai filosofia` (poor and naked you go, philosophy). Now my philosophy is not at all arranged so that one can live from it. It lacks the first requisites indispensable for a well-paid professorial philosophy, above all any speculative theology, which, in spite of that troublesome Kant with his critique of reason, is supposed to be and must be the chief theme of all philosophy, even if philosophy thereby receives the task of forever speaking about what it absolutely cannot know. Indeed, my philosophy does not even admit the fable so cleverly invented by the philosophy professors, and so indispensable to them, of a reason that knows, intuits, or perceives immediately and absolutely. One need only palm this off on one's readers at the beginning, in order afterward to drive, as comfortably as possible and almost with four horses, into the region beyond the possibility of all experience, which Kant has wholly and forever closed off to our knowledge; and there one then finds the fundamental dogmas of modern, Judaizing, optimistic Christianity directly revealed and most beautifully arranged. What, then, in all the world, has my philosophy, lacking these essential requisites, ruthless and unable to provide anyone a living, brooding, and having as its guiding star only truth, naked, unrewarded, friendless, often persecuted truth, steering straight toward it without looking right or left, to do with that `alma mater` (nourishing mother), the good, nourishing university philosophy, which comes carefully tacking along its way, burdened with a hundred intentions and a thousand considerations, always keeping before its eyes the fear of the Lord, the will of the ministry, the statutes of the established church, the wishes of the publisher, the encouragement of the students, the good friendship of colleagues, the course of daily politics, the momentary direction of the public, and whatever else? Or what does my quiet, serious search for truth have in common with the shrill school-song of lecture chairs and benches, whose innermost driving forces are always personal ends? Rather, the two kinds of philosophy are heterogeneous from the ground up. Therefore there is no compromise and no camaraderie with me, and no one finds his profit with me except perhaps the one who sought nothing but truth; hence none of the philosophical parties of the day do, for they all pursue their own aims, while I have only insights to offer, which fit none of them precisely because they are modeled on none. If my philosophy itself were to become fit for the professorial chair, entirely different times would first have to arise. It would be a fine thing indeed if a philosophy from which one cannot make a living were to gain air and light, perhaps even general attention! This therefore had to be prevented, and everyone had to stand together as one man against it. In contesting and refuting it, however, they do not have such an easy game; and this is a dubious means also because it draws the public's attention to the matter, and reading my writings might spoil for that public the taste for the lucubrations of the philosophy professors. For whoever has tasted seriousness will no longer relish amusement, especially of the tedious kind. Accordingly, the unanimously adopted system of silence and ignoring is the only right one, and I can only advise them to stay with it and continue it as long as it works, that is, until ignoring is someday inferred to be ignorance; then it will still be time enough to change course. Meanwhile everyone remains free to pluck out a little feather here and there for his own use, since the abundance of thoughts at home is not usually very oppressive. Thus the system of ignoring and silence may hold out for a good while yet, at least for the span of time I may still have to live, which is already much gained. Even if here and there an indiscreet voice has made itself heard, it will soon be drowned out by the loud discourse of the professors, who know how to entertain the public, with important faces, about quite other things. I advise, however, that somewhat stricter attention be paid to unanimity of procedure, and especially that young people be watched, since they are sometimes terribly indiscreet. For even so I cannot guarantee that the praised procedure will hold out forever, and I cannot answer for the final outcome. There is, namely, something peculiar about the guidance of the public, which on the whole is good and obedient. Although at almost all times we see the Gorgiases and Hippiases on top, although absurdity regularly culminates, and although it seems impossible that the voice of an individual could ever penetrate the chorus of deceivers and deceived, genuine works nevertheless always have an entirely distinctive, quiet, slow, powerful effect. As if by a wonder, one sees them at last rise out of the tumult, like an aerostat ascending out of the thick atmosphere of this earthly space into purer regions, where, once arrived, it remains and no one can pull it down again.

Written in Frankfurt am Main, February 1844.

#### Preface to the Third Edition.

What is true and genuine would more easily gain room in the world if those who are incapable of producing it were not at the same time sworn together not to let it arise. This circumstance has already hindered and delayed, if not entirely stifled, much that should have benefited the world. For me its consequence has been that, although I was only thirty years old when the first edition of this work appeared, I live to see this third edition no earlier than my seventy-second year. Yet I find comfort in *Petrarch's* words: `si quis, tota die currens, pervenit ad vesperam, satis est (de vera sapientia, p. 140)` (if someone, running all day, reaches evening, it is enough). At last I too have arrived, and I have the satisfaction, at the end of my career, of seeing the beginning of my influence, with the hope that, according to an old rule, it will last in proportion to how late it began.

In this third edition the reader will miss nothing that the second contains, but will receive considerably more, since through the additions made to it, with the same type, it has 136 more pages than the second.

Seven years after the appearance of the second edition, I published two volumes of *Parerga and Paralipomena*. What is included under the latter name consists of additions to the systematic exposition of my philosophy, and would have found its proper place in these volumes; but at that time I had to put it where I could, since it was very doubtful whether I would live to see this third edition. It may be found in the second volume of the aforementioned *Parerga*, and it is easily recognized by the headings of the chapters.

Frankfurt am Main, September 1859.
