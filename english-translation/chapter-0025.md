# §. 23.

The *will* as thing in itself is entirely different from its appearance, and wholly free from all the forms of appearance. It enters into these forms only when it appears; they therefore concern only its *objecthood* and are foreign to the will itself.

Even the most universal form of all representation, that of object for a subject, does not apply to it. Still less do the forms subordinate to this one, all of which have their common expression in the principle of sufficient reason. As is well known, time and space also belong here, and consequently so does the multiplicity that exists and becomes possible only through them.

In this latter respect I will call time and space, using an expression borrowed from the old Scholasticism proper, the `principium individuationis` (principle of individuation), and I ask that this be noted once and for all. For time and space alone are what make something that is the same and one according to essence and concept nevertheless appear as different, as multiplicity alongside and after one another. They are therefore the `principium individuationis`, the object of so much brooding and dispute among the Scholastics, collected in Suarez (*Metaphysical Disputations*, disp. 5, sect. 3).

According to what has been said, the will as thing in itself lies outside the domain of the principle of sufficient reason in all its forms, and is therefore absolutely groundless, although each of its appearances is entirely subject to the principle of sufficient reason. It is also free from all *multiplicity*, although its appearances in time and space are innumerable.

The will itself is one. Yet it is not one as an object is one, whose unity is known only in contrast to possible multiplicity. Nor is it one as a concept is one, which has arisen only through abstraction from multiplicity. Rather, it is one as that which lies outside time and space, outside the `principium individuationis`, that is, outside the possibility of multiplicity.

Only when all this has become fully clear to us through the following consideration of the appearances and different manifestations of the will will we completely understand the meaning of Kant's doctrine that time, space, and causality do not belong to the thing in itself, but are only forms of cognition.

The groundlessness of the will has in fact been recognized where the will manifests itself most clearly, as the will of the human being; and this will has been called free, independent. But immediately, because of the groundlessness of the will itself, people overlooked the necessity to which its appearance is everywhere subject, and declared actions free, though they are not. For every individual action follows with strict necessity from the effect of the motive upon the character.

As has already been said, all necessity is the relation of the consequent to the ground, and absolutely nothing more. The principle of sufficient reason is the universal form of all appearance, and the human being in his action must be subject to it, like every other appearance. But because the will is known immediately and in itself in self-consciousness, consciousness of freedom also lies in this consciousness.

What is overlooked, however, is that the individual, the person, is not will as thing in itself, but is already *appearance* of the will. As such, the individual is already determined and has entered into the form of appearance, the principle of sufficient reason.

This is the source of the strange fact that everyone takes himself `a priori` (prior to experience) to be entirely free, even in his individual actions, and thinks he could at any moment begin a different course of life, which would mean becoming someone else. Yet `a posteriori` (after experience), through experience, he finds to his astonishment that he is not free, but subject to necessity; that despite all his resolutions and reflections he does not change his conduct; and that from the beginning of his life to the end he must carry through the same character that he himself disapproves, and must, as it were, play to the end the role he has taken on.

I cannot develop this consideration further here, since as an ethical matter it belongs elsewhere in this work. For now I wish only to point out that the *appearance* of the will, which is groundless in itself, is nevertheless as appearance subject to the law of necessity, that is, to the principle of sufficient reason. This should prevent us from being disturbed by the necessity with which the appearances of nature occur when we recognize in them manifestations of the will.

Until now, only those changes that have no other ground than a motive, that is, a representation, have been regarded as appearances of the will. For this reason, in nature one attributed will only to human beings, and perhaps to animals, because cognition, representing, is indeed, as I have already mentioned elsewhere, the genuine and exclusive characteristic of animality.

But that the will is also active where no cognition guides it is shown to us most immediately by the instinct and constructive drives of animals.[^1] The fact that they have representations and cognition does not enter into the matter here at all, since the end toward which they work just as if it were a known motive remains entirely unknown to them. Their action here therefore occurs without a motive, is not guided by representation, and shows us first and most clearly how the will is active even without any cognition.

The one-year-old bird has no representation of the eggs for which it builds a nest; the young spider has none of the prey for which it spins a web; nor does the ant-lion have a representation of the ant for which it digs a pit for the first time. The larva of the stag beetle bites the hole in the wood, where it will undergo its transformation, twice as large if it is to become a male beetle as if it is to become a female one, in the former case in order to have room for horns of which it as yet has no representation.

In such action by these animals, as in their other action, the will is plainly active. But here it is active blindly: accompanied by cognition, to be sure, but not guided by it.

Once we have gained the insight that representation as motive is not a necessary and essential condition of the will's activity, we will more easily recognize the working of the will in cases where it is less obvious. We will then be no more inclined to attribute the snail's shell to a will foreign to the snail itself, yet guided by cognition, than to say that the house we ourselves build comes into existence through some will other than our own.

Rather, we will recognize both houses as works of the will objectifying itself in both appearances: in us, according to motives; in the snail, still blindly, as a formative drive directed outward.

The same will also works blindly in us in many ways: in all the functions of our body that no cognition guides, in all its vital and vegetative processes, digestion, circulation of the blood, secretion, growth, reproduction. Not only the body's actions, but the body itself through and through, is, as shown above, appearance of the will, objectified will, concrete will. Everything that takes place in it must therefore take place through will, although here this will is not guided by cognition and does not determine itself according to motives, but, acting blindly, according to causes that in this case are called *stimuli*.

By *cause*, in the strictest sense of the word, I mean that state of matter which, while necessarily bringing about another state, itself undergoes a change just as great as the one it causes. This is expressed by the rule: action and reaction are equal.

Furthermore, in cause properly so called, the effect increases in exactly the same proportion as the cause, and so too does the reaction. Thus, once the kind of effect is known, the degree of the effect can be measured and calculated from the degree of intensity of the cause, and conversely. Such causes, properly so called, operate in all appearances of mechanism, chemistry, and so on; in short, in all changes of inorganic bodies.

By contrast, I call a *stimulus* that cause which itself undergoes no reaction proportionate to its effect, and whose intensity does not run parallel in degree with the intensity of the effect, so that the effect cannot be measured by the stimulus. Instead, a small increase in the stimulus may produce a very great increase in the effect, or, conversely, may entirely abolish the previous effect, and so on.

All effects upon organic bodies as such are of this kind. Thus all properly organic and vegetative changes in the animal body proceed upon stimuli, not upon mere causes. But the stimulus, like every cause in general, and likewise the motive, never determines more than the point of entry of the expression of each force in time and space. It does not determine the inner essence of the force that expresses itself. In accordance with our previous derivation, we recognize this inner essence as will, and to it we therefore attribute both the unconscious and the conscious changes of the body.

The stimulus holds the middle position and makes the transition between the motive, which is causality that has passed through cognition, and cause in the strict sense. In individual cases it lies closer now to the motive, now to the cause, though it is still always to be distinguished from both.

For example, the rising of sap in plants occurs upon stimulus and cannot be explained by mere causes, according to the laws of hydraulics or capillary tubes. Nevertheless, it is probably supported by these and is in general already very close to purely causal change. By contrast, the movements of `Hedysarum gyrans` and `Mimosa pudica`, although still occurring upon mere stimuli, are nevertheless already very similar to movements upon motives and seem almost ready to make the transition to them.

The narrowing of the pupil under increased light occurs upon stimulus, but already passes over into movement upon motive, since it happens because light that is too strong would painfully affect the retina, and in order to avoid this we contract the pupil.

The occasion of erection is a motive, since it is a representation. Yet it acts with the necessity of a stimulus; that is, it cannot be resisted, and one must remove it in order to render it ineffective. The same holds for disgusting objects, which arouse the inclination to vomit.

We have just considered animal instinct as a true middle term of an entirely different kind between movement upon stimulus and action according to a known motive. One might be tempted to regard breathing as another middle term of this kind. For there has been dispute over whether it belongs to voluntary or involuntary movements, that is, in truth, whether it occurs upon motive or stimulus; and accordingly it might perhaps be explained as something intermediate between the two.

Marshall Hall, in *On the Diseases of the Nervous System*, § 293 and following, declares it a mixed function, since it stands under the influence partly of the cerebral, or voluntary, nerves and partly of the spinal, or involuntary, nerves. Nevertheless, in the end we must count it among expressions of will that occur upon motive. For other motives, that is, mere representations, can determine the will to hold breathing back or hasten it; and breathing has, like every other voluntary action, the appearance that one could entirely omit it and freely suffocate.

One could in fact do this as soon as some other motive determined the will so strongly that it outweighed the pressing need for air. According to some, Diogenes really ended his life in this way (`Diog. Laert. VI, 76`). Black people are also said to have done this (F. B. Osiander, *On Suicide* [1813], pp. 170-180). In this we would have a strong example of the influence of abstract motives, that is, of the superiority of properly rational willing over merely animal willing.

The fact that breathing is at least partly conditioned by cerebral activity is supported by the fact that hydrocyanic acid kills first by paralyzing the brain and thus indirectly inhibiting breathing. But if breathing is artificially maintained until that stupefaction of the brain has passed, no death at all occurs.

At the same time, breathing incidentally gives us here the most obvious example of the fact that motives act with just as much necessity as stimuli and mere causes in the strict sense, and can be rendered ineffective only by contrary motives, just as pressure is counteracted by counterpressure. For in breathing, the appearance that one can refrain from it is incomparably weaker than in other movements that occur upon motives. This is because the motive there is very urgent and very near, its satisfaction is very easy because of the tirelessness of the muscles that carry it out, as a rule nothing opposes it, and the whole process is supported by the individual's oldest habit.

And yet all motives really act with the same necessity. The recognition that necessity is common to movements upon motives and to those upon stimuli will make it easier for us to see that what occurs in the organic body upon stimuli and with complete conformity to law is nevertheless, according to its inner essence, will. Though the will is never subject in itself to the principle of sufficient reason, it is subject to that principle, that is, to necessity, in all its appearances.[^2]

Accordingly, we will not stop at recognizing animals, in their action as well as in their whole existence, embodiment, and organization, as appearance of will. We will also transfer this immediate cognition, which alone is given to us, of the essence in itself of things to plants, whose every movement occurs upon stimuli. The absence of cognition, and of movement upon motives conditioned by cognition, is the only essential difference between animal and plant.

Thus what appears for representation as plant, as mere vegetation, as blindly driving force, we will call, according to its essence in itself, will. We will recognize it as precisely what constitutes the basis of our own appearance, as this basis expresses itself in our action and already in the entire existence of our body itself.

Only the final step remains: to extend our way of considering things also to all those forces that act in nature according to universal, unalterable laws, in accordance with which all the movements of bodies occur, bodies that, entirely without organs, have no receptivity for stimulus and no cognition for motive. We must therefore apply the key to understanding the essence in itself of things, a key that only immediate cognition of our own essence could give us, also to these appearances of the inorganic world, which stand at the greatest distance from us.

If we now consider them with an inquiring gaze; if we see the powerful, unstoppable drive with which waters hurry toward the depths, the persistence with which the magnet always turns back toward the north pole, the longing with which iron flies toward it, the vehemence with which the poles of electricity strive for reunion, and which, just like human desire, is intensified by obstacles; if we see the crystal shoot up quickly and suddenly, with such regularity of formation, which is plainly only a completely decided and precisely determined striving in different directions, seized and held fast by solidification; if we notice the selection by which bodies, set free by the fluid state and released from the bonds of rigidity, seek and flee one another, unite and separate; if, finally, we feel quite immediately how a weight, whose striving toward the mass of the earth our body obstructs, presses and pushes upon it without ceasing, pursuing its one endeavor: then it will cost us no great effort of imagination, even from so great a distance, to recognize our own essence again.

It is the very same essence that in us pursues its ends in the light of cognition, but here, in the weakest of its appearances, strives only blindly, dimly, one-sidedly, and unchangeably. Yet because it is everywhere one and the same, just as the first dawn shares the name sunlight with the rays of full noon, it must here as there bear the name *will*. This word designates what is the being in itself of every thing in the world and the sole kernel of every appearance.

The distance, however, indeed the appearance of a complete difference between the appearances of inorganic nature and the will that we perceive as the inner being of our own essence, arises chiefly from the contrast between wholly determinate conformity to law in the one kind of appearance and apparently lawless arbitrariness in the other.

For in the human being, individuality comes powerfully to the fore. Each person has a character of his own; hence the same motive does not have the same force over everyone. A thousand secondary circumstances, which have room in the individual's wide sphere of cognition but remain unknown to others, modify the effect of the motive. For this reason, the action cannot be predicted from the motive alone, because the other factor is missing: exact knowledge of the individual character and of the cognition that accompanies it.

By contrast, the appearances of natural forces display the other extreme. They act according to universal laws, without deviation, without individuality, according to openly evident circumstances, subject to the most exact predetermination; and the same natural force expresses itself in millions of its appearances in exactly the same way.

In order to clarify this point, and to demonstrate the identity of the *one* and indivisible will in all its very different appearances, in the weakest as in the strongest, we must first consider the relation that the will as thing in itself has to its appearance, that is, the relation that the world as will has to the world as representation. This will open for us the best path to a deeper investigation of the whole subject treated in this second book.[^3]
