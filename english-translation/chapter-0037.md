# §. 35.

In order to gain a deeper insight into the essence of the world, it is absolutely necessary to learn to distinguish the will as thing in itself from its adequate objecthood; then to distinguish the different grades on which this objecthood comes forward more clearly and completely, that is, the Ideas themselves, from the mere appearance of the Ideas in the forms of the principle of sufficient reason, the mode of cognition in which individuals are confined.

Then one will agree with Plato when he ascribes genuine being only to the Ideas, but grants to things in space and time, this world that is real for the individual, only an apparent, dreamlike existence. Then one will see how one and the same Idea reveals itself in so many appearances, and presents its essence to knowing individuals only piece by piece, one side after another. One will then also distinguish the Idea itself from the way its appearance falls under the observation of the individual, recognizing the former as essential and the latter as inessential.

Let us consider this first in the smallest things, and then in the greatest.

When clouds move, the shapes they form are not essential to them; they are indifferent to them. But that, as elastic vapor, they are compressed by the thrust of the wind, driven away, expanded, torn apart: this is their nature, the essence of the forces that objectify themselves in them, the Idea. The particular shapes at any given time exist only for the individual observer.

For the brook that rolls downward over stones, the eddies, waves, and foam formations it shows are indifferent and inessential. That it follows gravity and behaves as an inelastic, wholly displaceable, formless, transparent fluid: this is its essence, this is the Idea, *if cognized intuitively*. Those formations exist only for us, so long as we cognize as individuals.

The ice on the windowpane shoots forth according to the laws of crystallization, which reveal the essence of the natural force appearing here and present the Idea. But the trees and flowers it forms in doing so are inessential and exist only for us.

What appears in clouds, brook, and crystal is the weakest echo of that will which comes forward more perfectly in the plant, still more perfectly in the animal, and most perfectly in the human being. But only what is *essential* in all those grades of its objectivation constitutes the *Idea*. By contrast, the unfolding of the Idea, as it is drawn apart into manifold and many-sided appearances in the forms of the principle of sufficient reason, is inessential to the Idea. It lies merely in the mode of cognition of the individual, and has reality only for this individual.

The same necessarily also holds for the unfolding of that Idea which is the most perfect objecthood of the will. Consequently the history of the human race, the throng of events, the change of times, the many-shaped forms of human life in different lands and centuries: all this is only the accidental form of the appearance of the Idea. It does not belong to the Idea itself, in which alone the adequate objecthood of the will lies, but only to the appearance that falls into the cognition of the individual. It is as foreign, inessential, and indifferent to the Idea itself as the shapes they portray are to the clouds, the form of its eddies and foam formations to the brook, and its trees and flowers to the ice.

Whoever has grasped this well, and knows how to distinguish the will from the Idea, and the Idea from its appearance, will find that world-events have significance only insofar as they are the letters from which the Idea of humanity can be read, but not in and for themselves. He will not believe, with people generally, that time produces something actually new and significant; that through it or in it something absolutely real comes into existence; or even that time itself, as a whole, has beginning and end, plan and development, and perhaps as its final goal the highest perfection, according to their concepts, of the last generation, which lives for thirty years.

Therefore he will no more appoint with Homer an entire Olympus full of gods to direct those events in time than he will, with Ossian, take the shapes of the clouds for individual beings; for, as has been said, both have equal significance in relation to the Idea appearing in them. In the manifold shapes of human life and the unceasing change of events, he will consider only the Idea as the enduring and essential thing. In this Idea the will to life has its most perfect objecthood, and it shows its different sides in the qualities, passions, errors, and excellences of the human race: in self-interest, hatred, love, fear, boldness, frivolity, dullness, cunning, wit, genius, and so on. All these, flowing together and congealing into a thousandfold variety of forms, that is, individuals, continually perform the great and the small history of the world. In itself it makes no difference whether what sets them in motion is nuts or crowns.

Finally, he will find that things are in the world as they are in Gozzi's dramas, in all of which the same characters always appear, with the same intention and the same fate. The motives and events are, of course, different in each play; but the spirit of the events is the same. The characters of one play also know nothing of the events in another, even though they themselves acted in it. Therefore, after all the experiences of the earlier plays, Pantalone has still not become more agile or more generous, Tartaglia more conscientious, Brighella more courageous, or Colombina more modest.

Suppose we were once granted a clear view into the realm of possibility and over all the chains of causes and effects; suppose the Earth-spirit came forward and showed us in one image the most excellent individuals, illuminators of the world and heroes, whom chance destroyed before the time of their effectiveness; then the great events that would have changed world history and brought about periods of the highest culture and enlightenment, but which the blindest contingency, the most insignificant accident, obstructed at their very beginning; finally the glorious powers of great individuals, which would have made whole ages fruitful, but which they, misled by error or passion, or forced by necessity, uselessly squandered on unworthy and barren objects, or even wasted in play: if we saw all this, we would shudder and lament over the lost treasures of whole ages.

But the Earth-spirit would smile and say: "The source from which individuals and their powers flow is inexhaustible and infinite, like time and space; for they, just like these forms of all appearance, are after all only appearance, visibility of the will's activity. No finite measure can exhaust that infinite source. Therefore, for every event or work stifled in the seed, the undiminished infinity still always stands open for its return. In this world of appearance, true loss is just as impossible as true gain. The will alone is: it, the thing in itself, it is the source of all those appearances. Its self-cognition, and the affirmation or denial that decides itself on the basis of this self-cognition, is the only event in itself."[^1]

[^1]: This last sentence cannot be understood without acquaintance with the following book.
