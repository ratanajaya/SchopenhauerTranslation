# §. 34.

The transition, already described as possible but to be regarded only as an exception, from common cognition of individual things to cognition of the Idea occurs suddenly, when cognition tears itself free from the service of the will. By this very fact the subject ceases to be merely individual, and is now the pure, will-less subject of cognition, which no longer pursues relations according to the principle of sufficient reason, but rests in fixed contemplation of the presented object, outside its connection with anything else, and is absorbed in it.

In order to become clear, this necessarily requires a detailed explanation. For the time being one must get past whatever seems strange in it, until that strangeness disappears of itself once the whole thought to be communicated in this work has been grasped.

When, lifted by the power of the mind, one lets go of the ordinary way of considering things; when one stops pursuing only their relations to one another, whose final goal is always their relation to one's own will, along the guiding thread of the forms of the principle of sufficient reason; when, therefore, one no longer considers the where, the when, the why, and the for-what of things, but solely and exclusively the *what*; when, moreover, one does not let abstract thinking, the concepts of reason, take possession of consciousness, but instead gives the whole power of one's mind over to intuition, immerses oneself entirely in it, and lets the whole of consciousness be filled by the calm contemplation of the natural object just present, whether a landscape, a tree, a rock, a building, or whatever else it may be; when, according to a meaningful German expression, one wholly *loses oneself* in this object, that is, precisely forgets one's individuality, one's will, and remains existing only as pure subject, as clear mirror of the object; so that it is as if the object alone were there, without anyone perceiving it, and one can therefore no longer separate the beholder from the intuition, but the two have become one, since the whole of consciousness is completely filled and occupied by a single intuitive image: then, when in this way the object has stepped out of all relation to anything outside it, and the subject has stepped out of all relation to the will, what is cognized is no longer the individual thing as such. Rather, it is the *Idea*, the eternal form, the immediate objecthood of the will at this grade.

And precisely through this, the one absorbed in this intuition is at the same time no longer individual; for the individual has lost himself in such intuition. Instead, he is the *pure*, will-less, painless, timeless *subject of cognition*.

This, taken by itself, is now so striking that I know very well it confirms the saying that comes from Thomas Paine, `du sublime au ridicule il n'y a qu'un pas` (from the sublime to the ridiculous there is only one step). But what follows will gradually make it clearer and less strange. This was also what hovered before Spinoza when he wrote: `mens aeterna est, quatenus res sub aeternitatis specie concipit (Eth. V, pr. 31, schol.)` (the mind is eternal insofar as it conceives things under the aspect of eternity).[^1]

In such contemplation, then, at one stroke the individual thing becomes the *Idea* of its species, and the intuiting individual becomes the *pure subject of cognition*. The individual as such cognizes only individual things; the pure subject of cognition cognizes only Ideas.

For the individual is the subject of cognition in its relation to a determinate individual appearance of the will, and is serviceable to this appearance. This individual appearance of will, as such, is subject to the principle of sufficient reason in all its forms. Therefore all cognition relating to it also follows the principle of sufficient reason, and for the purposes of the will no cognition is useful except this one, which always has only relations to the object. The knowing individual as such, and the individual thing known by him, are always somewhere, sometime, and members in the chain of causes and effects.

The pure subject of cognition and its correlate, the Idea, have stepped out of all those forms of the principle of sufficient reason. Time, place, the individual that cognizes, and the individual that is cognized have no significance for them.

Only when, in the way described, a knowing individual raises himself to the pure subject of cognition, and precisely thereby the object considered raises itself to the Idea, does the *world as representation* emerge wholly and purely, and the complete objectivation of the will take place, since the Idea alone is its *adequate objecthood*. The Idea includes object and subject in itself in equal measure, since these are its only form; but in it the two hold one another in perfect equilibrium. Just as here too the object is nothing but the representation of the subject, so also the subject, by being wholly absorbed in the intuited object, has itself become this object, since the whole of consciousness is now nothing but that object's clearest image.

This very consciousness, if one thinks all the Ideas, or grades of the objecthood of the will, as passing through it in sequence, is what properly makes up the whole *world as representation*. The individual things of all times and spaces are nothing but the Ideas multiplied by the principle of sufficient reason, the form of cognition of individuals as such, and thereby clouded in their pure objecthood.

When the Idea emerges, subject and object can no longer be distinguished in it, because only when they completely fill and interpenetrate one another does the Idea, the adequate objecthood of the will, the world as representation proper, arise. In just the same way, the individual that cognizes in this process and the individual that is cognized are, as things in themselves, not distinct. For if we entirely abstract from that world as representation proper, nothing remains except the *world as will*.

The will is the in-itself of the Idea, which objectifies it perfectly. It is also the in-itself of the individual thing and of the individual that cognizes it, both of which objectify it imperfectly. As will, outside representation and all its forms, it is one and the same in the contemplated object and in the individual who, raising himself in this contemplation, becomes conscious of himself as pure subject. Therefore those two are not distinct in themselves; for in themselves they are the will, which here cognizes itself. Multiplicity and difference exist only as the manner in which this cognition comes to the will, that is, only in appearance, by virtue of appearance's form, the principle of sufficient reason.

Just as little as I, without the object, without the representation, am a knowing subject, but merely blind will, so too the thing known, without me as subject of cognition, is not object, but merely will, blind drive. This will is in itself, that is, outside representation, one and the same with mine. Only in the world as representation, whose form is always at least subject and object, do we separate into cognized and cognizing individual.

As soon as cognition, the world as representation, is abolished, nothing at all remains except mere will, blind drive. That the will should obtain objecthood, should become representation, presupposes, at one stroke, both subject and object. But that this objecthood should be pure, complete, adequate objecthood of the will presupposes the object as Idea, free from the forms of the principle of sufficient reason, and the subject as pure subject of cognition, free from individuality and serviceability to the will.

Whoever, in the way described, has sunk and lost himself so deeply in the intuition of nature that he exists only as purely knowing subject, thereby becomes immediately aware that, as such, he is the condition, and therefore the bearer, of the world and of all objective existence, since this now presents itself as dependent on his own existence. He therefore draws nature into himself, so that he feels it now only as an accident of his being. In this sense *Byron* says:

> Are not the mountains, waves, and skies, a part<br>
> Of me and of my soul, as I of them?[^2]

But how could someone who feels this regard himself, in contrast with imperishable nature, as absolutely perishable? Rather, he will be seized by the consciousness of what the Upanishad of the Veda expresses: `Hae omnes creaturae in totum ego sum, et praeter me aliud ens non est. (Oupnekhat, I, 122.)` (All these creatures, in their totality, are I, and apart from me there is no other being).[^3]
