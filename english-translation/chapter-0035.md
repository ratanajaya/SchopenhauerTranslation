# §. 33.

Since, then, as individuals we have no cognition other than cognition subject to the principle of sufficient reason, and since this form excludes cognition of the Ideas, it is certain that, if it is possible for us to rise from cognition of individual things to cognition of Ideas, this can happen only through a change taking place in the subject. This change must correspond and be analogous to that great change in the whole kind of the object; and by virtue of it, the subject, insofar as it cognizes an Idea, is no longer individual.

We will remember from the preceding book that cognition in general itself belongs to the objectivation of the will at its higher grades, and that sensibility, nerves, and brain are, like the other parts of the organic being, simply the expression of the will at this grade of its objecthood. Therefore the representation that arises through them is likewise destined for the will's service, as a means, a `μηχανη` (contrivance), for attaining its now more complicated (`πολυτελεστεϱα`, more elaborate) ends, and for preserving a being that has many different needs.

Originally, then, and according to its essence, cognition is entirely serviceable to the will. Just as the immediate object, which becomes cognition's starting point through application of the law of causality, is only objectified will, so too all cognition that proceeds according to the principle of sufficient reason remains in a nearer or more distant relation to the will. For the individual finds his body as an object among objects, to all of which this body has many different relations and connections according to the principle of sufficient reason. Consideration of these relations therefore always leads back, by a nearer or more distant path, to his body, and thus to his will.

Since it is the principle of sufficient reason that places objects in this relation to the body, and thereby to the will, the cognition that serves the will will also strive only to learn, from objects, precisely the relations established by the principle of sufficient reason. It will therefore follow their many different relations in space, time, and causality. For only through these is the object *interesting* to the individual, that is, does it have a relation to the will.

Consequently cognition in the service of the will really cognizes nothing more in objects than their relations. It cognizes objects only insofar as they exist at this time, in this place, under these circumstances, from these causes, with these effects: in a word, as individual things. If all these relations were removed, then the objects too would have vanished for this cognition, precisely because it cognized nothing else in them.

Nor may we conceal that what the sciences consider in things is, in essence, likewise nothing other than all this: their relations, the relations of time and space, the causes of natural changes, the comparison of forms, the motives of events, and thus nothing but relations. What distinguishes science from common cognition is only its form, its systematic character, the easing of cognition by comprehending everything individual under the universal through the subordination of concepts, and the completeness thereby attained.

Every relation itself has only a relative existence. For example, every being in time is also, in turn, a non-being; for time is precisely that by which opposed determinations can belong to the same thing. Hence every appearance in time is also, in turn, not; for what separates its beginning from its end is just time, something essentially passing away, unstable, and relative, here called duration. Time, however, is the most universal form of all objects of cognition that stands in the service of the will, and the archetype of its other forms.

As a rule, then, cognition always remains subject to the service of the will, since it arose for this service, indeed sprang from the will, so to speak, as the head springs from the trunk. In animals, this servitude of cognition under the will can never be abolished. In human beings, such abolition occurs only as an exception, as we will examine more closely at once.

This difference between human being and animal is expressed outwardly by the different relation of the head to the trunk. In the lower animals the two are still entirely fused together. In all animals the head is directed toward the earth, where the objects of the will lie. Even in the higher animals, head and trunk are still much more one than they are in the human being, whose head appears freely set upon the body, merely carried by it, not serving it.

This human privilege is displayed in the highest degree by the Apollo Belvedere: the far-seeing head of the god of the Muses stands so freely on the shoulders that it appears entirely wrested from the body, no longer subject to concern for it.
