# §. 5.

One must guard, however, against the great misunderstanding that, because intuition is mediated by cognition of causality, there is therefore a relation of cause and effect between object and subject. On the contrary, such a relation always occurs only between immediate and mediated object, and thus always only between objects.

That false presupposition is precisely what underlies the foolish dispute over the reality of the external world, in which dogmatism and skepticism confront one another, while dogmatism appears now as realism, now as idealism. Realism posits the object as cause and places its effect in the subject. Fichtean idealism makes the object into the effect of the subject.

But since, and this cannot be impressed strongly enough, no relation according to the principle of sufficient reason obtains between subject and object at all, neither of these two assertions could ever be proved, and skepticism made victorious attacks on both.

Just as the law of causality already precedes intuition and experience as their condition, and therefore cannot be learned from them, as Hume thought, so object and subject already precede all cognition as its first condition, and therefore also precede the principle of sufficient reason in general. For that principle is only the form of every object, the pervasive way and manner of its appearing; but the object always already presupposes the subject. Between the two, then, there can be no relation of ground and consequent.

My essay on the principle of sufficient reason is meant to accomplish precisely this: to present the content of that principle as the essential form of every object, that is, as the universal way and manner of all object-being, as something that belongs to the object as such. As such, however, the object everywhere presupposes the subject as its necessary correlate. The subject therefore always remains outside the domain in which the principle of sufficient reason is valid.

The dispute over the reality of the external world rests precisely on that false extension of the validity of the principle of sufficient reason to the subject as well; and, beginning from this misunderstanding, the dispute could never understand itself.

On the one hand, realistic dogmatism, taking representation to be the effect of the object, wants to separate these two, representation and object, which are precisely one and the same. It wants to assume a cause wholly different from representation: an object in itself, independent of the subject. This is something entirely unthinkable, for already as object it always presupposes the subject again, and therefore always remains only the subject's representation.

Skepticism, under the same false presupposition, opposes this by saying that in representation one always has only the effect, never the cause; that one therefore never knows the *being* of objects, but always only their *acting*; that this acting might perhaps have no similarity at all with being, and perhaps might even be assumed quite falsely, since the law of causality is first taken from experience, whose reality is then supposed to rest on that very law.

Both sides must now be instructed, first, that object and representation are the same; then, that the *being* of intuitive objects is precisely their *acting*; that the actuality of the thing consists precisely in this; and that the demand for the existence of the object outside the representation of the subject, as well as for a being of the actual thing different from its acting, has no meaning at all and is a contradiction. Therefore, cognition of the mode of action of an intuited object also exhausts the object itself insofar as it is object, that is, representation, since apart from this there remains nothing in it for cognition.

In this respect, then, the intuited world in space and time, which announces itself as sheer causality, is perfectly real. It is throughout what it gives itself out to be, and it gives itself wholly and without reserve as representation, cohering according to the law of causality. This is its empirical reality.

On the other hand, all causality exists only in the understanding and for the understanding. That whole actual, that is, acting, world is therefore, as such, always conditioned by the understanding and is nothing without it. But not only for this reason; already because, in general, no object without subject can be thought without contradiction, we must absolutely deny to the dogmatist who explains the reality of the external world as its independence from the subject any such reality of that world.

The whole world of objects is and remains representation, and for that very reason is, throughout and for all eternity, conditioned by the subject: that is, it has transcendental ideality. But it is not for that reason a lie or an illusion. It gives itself as what it is, as representation, and indeed as a series of representations whose common bond is the principle of sufficient reason. As such, it is intelligible to healthy understanding, even in its innermost meaning, and speaks to it in a language perfectly clear.

Only to a mind distorted by sophistical reasoning can it occur to dispute the world's reality. This always happens through an incorrect application of the principle of sufficient reason, which certainly connects all representations with one another, whatever kind they may be, but by no means connects them with the subject, nor with something that would be neither subject nor object, but merely the ground of the object. That is a non-concept, because only objects can be grounds, and always only grounds of objects.

If one investigates still more closely the origin of this question about the reality of the external world, one finds that, besides that false application of the principle of sufficient reason to what lies outside its domain, there is also a special confusion of its forms. The form that the principle has only with respect to concepts, or abstract representations, is transferred to intuitive representations, to real objects; and a ground of knowing is demanded of objects that can have no ground except a ground of becoming.

The principle of sufficient reason certainly governs abstract representations, concepts joined into judgments, in such a way that each judgment has its value, its validity, its whole existence, here called *truth*, solely and exclusively through the relation of the judgment to something outside it: its ground of knowledge, to which one must therefore always go back.

By contrast, over real objects, intuitive representations, the principle of sufficient reason governs not as the principle of the ground of *knowing*, but as the principle of the ground of *becoming*, as the law of causality. Each of these objects has already paid its debt to the principle by having *become*, that is, by having emerged as an effect from a cause. The demand for a ground of knowledge therefore has no validity or meaning here; it belongs to an entirely different class of objects.

For this reason, too, the intuitive world, so long as one remains with it, arouses neither scruple nor doubt in the observer. Here there is neither error nor truth; these are banished to the domain of the abstract, of reflection. Here, for the senses and the understanding, the world lies open, gives itself with naive truth as what it is: intuitive representation, which develops lawfully on the bond of causality.

So far as we have considered the question of the reality of the external world up to this point, it has always arisen from a wandering of reason that goes so far as to misunderstand itself. In this respect the question could be answered only by clarifying its content. After the whole essence of the principle of sufficient reason, the relation between object and subject, and the true character of sensible intuition had been investigated, the question had to cancel itself, because no meaning remained to it.

Yet that question also has another origin, entirely different from the purely speculative one indicated so far: an origin that is really empirical, although the question is still raised with a speculative intention. In this sense it has a much more intelligible meaning than in the former one, namely this: we have dreams; is not perhaps the whole of life a dream? Or, more precisely: is there a sure criterion between dream and actuality, between phantasms and real objects?

The claim that dreamed intuition has less liveliness and distinctness than actual intuition deserves no consideration at all, since no one has ever held the two side by side for comparison. One could compare only the *memory* of the dream with present actuality.

Kant solves the question this way: "The connection of representations among themselves according to the law of causality distinguishes life from dream." But in dream too, every particular thing likewise hangs together according to the principle of sufficient reason in all its forms; this connection breaks off only between life and dream, and between individual dreams.

Kant's answer could therefore still only be this: the *long* dream, life, has thoroughgoing connection within itself according to the principle of sufficient reason, but not with the *short* dreams, although each of these has the same connection within itself. Between these and the long dream, then, that bridge is broken, and by this one distinguishes the two.

Yet to conduct an inquiry according to this criterion into whether something was dreamed or actually happened would be very difficult and often impossible. We are by no means able to trace, link by link, the causal connection between every event we have lived through and the present moment; but for that reason we do not declare the event to have been dreamed. For this reason, in actual life we commonly do not use that kind of investigation to distinguish dream from actuality.

The only sure criterion for distinguishing dream from actuality is in fact none other than the wholly empirical one of waking up. Through waking, the causal connection between dreamed events and those of waking life is expressly and palpably broken.

Hobbes gives an excellent illustration of this in *Leviathan*, chapter 2. He observes that we easily take dreams afterward for actuality when, without intending to, we have slept clothed; especially when, in addition, some undertaking or plan occupies all our thoughts and concerns us in dream just as it does while waking. In such cases, waking is noticed almost as little as falling asleep; dream flows together with actuality and is mixed up with it. Then, to be sure, only the Kantian criterion remains. But if afterward, as often happens, the causal connection with the present, or its absence, simply cannot be discovered, then it must remain forever undecided whether an occurrence was dreamed or actually happened.

Here the close kinship between life and dream in fact comes very near to us; and we should not be ashamed to acknowledge it, after many great minds have recognized and expressed it. The Vedas and Puranas know no better comparison for the whole cognition of the actual world, which they call the web of Maya, and use none more often, than dream. Plato often says that human beings live only in dream, and that the philosopher alone strives to wake.

Pindar says (Pythian 8, 135):

> `σκιᾶς ὄναρ ἄνθρωπος`
> Translation: A human being is the dream of a shadow.

The source also gives the Latin rendering: `umbrae somnium homo` (a human being is a shadow's dream).

And Sophocles says:

> `ὁρῶ γὰρ ἡμᾶς οὐδὲν ὄντας ἄλλο, πλὴν`<br>
> `εἴδωλ', ὅσοιπερ ζῶμεν, ἢ κούφην σκιάν.`
> Translation: For I see that we who live are nothing other than phantoms, or a light shadow.

`Ajax 125.`

The source also gives the Latin rendering: `Nos enim, quicunque vivimus, nihil aliud esse comperio, quam simulacra et levem umbram.` (For I find that we, all who live, are nothing other than images and a light shadow.)

Beside these Shakespeare stands most worthily:

> `We are such stuff`<br>
> `As dreams are made of, and our little life`<br>
> `Is rounded with a sleep.`

*The Tempest*, Act 4, Scene 1. [^1]

Finally, Calderon was so deeply moved by this view that he sought to express it in a drama that is, in a certain sense, metaphysical: *Life Is a Dream*.

After these many poetic passages, may I too be permitted to express myself through a simile. Life and dreams are leaves of one and the same book. Reading in connected sequence is called actual life. But when the particular reading hour, the day, is over and the time of rest has come, we often still leaf idly through the book and open, without order or connection, now here, now there: often it is a page already read, often one still unknown, but always from the same book.

Such a single page read by itself is indeed out of connection with the consecutive reading; yet it does not fall so very far behind it when one considers that the whole of the consecutive reading likewise begins and ends extemporaneously, and thus is to be regarded only as a larger single page.

Although, then, individual dreams are separated from actual life by the fact that they do not intervene in the connection of experience that runs steadily through actual life, and although waking marks this distinction, that very connection of experience already belongs to actual life as its form; and dream, by contrast, also has a connection within itself. If one now takes up the standpoint of judgment outside both, one finds no determinate difference in their essence, and is compelled to concede to the poets that life is a long dream.

Let us now return from this empirical origin of the question of the reality of the external world, which stands entirely by itself, to its speculative origin. We found that the latter lay, first, in the false application of the principle of sufficient reason, namely between subject and object as well; and then again in the confusion of its forms, whereby the principle of the ground of knowing was transferred into the domain where the principle of the ground of becoming holds.

Nevertheless, that question could hardly have occupied philosophers so persistently if it were wholly without any true content, and if there did not lie in its innermost core some correct thought and meaning as its most proper origin. We must therefore assume that only when this thought entered into reflection and sought expression did it pass into those distorted forms and questions that do not understand themselves.

This, in my opinion, is indeed the case. As the pure expression of that innermost meaning of the question, which the question itself did not know how to reach, I set down this: What is this intuitive world besides being my representation? Is it, of which I am conscious only once, and indeed as representation, like my own body, of which I am conscious in a double way: on the one hand *representation*, on the other *will*?

The clearer explanation and the affirmation of this question will be the content of the second book, and the conclusions that follow from it will occupy the remaining part of this work.
