# §. 2.

That which knows everything and is known by no one is the *subject*. It is therefore the bearer of the world, the universal and always presupposed condition of everything that appears, of every object; for whatever exists, exists only for the subject. Everyone finds himself as this subject, but only insofar as he knows, not insofar as he is an object of cognition.

His body, however, is already object, and from this standpoint we therefore call even the body representation. For the body is object among objects and subject to the laws of objects, although it is immediate object. [^1] Like all objects of intuition, it lies in the forms of all cognition, in time and space, through which multiplicity exists.

The subject, by contrast, the knower that is never known, does not lie in these forms. Rather, it is itself always already presupposed by them. Therefore neither multiplicity nor its opposite, unity, belongs to it. We never know it; instead, it is precisely what knows wherever anything is known.

The world as representation, then, which is the only respect in which we are considering it here, has two essential, necessary, and inseparable halves. The one is the *object*: its form is space and time, and through these, multiplicity. The other half, however, the subject, does not lie in space and time; for it is whole and undivided in every representing being. Thus a single such being completes the world as representation with the object just as fully as the millions that exist; but if even that single being disappeared, the world as representation would no longer be.

These halves are therefore inseparable even for thought. Each of the two has meaning and existence only through and for the other; each exists with the other and disappears with it. They limit one another immediately: where the object begins, the subject ends.

The shared character of this boundary shows itself precisely in the fact that the essential, and therefore universal, forms of every object, which are time, space, and causality, can be found and completely known even without cognition of the object itself, proceeding from the subject; that is, in Kant's language, they lie `a priori` (prior to experience) in our consciousness. To have discovered this is one of Kant's chief merits, and a very great one.

I now maintain, in addition, that the principle of sufficient reason is the common expression for all these forms of the object of which we are conscious `a priori`; and that therefore everything we know purely `a priori` is nothing other than the content of that principle and what follows from it. In that principle, then, the whole of our cognition that is certain `a priori` is actually expressed.

In my essay on the principle of sufficient reason, I have shown in detail how every possible object whatsoever is subject to that principle; that is, how it stands in a necessary relation to other objects, on the one hand as determined, and on the other as determining. This goes so far that the whole existence of all objects, insofar as they are objects, representations and nothing else, runs back entirely to this necessary relation of theirs to one another, consists only in such a relation, and is therefore wholly relative. More on this soon.

I have further shown that, in accordance with the classes into which objects fall according to their possibility, that necessary relation which the principle of sufficient reason expresses in general appears in different forms; and through this, in turn, the correct division of those classes is confirmed. Here I constantly presuppose everything said there as known and present to the reader; for if it had not already been said there, it would necessarily have its place here.

[^1]: *On the Principle of Sufficient Reason*, 2nd ed., §. 22.
