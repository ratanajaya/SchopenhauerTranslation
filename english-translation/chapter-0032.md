# §. 30.

## Third Book.

The world as representation, second consideration:<br>
Representation independent of the principle of sufficient reason:<br>
the Platonic Idea: the object of art.

> Τι τὸ ὄν μὲν ἀεὶ, γένεσιν δὲ οὐϰ ἔχον; ϰαὶ τι τὸ γιγνύμενον<br>
> μὲν ϰαὶ ὐ πολλύμενον, ὔντως δὲ οὐδέποτε ὄν; ΠΛΑΤΩΝ
> Translation: What is that which always is and has no becoming? And what is that which becomes and passes away, but never truly is? Plato.

After, in the first book, we presented the world as mere *representation*, object for a subject, and then, in the second book, considered it from its other side and found this to be *will*, which alone turned out to be what that world still is besides representation, we called the world as representation, both as a whole and in its parts, in accordance with this cognition, the *objecthood of the will*. This means: the will that has become object, that is, representation.

We further remember that this objectivation of the will had many but determinate grades, at which, with gradually increasing clarity and completion, the essence of the will entered into representation, that is, presented itself as object. In these grades we already recognized Plato's Ideas, insofar as those grades are precisely the determinate species, or the original, unchanging forms and properties of all natural bodies, inorganic as well as organic, and also the universal forces that reveal themselves according to laws of nature.

All these Ideas, then, present themselves in countless individuals and particulars, to which they stand as models to their copies. The multiplicity of such individuals can be represented only through time and space, and their arising and passing away only through causality. In all these forms we recognize only the different configurations of the principle of sufficient reason, which is the ultimate principle of all finitude, of all individuation, and the universal form of representation as it falls within the cognition of the individual as such.

The Idea, by contrast, does not enter into that principle. Therefore neither multiplicity nor change belongs to it. While the individuals in which it presents itself are countless and ceaselessly come to be and pass away, the Idea remains unchanged as one and the same, and the principle of sufficient reason has no meaning for it.

But since the principle of sufficient reason is the form under which all cognition of the subject stands insofar as that subject cognizes as an *individual*, the Ideas will also lie entirely outside the sphere of cognition of the subject as such. Therefore, if the Ideas are to become object of cognition, this can happen only through the cancellation of individuality in the knowing subject. The closer and more detailed explanations of this are what will occupy us next.
